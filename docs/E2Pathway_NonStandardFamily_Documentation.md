# E-2 Dependent Visa: Non-Standard Family Structure Documentation

> **Disclaimer:** All content in this document is educational preparation guidance only — not legal advice. The legal analysis of whether a specific child qualifies, particularly in legitimation, informal adoption, or contested custody situations, requires review by a licensed U.S. immigration attorney. Trigger an attorney referral flag for any non-standard family structure scenario.

---

## Controlling Legal Framework

9 FAM 402.9 itself does not enumerate dependent document lists exhaustively — it delegates to the general definition of "child" in **INA § 101(b)(1)(A)–(E)** for E-class derivatives. The core rule for all E-2 dependents is that the child must be:

- **Unmarried**
- **Under 21 years of age**
- Fall within one of the recognized child categories under INA § 101(b)(1)

For the nationality requirement, unlike the principal investor, the **child's nationality does not need to match the treaty country** — they may be nationals of any country.

---

## 1. Stepchild of the Principal E-2 Investor

### Eligibility Rule

A stepchild qualifies as a "child" under **INA § 101(b)(1)(B)** if the **marriage creating the step-relationship occurred before the child turned 18**. The marriage must also still be legally subsisting at the time of the visa application — the step-relationship is terminated by death, divorce, or legal separation of the parties whose marriage created it (BIA, *Matter of Mourillon*, 18 I&N Dec. 122).

**Critically for an E-2 context:** A stepchild is the biological child of the E-2 *spouse*, not of the investor. The investor is the step-parent. The step-parent/investor does not need to adopt the child to sponsor them as a derivative.

### Required Documents

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Child's birth certificate | Proves parentage to the E-2 spouse |
| Marriage certificate (investor + spouse) | Establishes the marriage that creates the step-relationship |
| Proof marriage occurred before child's 18th birthday | Officer must verify the timing condition of INA 101(b)(1)(B) |
| Child's valid passport | Identity and travel document |
| DS-160 (nonimmigrant visa application) | Required for each dependent applicant |

### What Establishes the Step-Parent Relationship

The legal nexus is the **valid marriage between the investor and the child's biological parent**, confirmed by the marriage certificate. No adoption, no custody order, and no court proceeding are required solely to establish this relationship for visa purposes.

> ⚠️ **Note:** 9 FAM 402.9 is silent on the specific document list for stepchild E-2 dependents beyond the general derivative framework. The above is derived from INA 101(b)(1)(B) as applied across E and family-based visa categories.

## 2. Child from a Prior Relationship of the Principal E-2 Investor

A biological child of the **principal investor** from a prior relationship qualifies under **INA § 101(b)(1)(A)** as a natural, legitimate child, or under **INA § 101(b)(1)(C)** if born out of wedlock and later legitimated.

### Required Documents

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Child's birth certificate naming the investor as parent | Establishes biological relationship |
| Child's valid passport | Identity document |
| DS-160 for the child | Required for derivative application |
| Proof of legitimation (if applicable — see Section 4) | Required for out-of-wedlock children in some cases |
| Evidence child is unmarried and under 21 | Meets the definitional requirements |

The child need not be residing with the investor to qualify as a derivative — the key requirement is the legal parent-child relationship and that the child will be accompanying or joining the E-2 principal in the U.S.

## 3. Adopted Children

### Legally Adopted Children

An adopted child qualifies as a "child" under **INA § 101(b)(1)(E)** if:

- The adoption was **finalized before the child turned 16** (or age 18 if the child is an orphan sibling of another child already adopted)
- The adopting parent had **legal custody of the child for at least 2 years** prior to or after the adoption
- The adoptive parent **resided with the child for at least 2 years**

### Required Documents for Legally Adopted Child

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Full, final adoption decree or court order | Primary proof of adopted status |
| Child's original birth certificate | Identity and original parentage record |
| Proof of 2 years legal custody and co-residency | Statutory requirement under INA 101(b)(1)(E) |
| Child's passport | Identity and travel document |
| DS-160 | Derivative application |

### Informal Adoption, Guardianship, or Kinship Care

**These arrangements are NOT recognized for E-2 dependent purposes.** INA § 101(b)(1) does not include informal adoption, de facto guardianship, or customary-law adoptions unless they have been **formally decreed by a competent court with full, final legal effect**. A child under a legal guardianship arrangement — without a full adoption decree — does not qualify as an adopted "child" under INA 101(b)(1)(E).

> 🔴 **Platform flag:** This is one of the most common documentation gaps for applicants from countries where informal care arrangements are culturally standard (e.g., certain African, Latin American, or South/Southeast Asian practices). The platform should explicitly warn users of this distinction.

## 4. Born Inside vs. Outside of Marriage

This distinction matters significantly for documentation purposes:

| Status | Governing Provision | Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Born in wedlock (legitimate) | INA § 101(b)(1)(A) | Birth certificate showing both parents; straightforward |
| Born out of wedlock to a U.S. citizen mother | INA § 101(b)(1)(D) | Birth certificate; mother's citizenship proof |
| Born out of wedlock — father is the petitioner | INA § 101(b)(1)(C) | Birth certificate **plus** evidence of **legitimation** under the law of the child's or father's domicile |
| Born out of wedlock — no legitimation | INA § 101(b)(1)(D) | Only the **natural mother** can automatically claim the relationship; father must show legitimation |

**Legitimation** means placing the child born out of wedlock in the same legal position as a child born in wedlock through a recognized legal act — acknowledgment of paternity, court order, or applicable local law. If the child's home country law automatically legitimates a child by paternal acknowledgment (e.g., French law, some civil law jurisdictions), evidence of that acknowledgment (a recognized paternity declaration or civil registration) may suffice.

## 5. When a Child Relationship Is NOT Recognised for E-2 Derivative Purposes

Despite a biological or legal relationship existing, recognition is **denied** in the following scenarios:

- **Stepchild where the marriage occurred after the child turned 18** — INA 101(b)(1)(B) condition not met
- **Adopted child adopted after age 16** (or 18 in the sibling exception)
- **Adopted child where 2-year custody/residency requirement not met**
- **Informal/customary adoption without a formal court decree**
- **Stepchild whose creating marriage has ended** by divorce, death, or legal separation at time of application
- **A child who is married** — even if under 21, a married child does not qualify as a dependent under any INA 101(b)(1) category
- **A child who has turned 21** — age-out terminates derivative E-2 status on the child's 21st birthday
- **Children conceived by ART (assisted reproduction)/surrogacy** — 9 FAM and USCIS are still developing policy in this space; legal parentage follows the law of the state or country of birth; if legal parentage is ambiguous or contested, recognition may be denied or require additional adjudicative steps

## 6. Custody Orders When the Other Parent Is Not Applying

9 FAM 402.9 is **largely silent** on whether a custody order is required from USCIS/State Department solely for purposes of issuing the E-2 derivative visa itself.

### U.S. Visa Issuance

USCIS/the consulate does **not** require proof that the non-applying parent has consented to the child's U.S. travel as a condition of visa issuance. The visa is granted based on the qualifying relationship to the E-2 principal.

### Practical Travel and International Law Complications

The **non-applying parent's consent** is a separate legal issue governed by:

- The child's country of citizenship/residence exit laws
- The Hague Convention on International Child Abduction (applicable between the U.S. and 100+ countries)
- Country-specific child custody and travel laws

Many countries (including Canada) require a notarized travel consent letter from a non-accompanying parent before a minor can exit the country. Without the other parent's consent, the child may be denied departure even if the U.S. visa is granted.

> 📌 **Platform advisory language (suggested):** *"A U.S. visa does not authorize the child to depart their country of residence. If the child's other biological or legal parent is not part of this application, you may need a notarized consent letter or formal custody documentation under [child's home country] law before the child can travel."*

If there is a formal custody order granting the E-2 applicant sole custody, that order strengthens the visa application package by preempting officer questions and is worth including. If the situation is contested, an attorney referral is appropriate.

## Platform Implementation: Branching Logic Checklist

For the documentation engine, the following flag/branch logic is recommended:

- **Q: Is this child biological or adopted?** → Branch to legitimacy/adoption sub-questions
- **Q: Was child born during a marriage?** → If no, trigger legitimation documentation check
- **Q: Is child the biological child of your current spouse (not you)?** → Stepchild path; verify marriage pre-age-18 condition
- **Q: Was adoption finalized by a court?** → If informal/guardianship, trigger disqualification warning
- **Q: Is the child's other parent part of this application?** → If no, trigger home-country travel consent advisory
- **Q: Is child currently 17 or will turn 21 before expected green card?** → Trigger age-out planning alert
