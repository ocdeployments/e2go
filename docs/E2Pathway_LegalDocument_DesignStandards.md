# Legal Document Design Standards: Typography, Formatting & Presentation
## For E-2 Visa Applications Submitted to U.S. Consulates

> **Purpose:** This document defines the look, feel, font, and formatting standards for all documents generated by the E2Pathway platform. Every document produced must meet or exceed the standards applied in U.S. federal court filings and professional legal submissions. The Toronto consulate officer has minutes to review a 50-page package — presentation quality is a credibility signal.

---

## The Core Principle: Documents Must Signal Competence Before They Are Read

A consular officer forms a first impression within seconds of opening a package. A document that looks official, clean, and professionally typeset signals that the applicant is organised, detail-oriented, and serious. A document with inconsistent fonts, cramped margins, or poor spacing signals the opposite. Presentation is not cosmetic — it is part of the case.

---

## 1. Font Selection

### The Gold Standard: Century Schoolbook

**Century Schoolbook is the recommended primary font for all E2Pathway-generated legal documents.**

- The **U.S. Supreme Court** requires briefs to use a font from the Century family (Century Expanded, New Century Schoolbook, or Century Schoolbook)
- Century Schoolbook has what practitioners call the "federal court feel" — authoritative, formal, highly legible
- It is a **serif font**, which is the dominant convention in legal writing — serif typefaces are considered more professional and easier to read in long-form documents
- It renders beautifully in PDF at 12pt

### Acceptable Alternatives (in order of preference)

| Font | Classification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Century Schoolbook** | Serif | ✅ Primary recommendation — federal court standard |
| **Garamond** | Serif | Elegant, readable, used in professional legal briefs |
| **Georgia** | Serif | Excellent on-screen and in print; universally available |
| **Book Antiqua / Palatino** | Serif | On UT Law's "generally tolerable" list for legal documents |
| **Times New Roman** | Serif | Widely used but considered dated; the 7th Circuit discourages it |
| **Cambria** | Serif | Modern serif; clean and professional for cover letters |
| **Calibri** | Sans-serif | Acceptable for secondary documents; too informal for primary submissions |
| **Arial** | Sans-serif | Used by the Connecticut appellate court; professional but lacks gravitas for visa docs |

### Fonts to Avoid

- **Calibri as a primary font** — perceived as informal/default Microsoft Word
- **Times New Roman** — still acceptable but increasingly seen as a "didn't think about it" choice
- **Helvetica / Arial** — too corporate/sans-serif for primary legal documents; acceptable for labels and divider tabs only
- **Any decorative, script, or display font** — never in a legal submission
- **Comic Sans, Papyrus, or novelty fonts** — self-explanatory

## 2. Font Size

| Element | Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Body text** | **12pt** | Non-negotiable — the standard across U.S. federal courts and Canadian Federal Court Rules |
| **Section headings (H1)** | **14pt bold** | Used for major tab headings (e.g., "TAB D — COVER LETTER") |
| **Subheadings (H2)** | **12pt bold** | Used within sections |
| **Sub-subheadings (H3)** | **12pt bold italic** | Third-level headings |
| **Footnotes** | **10pt** | Footnotes may be single-spaced and at 10pt |
| **Table content** | **10–11pt** | Slightly smaller acceptable within tables to manage space |
| **Headers / Footers** | **10pt** | Page numbers, document title |
| **Captions / Labels** | **10pt** | Photo captions, exhibit labels |

> ⚠️ **Hard floor: Never use a font size below 10pt in any document submitted to a consulate.** Anything smaller signals that the author is trying to cram in content rather than edit for conciseness.

---

## 3. Line Spacing

| Element | Spacing | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Body text** | **Double-spaced (2.0)** | Standard for formal legal documents and court filings |
| **Cover letter** | **1.15 or 1.5** | Business letter format — single or near-single spacing is standard for correspondence |
| **Block quotations (long quotes)** | **Single-spaced, indented** | Indented 0.5" on both sides |
| **Footnotes** | **Single-spaced** | Standard exception to the double-spacing rule |
| **Tables** | **Single-spaced** | Tables are always single-spaced |
| **Section headings** | **Single-spaced** | Headings do not require double-spacing |
| **Between paragraphs** | **No extra spacing (0pt before/after)** | Let the line spacing create visual separation |

> **Platform note:** The cover letter (Tab D) follows **business letter format** — 1.15 line spacing, not double-spaced. All other formal documents (declarations, business plan, source of funds narrative) use double-spacing.

---

## 4. Margins

| Margin | Size | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Top** | **1 inch (2.54 cm)** | 43 CFR § 4.408; Canadian Federal Court Rules |
| **Bottom** | **1 inch (2.54 cm)** | Standard |
| **Left** | **1.25 inches (3.17 cm)** | Slightly wider for binding/hole-punching |
| **Right** | **1 inch (2.54 cm)** | Standard |

> **Why wider left margin:** Documents submitted in a binder or tabbed package are typically hole-punched or bound on the left. A 1.25" left margin ensures no text is obscured by the binding.

> **Canadian Federal Court Rules (SOR/98-106, Section 65)** formally require left/right margins of not less than 3.5 cm (~1.38") — a useful reference point for the professional standard.

## 5. Page Setup

| Setting | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Paper size** | 8.5" × 11" (Letter) — standard for U.S. submissions |
| **Print side** | **Single-sided only** — do not print on both sides of a page |
| **Page numbers** | **Sequential, centered at bottom** — cover page unnumbered; Table of Contents uses lowercase Roman numerals (i, ii, iii); body uses Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3...) |
| **File format** | **PDF** — always submit as text-searchable PDF, not scanned images |
| **PDF quality** | All text must be selectable and searchable — not a flat scan |
| **Document size** | Maximum 50 pages per package (Toronto consulate rule) |

## 6. Heading Hierarchy

The heading structure should mirror the document's logical organization and make it easy for an officer to navigate at a glance.

```
LEVEL 1 — TAB IDENTIFIER (ALL CAPS, 14pt bold, centered)
  Example: TAB D — COVER LETTER

Level 2 — Major Section Heading (Title Case, 12pt bold, left-aligned)
  Example: Investment Overview

  Level 3 — Subsection Heading (Title Case, 12pt bold italic, left-aligned)
    Example: Source of Investment Funds

      Body text (12pt, regular weight, double-spaced, left-aligned or justified)
```

### Numbering Convention (for business plan and structured documents)

- Major sections: **I. II. III.** (uppercase Roman numerals)
- Primary subheadings: **A. B. C.**
- Secondary subheadings: **1. 2. 3.**
- Tertiary: **a. b. c.**

---

## 7. Text Alignment

| Element | Alignment |
|---|---|
| **Body text** | **Justified** (both left and right edges aligned) — gives documents a formal, book-like appearance |
| **Headings** | **Left-aligned** (or centered for tab/section titles) |
| **Cover letter body** | **Left-aligned** (business letter convention) |
| **Tables** | Left-aligned cells; right-align numbers |
| **Page numbers** | Centered at bottom |

> **Note:** Justified alignment is standard for formal legal documents submitted to courts and government bodies. It creates clean, professional-looking text blocks. However, it requires hyphenation to be managed — avoid rivers of white space in justified text.

---

## 8. Paragraph Formatting

- **First line indent:** 0.5" indent for the first line of each paragraph (traditional legal format), OR
- **Block paragraphs with space between:** No indent, with a blank line between paragraphs (modern business letter style — preferred for cover letters)
- **No orphaned headings:** A heading must never appear at the bottom of a page without at least two lines of body text following it on the same page
- **No widowed lines:** The last line of a paragraph should not appear alone at the top of a new page
- **Max paragraph length:** Keep paragraphs to 5–8 lines. Long, unbroken paragraphs are harder for a busy officer to scan

---

## 9. Document Structure for E-2 Package

Each document in the package should open with a consistent header block:

```
[APPLICANT FULL NAME]
[APPLICATION TYPE: E-2 Treaty Investor Visa]
[TAB IDENTIFIER]
[DATE PREPARED: Month DD, YYYY]
```

And close with a consistent footer on every page:
```
[Last Name] — E-2 Application — Tab [X]          Page [N] of [Total]
```

---

## 10. Tables

Tables appear in the business plan (financial projections), source of funds chronology, and organizational charts.

- Use **simple, clean borders** — single-line borders in a neutral gray (#595959 or similar)
- **Header row:** Bold, 11pt, slightly shaded background (light gray — no color)
- **Body rows:** 10–11pt, alternating white/very light gray rows for readability
- **No decorative colors** — tables in a legal submission must not use colored fills, gradient headers, or branded color schemes
- **Column widths:** Sized to content — do not leave columns artificially wide

## 11. Cover Letter Specific Formatting (Tab D)

The cover letter follows **formal business letter format**, not legal brief format:

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| Font | Century Schoolbook or Georgia, 12pt |
| Line spacing | 1.15 |
| Margins | 1" all sides |
| Alignment | Left-aligned (not justified) |
| Salutation | "Dear Consular Officer:" |
| Closing | "Respectfully submitted," |
| Signature block | Name, title (E-2 Treaty Investor), date |
| Length | 2–3 pages maximum |
| Paragraphs | Block style (no indent; blank line between paragraphs) |

## 12. PDF Generation Standards

All E2Pathway-generated documents must meet these PDF standards:

- ✅ **Text-searchable PDF** — never a scanned image; officer must be able to select and copy text
- ✅ **Embedded fonts** — fonts must be embedded in the PDF so they render correctly on any device
- ✅ **Correct page size** — Letter (8.5" × 11"), not A4
- ✅ **Bookmarks / outlines** — for multi-section documents, PDF bookmarks should mirror the heading structure
- ✅ **File naming convention:** `LastName_TabD_CoverLetter.pdf` — clear, professional, officer-navigable
- ✅ **No password protection** — consulate staff must be able to open without credentials
- ✅ **No tracked changes or comments** — all markup must be accepted/removed before PDF export

## 13. Physical Package Presentation (When Printing)

When the package is submitted or brought to the interview:

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Paper quality** | White, 24 lb bond paper minimum — not standard 20 lb copy paper |
| **Binder** | White or black 1.5"–2" three-ring binder |
| **Tab dividers** | Labeled plastic tab dividers — one per tab (Tab A through Tab L) |
| **Tab labels** | Bold, 12pt font — matching the document headings |
| **Table of contents** | First page inside the binder cover — lists all tabs with page numbers |
| **No staples** | Individual documents within a tab should not be stapled — use the binder rings |
| **No color printing** | Body text and tables in black only; photos can be in color |
| **Photo exhibits** | Printed 4×6" or full-page — labeled with captions below each photo |

## 14. The Officer's Perspective — What "Professional" Signals

Based on practitioner reports and immigration officer accounts:

> *"An immigration officer has just minutes to review your visa application. When you have just minutes to make your case, every second counts. Organised sections, executive summaries, highlighted key points, and labeled cross-references are what separate approvals from 221(g) requests."*
> — Jennifer Grady, Esq. (immigration attorney, 99%+ approval rate)

A professionally formatted package communicates:
1. **The applicant is organised** — they will run their business the same way
2. **The applicant respects the officer's time** — they structured the package for navigation, not for volume
3. **The applicant is serious** — this is not a hobby project; it is a professional venture
4. **Nothing is hidden** — clear labeling and a table of contents signal transparency
