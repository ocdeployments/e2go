## What 221(g) Means

A refusal under Section 221(g) of the Immigration and Nationality Act
is **not a final visa denial.** It means the consular officer was unable
to issue the visa at the time of the interview because:
- Additional documents or information are required, OR
- The application requires further administrative processing (security
  clearances, background checks, or interagency review)

> "If you receive notice that you have been refused under Section 221(g)
> of the Immigration and Nationality Act, it does not mean that the visa
> has been refused permanently; it means that more information is required
> before a final decision can be made."
> — U.S. Embassy Canada (ca.usembassy.gov)

**The CEAC portal (ceac.state.gov) will show your status as "Refused"
even during 221(g) processing.** This is technically accurate — the
application is in a refused state — but does not mean the visa will
ultimately be denied. Many 221(g) cases are resolved with visa issuance.

## The Two Types of 221(g)

### Type 1 — Document Request

The officer identified a specific gap in your application and is requesting
additional documentation or information before making a final decision.

You will receive a **written request** specifying exactly what is needed.
This is the more straightforward type of 221(g) — you know what to provide
and can respond directly.

**Common document requests in E-2 cases:**
- Additional source of funds documentation
- Updated business financial statements
- Missing corporate documents (operating agreement, articles of organization)
- Updated bank records showing funds are still in the business account
- Additional evidence of business operations (lease, licenses, payroll records)
- Explanation of a specific aspect of the application
- Additional evidence of non-immigrant intent

**Deadline to respond:**
> "You have one year from the date of the refusal to submit the additional
> information, and no further action can be taken on your application until
> you provide this information."
> — U.S. Embassy Canada (ca.usembassy.gov)

If you do not respond within one year, you must file a new application
and pay the MRV fee again.

### Type 2 — Administrative Processing (Security Clearance)

The application has been flagged for additional background review that
does not require action from the applicant. This is an internal government
process involving:
- Interagency security clearances
- Name checks against security databases
- Technology Alert List (TAL) review
- Nationality-based review protocols

You will typically receive a letter stating your application is "subject
to administrative processing" with no specific document request.

> "Administrative processing occurs when a visa cannot be issued at the
> time of the interview... The most frequent causes include:
> - Missing documents
> - Interagency security checks (name matches, Technology Alert List reviews)
> - Nationality-based or sensitive occupation reviews
> - Biometric or biographic matches to security databases"
> — Wolfsdorf Rosenthal Immigration (2025)

For most Canadian E-2 applicants, Type 2 administrative processing is
uncommon and typically brief. The security check architecture is primarily
designed for applicants from countries with heightened security concerns.

## Timelines

**Document Request (Type 1):**
- Response from applicant: as soon as possible (within one year maximum)
- Officer review of submitted documents: typically 2–8 weeks
- Can be faster if documents are complete and compelling

**Administrative Processing (Type 2):**
> "The Bureau of Consular Affairs considers 90 days to be the normal
> processing time."
> — Harvard HIO Administrative Processing FAQ

In practice:
- Most routine E-2 administrative processing cases: 2–6 weeks
- Cases involving security flags: potentially months
- Some cases have remained pending for 6–12+ months

**Inquiring about status:**
> "Before inquiring about status of administrative processing, applicants
> or their representatives must wait at least 180 days from the date of
> interview or submission of supplemental documents, whichever is later."
> — U.S. Embassy Canada (ca.usembassy.gov)

Premature inquiries are discouraged and do not expedite processing.
An attorney may contact the consulate on your behalf after 180 days.

## How to Respond to a 221(g) Document Request

### Step 1 — Read the Request Carefully

The 221(g) letter specifies exactly what is needed. Read it word for word.
Do not assume — if the request is ambiguous, consult an immigration attorney
before responding.

### Step 2 — Organize Your Response

Submit a professional, organized response package:
- Cover letter identifying: the applicant name, case number, interview date,
  and a brief statement that you are responding to the 221(g) of [date]
- Numbered exhibit list corresponding to each document requested
- Each document clearly labeled with the exhibit number
- Do not submit documents not requested — excess material can cause confusion

### Step 3 — Submission Method

For applications at the U.S. Consulate General in Montreal (which handles
applications from Canadian applicants including Toronto-area applicants):

> "You may send requested information to the U.S. Consulate General in
> Montreal using courier services that are offered at no extra cost for
> the applicant."
> — U.S. Embassy Canada (ca.usembassy.gov)

Follow the courier instructions on the U.S. Embassy Canada website for
current submission procedures. Do not hand-deliver documents unless
specifically instructed to do so.

### Step 4 — Track Your Status

Monitor your application status at **ceac.state.gov** using your case number.
The status will change from "Refused" to "Issued" when the visa is approved.

## What to Do While Waiting

**Do not:**
- Book non-refundable flights or sign U.S. leases while your case is pending
- File a new visa application — this does not expedite processing and
  may complicate the existing case
- Inquire before 180 days (unless a genuine emergency exists)

**Do:**
- Ensure your passport will remain valid for at least 6 months beyond
  your intended entry date
- If your business is already operating in the U.S. under a different
  status (e.g., you entered as a visitor and are not yet working), maintain
  lawful status throughout the processing period
- Consult an immigration attorney if the delay is causing material business
  harm or if you have not received a response after 6+ months

## 221(g) vs. 214(b) — The Critical Distinction

| | 221(g) | 214(b) |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Not enough information to decide yet | Visa denied — you failed to qualify |
| Finality | Not final — can be resolved | Final at that interview |
| Appeal | Not applicable (not a denial) | No appeal available |
| Path forward | Provide requested documents or await processing | Reapply with strengthened application |
| Status on CEAC | "Refused" (misleading) | "Refused" |
| Timeline | Days to months | Immediate — decision made at interview |

## E-2-Specific 221(g) Scenarios

**Scenario 1 — Investment funds not yet fully deployed:**
Officer issues 221(g) requesting evidence that the investment is complete.
Response: Updated business bank statements, additional invoices, and a
statement of all expenditures to date.

**Scenario 2 — Business plan financial projections questioned:**
Officer requests support for revenue projections. Response: Local market
research data, comparable business sales data, franchise FDD Item 19
data (if applicable), and a written explanation of the methodology.

**Scenario 3 — Source of funds gap:**
Officer requests documentation of a specific deposit or transfer in the
funds trail. Response: Bank statements for the missing period, explanation
letter, and any supporting documentation (employment records, sale
documents, etc.).

**Scenario 4 — Business not yet operational:**
Officer issues 221(g) because the business is still in the setup phase.
This is a common E-2 scenario — the investor is in the process of investing.
Response: Evidence of commitments made (signed lease, franchise agreement,
equipment orders) and a clear timeline for opening.
