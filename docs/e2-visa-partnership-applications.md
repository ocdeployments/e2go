# E-2 Visa — Partnership Applications (50/50 Structures)
**kb/e2-visa-partnership-applications.md**
*Sources: 9 FAM 402.9-6(F); 9 FAM 402.9-6(D); 9 FAM 402.9-11(A);
22 CFR 41.51; RJ Immigration Law; U.S. Immigration Advisor (2026)*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## Overview

Two people can co-invest in a single U.S. business and each apply for
independent E-2 visa status. This is called a partnership application —
though the business is typically structured as an LLC (not a legal
partnership), and each applicant files their own DS-160 and DS-156E.

This structure is attractive for:
- Two Canadian couples (two families investing together)
- Friends or professional colleagues co-founding a business
- Spouses where both want independent E-2 work authorization

---

## The 50% Ownership Rule for Partners

Each applicant must independently satisfy the **develop and direct**
requirement. The most straightforward way to do this is through the
50% ownership rule:

> "The treaty investor must demonstrate this by showing at least 50%
> ownership of the enterprise."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(F)

In a 50/50 structure, each partner owns exactly 50% — which meets the
"at least 50%" threshold for each person independently.

**Important:** This means a 40/60 split does NOT satisfy the requirement
for the 40% owner unless they can demonstrate operational control through
a managerial position or other means.

---

## Each Partner's Investment Must Be Substantial Independently

Each applicant must independently satisfy the **substantiality** requirement.
The investment is evaluated on a per-applicant basis, not on the combined total.

> "Where two treaty investors each own 50 percent of a business, each investor's
> investment must be substantial in relation to the total cost of the enterprise."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(d) (principle)

**What this means in practice:**

Assume a business costs $200,000 to establish:
- Partner A invests $100,000 (50% of total cost) → Likely substantial ✅
- Partner B invests $100,000 (50% of total cost) → Likely substantial ✅

If Partner A invests $180,000 and Partner B invests $20,000:
- Partner A at $180,000 / $200,000 = 90% → Clearly substantial ✅
- Partner B at $20,000 / $200,000 = 10% → Likely fails proportionality ❌

**The practical guidance:** In a 50/50 partnership, each partner should
contribute an approximately equal share of the total investment.

## Role Differentiation — The Critical Requirement

Each E-2 applicant must have a genuine, differentiated management role
in the business. The officer evaluates whether both applicants actually
develop and direct the enterprise — or whether one person is doing all
the work and the other is merely a passive co-owner.

### What Strong Role Differentiation Looks Like

**Partner A — President / Chief Operating Officer**
- Day-to-day operations management
- Staffing and HR decisions
- Vendor and supplier management
- Compliance and licensing
- Customer service standards and protocols

**Partner B — Vice President / Chief Financial & Marketing Officer**
- Financial management and bookkeeping oversight
- Marketing strategy and advertising
- Business development and sales
- Financial reporting and investor relations
- Technology and systems management

Each role should:
- Have a distinct title that reflects a different functional area
- Cover responsibilities that do not significantly overlap
- Be supported by the applicant's professional background and qualifications
- Be described in the business plan's management section and org chart

### What Weak Role Differentiation Looks Like

- Both applicants: "Co-owner responsible for all business operations"
- Both applicants with identical job descriptions
- One applicant with a defined role, the other vaguely listed as "advisor"
- Roles that both describe customer-facing or operational duties without
  any separation of responsibility

## Single Binder vs. Two Separate Binders

### The Standard Approach: Two Separate Complete Binders

Each E-2 applicant typically submits a **complete, independent application
binder** that satisfies all E-2 requirements from their individual perspective.

Each binder includes:
- The applicant's own DS-160 and DS-156E
- The applicant's own Cover Letter (addressing all E-2 criteria)
- The applicant's own personal documents (passport, photos, financial docs)
- Shared business documents (both reference the same business plan,
  the same financial statements, the same asset register)
- The applicant's specific source of funds documentation
- The applicant's specific qualifications and biography

**Why separate binders:** Each consular officer conducts a separate
interview for each applicant. Having a self-contained binder for each
applicant ensures the officer has everything they need without having to
reference the other applicant's file.

### Shared Documents

The following documents are typically shared (copies in each binder):
- Business plan (identical — represents the business)
- Financial projections
- Asset register (Template 3)
- Hiring plan
- Lease agreement
- Franchise agreement (if applicable)
- Business bank account records
- Organizational chart (which shows both partners and their roles)

### Individual Documents (Each Person's Own)

- Cover letter (each writes their own, addressing their specific role)
- DS-160 form
- DS-156E form
- Passport and identity documents
- Source of funds documentation (each person documents their own contribution)
- Qualifications and professional background
- Proof of treaty nationality

## Interview Logistics for Partnerships

At the U.S. Consulate General in Toronto (the standard interview location
for Canadian E-2 applicants):

- Each applicant schedules their own appointment
- Interviews may be the same day or different days
- Partners are typically interviewed separately
- The consular officer for each interview may be different

**What this means for preparation:**
- Each applicant must be able to independently and confidently explain:
  - Their investment amount and source of funds
  - Their specific management role in the business
  - The business concept, market, and financial projections
  - Why they are qualified for their stated role
  - Their non-immigrant intent

- Partners should be consistent on shared facts (total investment, business
  description, projected revenue, number of jobs) but describe their
  individual roles from their own perspective

## Dependants in Partnership Applications

Each principal investor can include their own dependants (spouse and
unmarried children under 21) on E-2 dependent status.

In a 50/50 partnership with two families:
- Family A: Principal investor (E-2) + spouse (E-2D) + children (E-2D)
- Family B: Principal investor (E-2) + spouse (E-2D) + children (E-2D)

**Spousal work authorization:**
E-2 dependent spouses are eligible for employment authorization in the
United States. Each spouse applies for an EAD (Employment Authorization
Document) after arriving in the U.S.

Note: The E-2 dependent spouse's work authorization is not limited to
the partner's business — they can work for any employer.

## Common Pitfalls in Partnership Applications

| Pitfall | Risk | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Unequal investment contributions | Minor investor fails substantiality | Equalize contributions or document why disparity is appropriate |
| Identical role descriptions | Both fail develop and direct | Create clearly differentiated functional roles with distinct titles |
| One binder for both applicants | Officer may request additional materials | Prepare complete separate binders for each applicant |
| Partners coached on each other's answers | Inconsistency detected in separate interviews | Each partner must know the whole business independently |
| Minor partner has no real role | Fails develop and direct | Ensure both partners genuinely participate in management |
