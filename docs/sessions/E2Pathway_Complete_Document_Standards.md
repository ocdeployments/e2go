## The Core Principle: Documents Must Signal Competence Before They Are Read

A consular officer forms a first impression within seconds of opening a package. A document that looks official, clean, and professionally typeset signals that the applicant is organised, detail-oriented, and serious. A document with inconsistent fonts, cramped margins, or poor spacing signals the opposite. Presentation is not cosmetic — it is part of the case.

## 1. Font Selection

### The Gold Standard: Century Schoolbook

**Century Schoolbook is the recommended primary font for all E2go-generated legal documents.**

- The **U.S. Supreme Court** requires briefs to use a font from the Century family (Century Expanded, New Century Schoolbook, or Century Schoolbook)
- Century Schoolbook has what practitioners call the "federal court feel" — authoritative, formal, highly legible
- It is a **serif font**, which is the dominant convention in legal writing — serif typefaces are considered more professional and easier to read in long-form documents
- It renders beautifully in PDF at 12pt

### Acceptable Alternatives (in order of preference)

| Font | Classification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Century Schoolbook** | Serif | ✅ Primary recommendation — federal court standard |
| **Garamond** | Serif | Elegant, readable, used in professional legal briefs |
| **Georgia** | Serif | Excellent on-screen and in print; universally available |
| **Book Antiqua / Palatino** | Serif | On UT Law's "generally tolerable" list for legal documents |
| **Times New Roman** | Serif | Widely used but considered dated; the 7th Circuit discourages it |
| **Cambria** | Serif | Modern serif; clean and professional for cover letters |
| **Calibri** | Sans-serif | Acceptable for secondary documents; too informal for primary submissions |
| **Arial** | Sans-serif | Used by the Connecticut appellate court; professional but lacks gravitas for visa docs |

### Fonts to Avoid

- **Calibri as a primary font** — perceived as informal/default Microsoft Word
- **Times New Roman** — still acceptable but increasingly seen as a "didn't think about it" choice
- **Helvetica / Arial** — too corporate/sans-serif for primary legal documents; acceptable for labels and divider tabs only
- **Any decorative, script, or display font** — never in a legal submission
- **Comic Sans, Papyrus, or novelty fonts** — self-explanatory

## 2. Font Size

| Element | Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Body text** | **12pt** | Non-negotiable — the standard across U.S. federal courts and Canadian Federal Court Rules |
| **Section headings (H1)** | **14pt bold** | Used for major tab headings (e.g., "TAB D — COVER LETTER") |
| **Subheadings (H2)** | **12pt bold** | Used within sections |
| **Sub-subheadings (H3)** | **12pt bold italic** | Third-level headings |
| **Footnotes** | **10pt** | Footnotes may be single-spaced and at 10pt |
| **Table content** | **10–11pt** | Slightly smaller acceptable within tables to manage space |
| **Headers / Footers** | **10pt** | Page numbers, document title |
| **Captions / Labels** | **10pt** | Photo captions, exhibit labels |

> ⚠️ **Hard floor: Never use a font size below 10pt in any document submitted to a consulate.** Anything smaller signals that the author is trying to cram in content rather than edit for conciseness.

---

## 3. Line Spacing

| Element | Spacing | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Body text** | **Double-spaced (2.0)** | Standard for formal legal documents and court filings |
| **Cover letter** | **1.15 or 1.5** | Business letter format — single or near-single spacing is standard for correspondence |
| **Block quotations (long quotes)** | **Single-spaced, indented** | Indented 0.5" on both sides |
| **Footnotes** | **Single-spaced** | Standard exception to the double-spacing rule |
| **Tables** | **Single-spaced** | Tables are always single-spaced |
| **Section headings** | **Single-spaced** | Headings do not require double-spacing |
| **Between paragraphs** | **No extra spacing (0pt before/after)** | Let the line spacing create visual separation |

> **Platform note:** The cover letter (Tab D) follows **business letter format** — 1.15 line spacing, not double-spaced. All other formal documents (declarations, business plan, source of funds narrative) use double-spacing.

---

## 4. Margins

| Margin | Size | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Top** | **1 inch (2.54 cm)** | 43 CFR § 4.408; Canadian Federal Court Rules |
| **Bottom** | **1 inch (2.54 cm)** | Standard |
| **Left** | **1.25 inches (3.17 cm)** | Slightly wider for binding/hole-punching |
| **Right** | **1 inch (2.54 cm)** | Standard |

> **Why wider left margin:** Documents submitted in a binder or tabbed package are typically hole-punched or bound on the left. A 1.25" left margin ensures no text is obscured by the binding.

> **Canadian Federal Court Rules (SOR/98-106, Section 65)** formally require left/right margins of not less than 3.5 cm (~1.38") — a useful reference point for the professional standard.

## 6. Heading Hierarchy

The heading structure should mirror the document's logical organization and make it easy for an officer to navigate at a glance.

```
LEVEL 1 — TAB IDENTIFIER (ALL CAPS, 14pt bold, centered)
  Example: TAB D — COVER LETTER

Level 2 — Major Section Heading (Title Case, 12pt bold, left-aligned)
  Example: Investment Overview

  Level 3 — Subsection Heading (Title Case, 12pt bold italic, left-aligned)
    Example: Source of Investment Funds

      Body text (12pt, regular weight, double-spaced, left-aligned or justified)
```

### Numbering Convention (for business plan and structured documents)

- Major sections: **I. II. III.** (uppercase Roman numerals)
- Primary subheadings: **A. B. C.**
- Secondary subheadings: **1. 2. 3.**
- Tertiary: **a. b. c.**

---

## 7. Text Alignment

| Element | Alignment |
|---|---|
| **Body text** | **Justified** (both left and right edges aligned) — gives documents a formal, book-like appearance |
| **Headings** | **Left-aligned** (or centered for tab/section titles) |
| **Cover letter body** | **Left-aligned** (business letter convention) |
| **Tables** | Left-aligned cells; right-align numbers |
| **Page numbers** | Centered at bottom |

> **Note:** Justified alignment is standard for formal legal documents submitted to courts and government bodies. It creates clean, professional-looking text blocks. However, it requires hyphenation to be managed — avoid rivers of white space in justified text.

---

## 8. Paragraph Formatting

- **First line indent:** 0.5" indent for the first line of each paragraph (traditional legal format), OR
- **Block paragraphs with space between:** No indent, with a blank line between paragraphs (modern business letter style — preferred for cover letters)
- **No orphaned headings:** A heading must never appear at the bottom of a page without at least two lines of body text following it on the same page
- **No widowed lines:** The last line of a paragraph should not appear alone at the top of a new page
- **Max paragraph length:** Keep paragraphs to 5–8 lines. Long, unbroken paragraphs are harder for a busy officer to scan

## 10. Tables

Tables appear in the business plan (financial projections), source of funds chronology, and organizational charts.

- Use **simple, clean borders** — single-line borders in a neutral gray (#595959 or similar)
- **Header row:** Bold, 11pt, slightly shaded background (light gray — no color)
- **Body rows:** 10–11pt, alternating white/very light gray rows for readability
- **No decorative colors** — tables in a legal submission must not use colored fills, gradient headers, or branded color schemes
- **Column widths:** Sized to content — do not leave columns artificially wide

## The Governing Rule: Toronto Consulate Requirement

The U.S. Consulate General in Toronto requires that all E-2 submissions:

- Be submitted as a **single PDF attachment** via evisacanada@state.gov
- Be **no longer than 50 pages** (excluding Tabs A, B, C, and the G-28)
- Be **organized into tabbed sections** in a defined order
- Include a **Comprehensive Index / Table of Contents**

> Source: ca.usembassy.gov — *"We will only accept submissions in PDF format as a single attachment, and the package must be no longer than 50 pages. All submissions should be organized into tabbed sections comprising a cover letter and comprehensive index/table of contents."*

This is not optional. A package without a Table of Contents, or one that is not tabbed and indexed, signals disorganisation before the officer reads a single word.

## 1. The Cover Page

### Does the Package Need a Cover Page?

**Yes.** Every professionally prepared E-2 package should open with a cover page. The U.S. Department of Justice Executive Office for Immigration Review confirms that cover pages are standard in all federal immigration filings and do **not** count toward page limits.

The cover page is the officer's first visual impression of the entire application.

### Cover Page Design Rules

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Layout** | Centered, vertically balanced on the page |
| **Font** | Century Schoolbook, 14pt for applicant name; 12pt for all other lines |
| **Applicant name** | All caps, bold, 16pt — the most prominent element |
| **Title line** | "E-2 TREATY INVESTOR VISA APPLICATION" — bold, 14pt, all caps |
| **Divider lines** | Thin horizontal rules (0.5pt) to separate sections — clean and formal |

- No photographs of the applicant
- No decorative borders, logos, or graphics
- No color — black text on white paper only
- No legal disclaimers (those belong in the cover letter, not the cover page)
- No long text — this is an identification page, not a summary

## 2. The Table of Contents / Index

### Why the Index Is Critical

The Toronto consulate explicitly requires a "Comprehensive Index/Table of Contents." This is the officer's navigation map. A well-constructed index allows the officer to locate any document in seconds without searching through the package.

Immigration attorneys with the highest approval rates report that the index is the second thing an officer looks at after the cover letter — it determines whether the rest of the package is reviewed systematically or at random.

### What the Index Must Contain

Every index entry must include:
1. **Tab identifier** (Tab A, Tab B, etc.)
2. **Section title** — exactly matching the tab label
3. **Brief description** of what is in the tab (1 line maximum)
4. **Page number** — the page where that tab begins

### Index Template — Toronto Format

```
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[APPLICANT NAME] — E-2 Treaty Investor Visa Application
COMPREHENSIVE INDEX / TABLE OF CONTENTS
Submitted to: U.S. Consulate General, Toronto
Date: [Month DD, YYYY]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TAB     SECTION TITLE                                     PAGE
──────  ────────────────────────────────────────────────  ────

Tab A   DS-160 Confirmation Page                             1
        Online nonimmigrant visa application confirmation

Tab B   Passport and Photograph                              2
        Current valid Canadian passport (bio page + visa pages)
        One (1) 2"×2" passport-style photograph

Tab C   Visa Fee Receipt                                     4
        MRV application fee payment confirmation

Tab D   Cover Letter                                         5
        Narrative addressing all E-2 eligibility requirements
        under 9 FAM 402.9

Tab E   Applicant Nationality Evidence                      10
        Canadian passport confirming treaty country citizenship

Tab F   Investment Evidence — Funds Committed                11
        Wire transfer confirmations, franchise fee receipts,
        lease deposits, business expenditure records

Tab G   Evidence of a Real and Operating Enterprise         18
        Business registration, EIN, lease agreement, licenses,
        bank account, photographs of premises

Tab H   Source of Funds                                     25
        Complete chronological funds trail from origin
        to U.S. business account

Tab I   Business Plan                                       33
        Five-year projections, market analysis, hiring plan,
        non-marginality evidence

Tab J   Investor Qualifications                             43
        CV/résumé, employment history, educational credentials,
        professional references

Tab K   Dependent Family Members (if applicable)            47
        Spouse and children: passports, marriage certificate,
        birth certificates, DS-160s

Tab L   Prior E-2 Approval (Renewals Only)                  50
        Prior visa stamp, I-94, tax returns, financial statements

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL PACKAGE: [XX] pages (excluding Tabs A, B, C)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### Index Design Rules

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Font** | Century Schoolbook 12pt |
| **Tab column** | Bold, left-aligned |
| **Section title** | Bold, sentence case |
| **Description line** | Regular weight, italic, 10pt, indented 0.25" |
| **Page numbers** | Right-aligned, aligned with a dot leader ( ............ ) |
| **Header** | Applicant name + document title + consulate + date |
| **Divider lines** | Thin horizontal rules at top and bottom |
| **Page number** | Lowercase Roman numeral (i) — index pages use Roman numerals |
| **Length** | Ideally one page; two pages maximum |

### Dot Leaders

Professional legal indexes use dot leaders (.........) between the section title and the page number. This is the standard in court filings, legal briefs, and government documents. It makes page numbers easy to locate at a glance.

```
Tab D   Cover Letter ..................................................  5
Tab E   Applicant Nationality Evidence ...............................  10
Tab F   Investment Evidence — Funds Committed ........................  11
```

```
Counted pages = all pages from Tab D onward (excluding divider pages)
NOT counted = Cover page + Table of Contents + Tab divider pages + Tabs A, B, C
Alert threshold = 48 pages (warn at 48; hard stop at 50)
```

## The Governing Principle: Documents Must Be Easy to Review Under Time Pressure

A consular officer reviewing a 50-page E-2 package is not reading — they are **scanning and verifying**. Every formatting decision must serve that reality. Headers and footers tell the officer where they are at any moment. Headings tell them what a section argues before they read it. Bullet points present facts in scannable form. Paragraph breaks give the eye a rest.

> *"Use visual devices and tools to make things easier on your reader. Paragraph breaks are critical. Where appropriate, use organizational devices like numbering, bullet point lists, charts, and timelines."*
> — Judge Ranjan, U.S. District Court (W.D. Pennsylvania), Brief-Writing Preferences

## 1. Running Page Header

Every page of the document body (excluding the cover page and tab dividers) should have a **running header** at the top of the page.

### Header Design

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  [APPLICANT FULL NAME]          E-2 Treaty Investor Visa    │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
│                                                             │
│   [Body text begins here...]                                │
```

| Element | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Left side** | Applicant's full legal name — 10pt, regular weight |
| **Right side** | "E-2 Treaty Investor Visa" — 10pt, regular weight |
| **Divider line** | Thin horizontal rule (0.5pt) below the header text, spanning full text width |
| **Font** | Century Schoolbook, 10pt |
| **Top margin** | Header sits within the 1" top margin — does not push body content down |
| **Color** | Black |

### What the Header Communicates

If pages are separated, printed, or reviewed out of order, the header on every page immediately re-identifies the applicant and the document type. This is a standard in all federal court filings and DOJ immigration submissions.

## 2. Running Page Footer

Every page of the body should have a **running footer** at the bottom, placed below a thin rule.

### Footer Design

```
│   [Body text ends here...]                                  │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
│  [Last Name] — E-2 Application — Tab D               Page 7 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Element | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Left side** | `[Last Name] — E-2 Application — Tab [X]` — 10pt |
| **Right side** | `Page [N]` — 10pt |
| **Divider line** | Thin horizontal rule (0.5pt) above the footer text |
| **Font** | Century Schoolbook, 10pt |
| **Color** | Black |
| **Bottom margin** | Footer sits within the 1" bottom margin |

### Footer Example

```
Kowalski — E-2 Application — Tab H                    Page 27
```

### Why This Exact Format

California, federal, and DOJ court rules all require the document's title or abbreviation in the footer — separated from the body by a rule line — along with the page number. The left-side identifier ensures that if the package is ever disassembled, every single page can be returned to its correct place without question.

## 3. Heading Structure Inside the Document

### The Three-Level System

Legal documents use a clear three-level heading hierarchy. Every heading level has a distinct visual identity so the officer can assess document structure at a glance.

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
LEVEL 1 — MAJOR SECTION HEADING
All caps, bold, 12pt, left-aligned, underlined
Spacing: 24pt before, 12pt after
═══════════════════════════════════════════════

Level 2 — Subsection Heading
Title case, bold, 12pt, left-aligned
Spacing: 18pt before, 6pt after
───────────────────────────────────────────────

Level 3 — Sub-subsection heading
Sentence case, bold italic, 12pt, left-aligned
Spacing: 12pt before, 6pt after
```

### Heading Rules from Legal Writing Authorities

1. **Write headings as full, informative sentences** — not topic labels [Columbia Law School; SF Bar Association]
   - ❌ Weak: "Investment Amount"
   - ✅ Strong: "The Investment of $180,000 USD Is Substantial Relative to the Total Cost of the Enterprise"

2. **Normal sentence capitalisation — never all-initial caps** [Bryan Garner, ABA Journal; SF Bar]
   - ❌ Wrong: "The Investment Is Substantial And Meets The E-2 Threshold"
   - ✅ Right: "The investment is substantial and meets the E-2 threshold"
   - Exception: LEVEL 1 headings use ALL CAPS as a structural separator — not because it is good style, but because it creates an unmistakable visual break between major sections

3. **Bold, not underline or italic, for emphasis in headings** [Columbia Law; Bryan Garner]

4. **Parallel structure across all headings at the same level** — if one Level 2 heading is a complete sentence, they all must be

5. **No footnotes appended to headings** [Bryan Garner, ABA Journal]

6. **Number with Roman numerals at Level 1, then A/B/C, then 1/2/3** [Columbia Law]:
   ```
   I.   INVESTMENT AND SUBSTANTIALITY
     A. The total investment of $180,000 meets the substantiality standard
        1. Funds were irrevocably committed prior to the visa application
        2. The investment represents 85% of the total acquisition cost
   ```

## 4. Body Text Formatting

### Paragraph Style

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Font | Century Schoolbook, 12pt |
| Line spacing | Double-spaced (2.0) for all formal documents |
| Alignment | **Justified** — both left and right edges aligned |
| First-line indent | 0.5" — traditional legal format |
| Max paragraph length | 8 lines — break longer paragraphs for scannability |
| Space between paragraphs | 0pt before, 0pt after (double-spacing provides separation) |
| Widow/orphan control | On — never leave a single line stranded at top or bottom of page |

### The Cover Letter Exception

The cover letter (Tab D) uses **business letter format**, not legal brief format:

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Line spacing | 1.15 |
| Alignment | Left-aligned (not justified) |
| Paragraphs | Block style — no indent; one blank line between paragraphs |
| Max paragraph length | 5–6 lines |

## 5. Bullet Points — When to Use and How to Format Them

### When Bullet Points Are Appropriate

Bullet points serve a specific function in legal documents — they are for **lists of facts, items, or evidence** that would be awkward in sentence form. They are not for arguments, conclusions, or analysis. The argument lives in the paragraphs. The evidence can live in bullets.

✅ **Use bullets for:**
- Lists of documents submitted (e.g., "The following documents are enclosed at Tab F:")
- Lists of factual items (e.g., business assets, employees, bank account transactions)
- Chronological timelines of events
- Checklists or verification items
- Financial items in a summary

❌ **Do not use bullets for:**
- Legal arguments or E-2 eligibility analysis (these belong in full paragraphs)
- Anything requiring explanation or context
- Anything where the relationship between items matters (use numbered lists instead)
- Replacing paragraphs to avoid writing — officers notice

### Bullet Point Format

```
The following documents establish the irrevocable commitment of investment
funds as of the date of this application:

    •   Wire transfer confirmation dated March 14, 2025 — $150,000 USD
        (First National Bank of Canada → Business Checking Account #XXXX)

    •   Franchise fee payment confirmation dated March 20, 2025 — $45,000 USD
        (Franchisor: [Name] — acknowledged receipt attached)

    •   Commercial lease deposit dated March 22, 2025 — $12,000 USD
        (Three months' rent: $4,000/month × 3 months)
```

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Bullet character** | Solid round bullet (•) — the standard in legal documents |
| **Indent** | 0.5" from left margin |
| **Hanging indent** | Second line of a bullet aligns with first line of text (not the bullet) |
| **Font** | Century Schoolbook, 12pt (same as body text) |
| **Spacing** | Single-spaced within a bullet; 4pt space between bullets |
| **Line length** | Max 65 characters per line — stay within the text block |
| **Sub-bullets** | Use sparingly; indent an additional 0.5"; use em dash (—) or hollow bullet (◦) |

### Numbered Lists vs. Bullet Points

Use **numbered lists** (1. 2. 3.) when:
- Order matters (chronology, sequence of events)
- You will refer back to a specific item ("as noted in item 3 above")
- There are more than 6 items (numbered lists are easier to reference)

Use **bullets** (•) when:
- Order does not matter
- Items are roughly equal in weight
- There are 3–6 items

## 6. Emphasis — Bold, Italic, Underline

Used sparingly in legal documents. The more you emphasise, the less any emphasis means.

| Element | When to Use | How to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Bold** | Key facts, dollar amounts, dates, legal standards | Sparingly — one or two instances per page maximum |
| *Italic* | Case names, defined terms, foreign words, document titles | Standard legal convention |
| Underline | Level 1 headings only | Not used in body text — looks like a hyperlink |
| ALL CAPS | Level 1 headings only | Never in body text |
| Colour highlight | Never in submitted documents | Review copies only |
| Strikethrough | Never | |

### The Emphasis Rule

> If more than one word per paragraph is bolded, you have too much emphasis. Bold is a signal that says "stop here — this is the key fact." If everything is key, nothing is.

## 7. Tables Inside Documents

Tables appear in the business plan (financial projections), source of funds (funds trail), and summary sections of the cover letter.

### Table Style

```
┌────────────────────────────┬──────────────────┬──────────────┐
│ DATE                       │ TRANSACTION      │ AMOUNT (USD) │
├────────────────────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────┤
│ March 14, 2025             │ Wire transfer     │  $150,000    │
│ March 20, 2025             │ Franchise fee     │   $45,000    │
│ March 22, 2025             │ Lease deposit     │   $12,000    │
├────────────────────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────┤
│ TOTAL INVESTED             │                  │  $207,000    │
└────────────────────────────┴──────────────────┴──────────────┘
```

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| Header row | Bold, 10–11pt, light gray fill (#E8E8E8) |
| Body rows | 10–11pt, alternating white / very light gray |
| Totals row | Bold, slightly darker fill |
| Borders | Single 0.5pt rule — neutral dark gray (#595959) |
| Numbers | Right-aligned in their column |
| Text | Left-aligned in their column |
| Font | Century Schoolbook or Calibri 11pt (Calibri acceptable inside tables) |
| Color | No decorative color — gray fills only |
| Spacing | Single-spaced within table |
| Caption | 10pt italic, above or below the table: *Table 1: Investment Funds Summary* |

## 8. White Space — The Silent Design Element

White space in a legal document is not empty — it is breathing room. It tells the officer's eye where one idea ends and the next begins.

### Mandatory White Space Rules

- **Before every Level 1 heading:** 24pt space (equivalent to one blank line in double-spaced text)
- **Before every Level 2 heading:** 18pt space
- **Before every bulleted list:** One blank line
- **After every bulleted list:** One blank line
- **After the introductory paragraph of a section:** No extra space — let double-spacing provide the rhythm
- **Before a table:** One blank line + table caption
- **After a table:** One blank line

### Page Break Rules

- **Always insert a manual page break before a Level 1 heading** that would otherwise appear at the bottom of a page
- **Never allow a heading to sit at the bottom of a page** with fewer than two lines of body text following it
- **Never allow the last line of a paragraph to sit alone at the top of a new page** (widow line)

## 9. The Overall Reading Experience — What the Document Represents

When a consular officer opens an E2go-generated document, the reading experience should feel like opening a **well-prepared legal brief from a mid-size immigration law firm** — not a template, not a form letter, not a school report.

### The Character of the Document

| Quality | How It Is Achieved |
|---|---|
| **Authority** | Century Schoolbook font; full-sentence headings; formal justified alignment |
| **Clarity** | Short paragraphs; clear heading hierarchy; bullet points for fact lists |
| **Completeness** | Every section answers its E-2 criterion; no unanswered questions left on the page |
| **Precision** | Specific dollar amounts, specific dates, specific legal standards cited |
| **Restraint** | No decoration, no color, no graphics — confidence through simplicity |
| **Professionalism** | Consistent formatting throughout; no font switching; no inconsistent spacing |

### What the Document Should NOT Feel Like

- ❌ A Word document with default formatting (Calibri 11pt, left-aligned, no structure)
- ❌ A template with obviously generic filler text
- ❌ A marketing brochure (color, images, branded headers)
- ❌ A form with blank spaces filled in by hand
- ❌ Anything that looks like it was generated and not reviewed

### The Standard to Aim For

The gold standard is the look and feel of a **U.S. Supreme Court amicus brief** or a **well-prepared immigration petition from a major law firm**:

- Clean white pages
- Generous margins
- Serif font, consistent throughout
- Section headings that tell you the argument before you read the paragraph
- Facts and figures in precise, scannable form
- No word wasted; no repetition
- Every page numbered and identified

> The document should communicate, through its appearance alone, that the applicant is the kind of person who runs a business with the same level of organisation, precision, and professionalism.
