## Overview

The entity structure you choose for your E-2 business affects:
- How your business income is taxed (once, or twice)
- Whether you pay self-employment tax on all profits or only on salary
- What forms you file annually
- Whether the entity qualifies for S Corporation election
- Your personal liability protection

Most E-2 investors use a **single-member LLC** or **multi-member LLC.** A small
number use S Corporations for tax efficiency once income exceeds a certain
threshold. C Corporations are rarely optimal for individual E-2 investors.

This document covers the four main structures: single-member LLC (SMLLC),
multi-member LLC, S Corporation, and C Corporation.

## Part 1 — Single-Member LLC (SMLLC)

### Formation

An LLC is formed at the **state level** — not federal. Each state has its own
formation rules, filing fees, and annual requirements. Common steps:
1. File Articles of Organization with the state (typically $50–$500 fee)
2. Draft an Operating Agreement (not always legally required, but strongly advised)
3. Obtain an EIN from the IRS (see below)
4. Open a business bank account
5. Register for state/local business licenses as required

### Federal Tax Treatment — Disregarded Entity

By default, the IRS treats a single-member LLC as a **disregarded entity** —
meaning the LLC does not exist separately from you for federal income tax
purposes.

> "For income tax purposes, an LLC with only one member is treated as an
> entity disregarded as separate from its owner, unless it files Form 8832
> and affirmatively elects to be treated as a corporation."
> — IRS: Single Member Limited Liability Companies (irs.gov)

**What disregarded entity means in practice:**
- No separate federal income tax return for the LLC
- All business income, expenses, and deductions reported on **Schedule C** of
  your personal Form 1040
- Business profit flows directly to your personal return
- You pay income tax at your individual marginal rate
- You pay **self-employment tax (15.3%)** on net business profit

### EIN (Employer Identification Number)

An SMLLC does not need an EIN for income tax purposes if it has no
employees and no excise tax liability.

> "A single-member LLC that is a disregarded entity that does not have
> employees and does not have an excise tax liability does not need an EIN."
> — IRS: Single Member Limited Liability Companies (irs.gov)

**However**, you should obtain an EIN to:
- Open a business bank account (most banks require it)
- Hire employees in the future
- Establish business credit
- Maintain clear separation between personal and business finances

Obtain an EIN online for free at irs.gov/ein. Takes approximately 5 minutes.

### Annual Tax Forms (SMLLC — Disregarded Entity)

| Form | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Schedule C (with Form 1040) | Business profit and loss |
| Schedule SE (with Form 1040) | Self-employment tax calculation |
| Form 1040 | Personal income tax return |
| FinCEN 114 / Form 8938 | If applicable (foreign accounts) |

No separate business tax return is required.

### Liability Protection

Despite the federal tax treatment as a disregarded entity, the LLC remains
a **separate legal entity** under state law. This means your personal assets
are generally protected from business debts and lawsuits — subject to proper
maintenance of the corporate formality (separate bank account, no commingling
of personal and business funds).

## Part 2 — Multi-Member LLC

### Tax Treatment — Partnership

A multi-member LLC is taxed as a **partnership** by default.

- The LLC files **Form 1065** (U.S. Return of Partnership Income) annually
- Each member receives a **Schedule K-1** showing their share of income,
  losses, deductions, and credits
- Members report their K-1 income on their personal Form 1040 (Schedule E)
- Each member pays self-employment tax on their distributive share of
  active business income

### Relevance to E-2 Partnership Applications

If two E-2 applicants are co-investors (e.g., a 50/50 partnership), their
U.S. business is typically structured as a multi-member LLC taxed as
a partnership. Each partner files Form 1040 with Schedule E and pays SE tax
on their share of active income.

See also: `kb/e2-visa-partnership-applications.md` for immigration implications
of 50/50 structures.

### Annual Tax Forms (Multi-Member LLC)

| Form | Purpose | Due Date |
|---|---|---|
| Form 1065 | Partnership return | March 15 |
| Schedule K-1 (for each member) | Member's share of income | With Form 1065 |
| Form 1040 + Schedule E | Personal return (each member) | April 15 |

## Part 3 — S Corporation

### What Is an S Corporation?

An S Corporation is a corporation that has made a special election with the
IRS to be taxed as a pass-through entity under Subchapter S of the Internal
Revenue Code.

> "S corporations are corporations that elect to pass corporate income,
> losses, deductions, and credits through to their shareholders for federal
> tax purposes. Shareholders of S corporations report the flow-through of
> income and losses on their personal tax returns and are assessed tax at
> their individual income tax rates."
> — IRS: S Corporations (irs.gov)

### Why E-2 Investors Consider S Corp Status

The primary tax advantage of an S Corporation over an SMLLC (disregarded
entity) is **FICA/SE tax savings** once business income is high enough to
make the structure worthwhile.

**SMLLC (disregarded entity):**
All net profit is subject to SE tax (15.3% on first $176,100; 2.9% above)

**S Corporation:**
- Owner pays themselves a **reasonable salary** — FICA applies on salary only
  (6.2% SS + 1.45% Medicare × 2 for employer + employee = 15.3% total on salary)
- Remaining profit distributed as **owner distributions** — not subject to FICA
- Net result: Only the salary portion bears the full FICA burden

**Example:**
- Net business profit: $180,000
- Reasonable salary set at: $80,000
- FICA on salary: ~$12,240 (employer + employee combined)
- Distributions: $100,000 — no FICA
- Tax savings vs. SMLLC: approximately $9,180–$15,300 depending on income level

> ⚠️ The IRS scrutinizes S Corporation salary levels. You must pay yourself
> a "reasonable salary" — not an artificially low salary designed solely to
> minimize FICA. The IRS has successfully challenged and penalized S Corp
> owners who paid themselves nominal salaries. Engage a CPA to determine an
> appropriate salary level for your role and industry.

### S Corporation Eligibility Requirements

To elect S Corporation status, the entity must:
- Be a **domestic corporation** (formed in a U.S. state)
- Have **no more than 100 shareholders**
- Have only **one class of stock**
- Have only **allowable shareholders:** individuals, certain trusts, and estates
  (no partnerships, corporations, or non-resident aliens as shareholders)

> Source: IRS — S Corporations (irs.gov); IRS — About Form 2553 (irs.gov)

**Important for E-2 applicants:** Non-resident aliens cannot be S Corporation
shareholders. However, E-2 holders who are U.S. **resident aliens** (having
passed the Substantial Presence Test) can be S Corporation shareholders —
because resident aliens are not "nonresident aliens" for this purpose.

### How to Elect S Corporation Status — Form 2553

File **Form 2553 (Election by a Small Business Corporation)** with the IRS.

**Timing requirements:**
- Must be filed **no later than 2 months and 15 days after the beginning of
  the tax year** the election is to take effect
- OR at any time during the preceding tax year

> "The form should be filed with the IRS before the 16th day of the third
> month of your business tax year. Or before the 15th day in the second month
> of your business tax year if the year is 2.5 months or less."
> — Block Advisors (IRS Form 2553 guidance)

**Late election relief:** Available under Rev. Proc. 2013-30 if reasonable
cause exists for the late filing.

Form 2553 **cannot be e-filed** — it must be mailed or faxed to the IRS.

### Annual Tax Forms (S Corporation)

| Form | Purpose | Due Date |
|---|---|---|
| Form 1120-S | S Corporation income tax return | March 15 |
| Schedule K-1 (for each shareholder) | Shareholder's share of income | With 1120-S |
| Form W-2 | Owner's salary (must be on payroll) | January 31 |
| Form 941 | Quarterly payroll tax deposits | Quarterly |
| Form 1040 + Schedule E | Personal return | April 15 |

## Part 4 — C Corporation

### Tax Treatment — Double Taxation

A C Corporation is taxed at the **corporate level** (21% flat federal rate
as of 2026) and again at the **shareholder level** when dividends are
distributed (typically 15–20% qualified dividend rate).

> Source: PwC Tax Summaries — United States Corporate Tax (taxsummaries.pwc.com)

**Result:** Most income earned in a C Corporation faces effective combined
tax rates of 36–40%+ before reaching the owner's pocket — significantly higher
than pass-through structures.

### When C Corps Are Used by E-2 Investors

C Corporations are rarely the right choice for individual E-2 investors.
They may be appropriate when:
- You plan to raise outside equity investment (investors prefer C Corps)
- You intend to pursue venture capital or institutional funding
- You plan to eventually go public
- You need to retain large amounts of income in the business (the 21%
  corporate rate may be lower than your personal rate for retained earnings)

For most E-2 service businesses, retail businesses, and franchise operations,
a C Corporation creates unnecessary tax complexity and double taxation.

### Annual Tax Forms (C Corporation)

| Form | Purpose | Due Date |
|---|---|---|
| Form 1120 | Corporate income tax return | April 15 |
| Form W-2 | Shareholder-employee salary | January 31 |
| Form 1040 | Personal return (for salary and dividends) | April 15 |

## Part 5 — Choosing the Right Structure for Your E-2 Business

| Factor | SMLLC | Multi-Member LLC | S Corp | C Corp |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Formation simplicity | ✅ Simple | ✅ Moderate | ⚠️ Requires election | ⚠️ Complex |
| Pass-through taxation | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |
| SE tax on all profit | ❌ Yes | ❌ Yes (active income) | ✅ Only on salary | N/A |
| SE tax savings at high income | ❌ No | ❌ No | ✅ Yes | N/A |
| Annual compliance burden | ✅ Low | ⚠️ Moderate | ⚠️ High | ❌ Highest |
| Payroll required | ❌ No | ❌ No | ✅ Yes (reasonable salary) | ✅ Yes |
| 100 shareholder limit | N/A | N/A | ❌ Yes | ✅ No limit |
| Non-resident alien shareholders | N/A | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |
| Typical E-2 use | ✅ Most common | ✅ Partnership applications | ✅ Income >~$80K | ❌ Rare |

### General Recommendation

- **Starting out / income under ~$60,000 net:** SMLLC (disregarded entity)
  — lowest compliance cost, simplest filing
- **Income above ~$80,000 net:** Evaluate S Corporation election with a CPA
  — SE tax savings may exceed the additional compliance cost
- **50/50 co-investor E-2 application:** Multi-member LLC taxed as a partnership
- **Venture-backed / fundraising path:** C Corporation (but this is uncommon
  for E-2 applications)
