# RRSP, TFSA & Registered Accounts — Cross-Border Guide for E-2 Applicants

*Sources: CRA, IRS Rev. Proc. 2014-55, Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article XVIII),
FinCEN, CRA T4058, CRA canada.ca/tfsa/non-resident*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## Overview

When a Canadian becomes a non-resident and moves to the United States on an
E-2 visa, their registered accounts — RRSP, RRIF, TFSA, RESP, RDSP — are
not automatically closed or transferred. Each account type has distinct rules
governing what happens to it at the point of departure and ongoing, both under
Canadian law and U.S. tax law.

This is one of the most consequential financial planning decisions for a
Canadian E-2 applicant. The stakes include:
- Departure tax consequences (or exemptions)
- Canadian withholding tax on withdrawals as a non-resident
- U.S. tax treatment of the same withdrawals
- FBAR and FATCA reporting obligations in the U.S.
- Potential double taxation if the treaty position is not properly claimed

**The bottom line: Do not make any decisions about registered accounts
without consulting a cross-border CPA.**

## RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan)

### At Departure — Canadian Rules

RRSPs are **exempt from the deemed disposition rules.** Your RRSP is not
considered sold when you leave Canada. The assets inside your RRSP remain
sheltered from departure tax.

> Source: CRA — Departure Tax (canada.ca); Income Tax Act s. 128.1(10)

Your RRSP can remain open and invested while you are a non-resident.
You do not need to collapse or transfer it when you leave.

**However:**
- You **cannot make new contributions** to your RRSP as a non-resident
  and receive a Canadian tax deduction (you have no Canadian earned income
  to deduct against)
- Contributions made as a non-resident may result in over-contribution
  penalties
- Your RRSP contribution room does not grow while you are a non-resident
  (contribution room is based on Canadian earned income)

### Withdrawals as a Non-Resident — Canadian Withholding Tax

When you withdraw from your RRSP as a non-resident, the Canadian financial
institution is required to withhold **Part XIII tax** before remitting the
balance to you.

Standard rate: **25%**

Under the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article XVIII), the withholding rate
is reduced:
- **Periodic payments** (regular, scheduled withdrawals or annuity
  payments): **15%**
- **Lump-sum withdrawals** (full or large one-time withdrawal): **25%**
  (the treaty does not reduce the rate on lump-sum withdrawals from RRSPs
  in most cases)

> Source: CRA — Rates for Part XIII Tax (canada.ca);
> Canada-U.S. Tax Convention, Article XVIII

**Practical implication:** If you plan to draw down your RRSP while in
the U.S., structuring withdrawals as periodic payments rather than a lump
sum can reduce Canadian withholding from 25% to 15%.

To claim the reduced treaty rate, your Canadian financial institution will
require you to complete a **NR301 form** (Declaration of Eligibility for
Benefits Under a Tax Treaty) or equivalent.

### RRSP — U.S. Tax Treatment

This is the area where the most mistakes are made.

**Under U.S. domestic tax law,** income that accrues inside a foreign
pension plan (including an RRSP) is generally taxable in the U.S. in the
year it accrues — even if not distributed. This would make RRSP
tax-deferral effectively meaningless for U.S. residents.

**Under the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty (Article XVIII(7)) and IRS Revenue
Procedure 2014-55,** U.S. residents (including E-2 holders who become
resident aliens) can elect to defer U.S. tax on RRSP income until it is
actually distributed from the plan — just like an IRA or 401(k).

**This election is now automatic.** Under Rev. Proc. 2014-55, eligible
individuals are treated as having automatically made the deferral election.
You do not need to file Form 8891 (which was eliminated by this procedure).

**Who qualifies:**
- U.S. citizens and resident aliens (including E-2 holders who pass the
  Substantial Presence Test)
- Who file U.S. tax returns for every year they held the RRSP
- Who include any actual RRSP distributions as income on their U.S. returns

> Source: IRS Rev. Proc. 2014-55; KPMG IES Flash Alert 2014-093

**When you withdraw from your RRSP:**
- The gross withdrawal amount is includable as ordinary income on your
  U.S. federal return (Form 1040)
- You receive a foreign tax credit for the Canadian withholding tax paid
  (to avoid double taxation)
- Net result: you pay the higher of Canadian withholding (15-25%) or
  U.S. marginal rate, not both in full

### RRSP — FBAR Reporting

Once you become a U.S. resident alien, your Canadian RRSP is a **foreign
financial account** for FBAR purposes.

**FBAR filing trigger:** If the aggregate value of all your foreign
financial accounts (including RRSP, TFSA, Canadian bank accounts, etc.)
exceeds **USD $10,000 at any point during the calendar year,** you must
file FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) by April 15 (with automatic extension to October 15).

> Source: FinCEN — Report Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (fincen.gov)

### RRSP — Form 8938 (FATCA)

If your RRSP value exceeds the FATCA reporting thresholds, you must also
report it on **IRS Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial
Assets),** filed with your U.S. tax return.

FATCA thresholds (for individuals living in the U.S.):
- Single / MFS: more than $50,000 on the last day of the year, or more
  than $75,000 at any point during the year
- MFJ: more than $100,000 on the last day of the year, or more than
  $150,000 at any point during the year

## TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account)

### At Departure — Canadian Rules

TFSAs are also **exempt from the deemed disposition rules.** Your TFSA is
not collapsed or taxed when you leave Canada.

**However, critical rules apply once you become a non-resident:**

1. **You cannot contribute to your TFSA as a non-resident.**
   If you make a contribution while a non-resident, CRA imposes a
   **1% monthly tax** on the contribution amount until it is withdrawn
   or you resume Canadian residency.

2. **You do not accumulate new TFSA contribution room** while a non-resident.
   TFSA contribution room accrues only in years you are a Canadian resident
   aged 18 or older.

3. **You can withdraw from your TFSA as a non-resident.** Withdrawals
   from a TFSA are not subject to Canadian withholding tax under domestic
   Canadian law.

4. **Withdrawal room is reinstated — but only when you become a Canadian
   resident again.** The withdrawn amount is added back to your contribution
   room in the following calendar year, but only becomes available when you
   resume residency.

> Source: CRA — How Non-Residency Affects Your TFSA (canada.ca)

### TFSA — U.S. Tax Treatment

**This is the most important TFSA fact for E-2 applicants:**

The TFSA receives **no tax-free treatment under U.S. federal tax law.**

For U.S. residents (including E-2 holders who are resident aliens), TFSA
income — interest, dividends, capital gains earned inside the TFSA — is
**fully taxable in the U.S. in the year earned,** under IRC Section 61.

The Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty does not provide relief for TFSAs because
TFSAs are not specifically recognized under Article XVIII (which covers
pension plans). The CRA and IRS have not reached a bilateral agreement
on TFSA treatment.

> Source: Taxes for Expats — TFSA Tax Implications for U.S. Expats (2026);
> IRS Publication 519

**Practical implication:**
- If you have a significant TFSA, consider withdrawing the balance (in full
  or in part) **before** you become a U.S. resident alien, while you can
  still benefit from its tax-free status in Canada
- After becoming a U.S. resident, the TFSA functions essentially like a
  taxable brokerage account for U.S. tax purposes — there is no advantage
  to keeping funds in it from a U.S. perspective

### TFSA — FBAR and FATCA

Your TFSA is a foreign financial account for FBAR and FATCA purposes
once you are a U.S. resident alien. The same reporting thresholds
described above for RRSPs apply.

## RRIF (Registered Retirement Income Fund)

If you have converted your RRSP to an RRIF before departing Canada,
the same rules as RRSP apply:
- Exempt from deemed disposition at departure
- Mandatory minimum annual withdrawals continue
- Part XIII withholding tax (25% standard; 15% under treaty for
  periodic payments) applies to all withdrawals as a non-resident
- U.S. tax deferral under Rev. Proc. 2014-55 applies
- FBAR and FATCA reporting required

---

## RESP (Registered Education Savings Plan)

RESPs do not receive treaty protection and are subject to more complex
rules as a non-resident:

- Government grants (CESG) may need to be repaid if the plan is collapsed
  and the beneficiary is no longer a Canadian resident
- Income inside the RESP (accumulated income payments) is subject to
  Part XIII withholding tax when withdrawn as a non-resident
- U.S. tax treatment of RESP income is uncertain — no treaty protection

**Recommendation:** If you have an RESP with a beneficiary who is moving
with you to the U.S., consult a cross-border CPA before departing. Depending
on the beneficiary's future plans (Canadian university vs. U.S. university),
the optimal strategy varies significantly.

---

## RDSP (Registered Disability Savings Plan)

RDSPs have complex rules that intersect with Canadian disability benefits.
Non-resident RDSP holders should consult a specialist. General rules:
- Non-residents can hold an RDSP but cannot contribute
- Government bonds and grants may be clawed back if the beneficiary
  ceases to be a Canadian resident for an extended period

## Optimal Withdrawal Strategy — RRSP as a Non-Resident

The general framework for efficient RRSP drawdown as a U.S. resident:

**Phase 1 — Pre-departure (while still a Canadian resident):**
- Consider making RRSP withdrawals in years where your Canadian income
  is lower than usual (e.g., year of departure, part-year income)
- Withdrawals while a Canadian resident are taxed at your Canadian
  marginal rate — no withholding tax, but full Canadian income tax applies

**Phase 2 — After departure, before U.S. residency is established:**
- Brief window if you depart mid-year and are not yet a U.S. resident alien
- In this dual-status period, RRSP withdrawals may be subject only to
  Canadian withholding (15% or 25%) and not U.S. income tax
- This window is narrow and requires careful planning — get specialist advice

**Phase 3 — As a U.S. resident alien:**
- Periodic withdrawals attract 15% Canadian withholding
- Full withdrawal amount is includable on U.S. 1040
- Foreign tax credit reduces U.S. tax by the Canadian withholding paid
- Net: you pay the difference between your U.S. marginal rate and the 15%
  already withheld by Canada

**Key question for your CPA:**
"What is the optimal annual RRSP withdrawal amount that minimizes
total combined Canadian and U.S. tax on my registered account balances
over the next 10 years?"
