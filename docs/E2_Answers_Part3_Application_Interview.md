# PART 5 — APPLICATION PROCESS & DOCUMENTS (Questions 116–155)

---

**Q116. What is the overall step-by-step process for a Canadian applying for
the E-2 visa at the Toronto consulate?**

The consular process for Canadian applicants follows this sequence:

1. **Confirm eligibility** — Canadian citizenship, qualifying business, sufficient
   investment
2. **Establish the business** — Form LLC/Corp, obtain EIN, open US bank account,
   sign lease or franchise agreement, begin licensing applications
3. **Deploy investment funds** — Wire funds to US business account; keep all
   receipts and wire confirmations
4. **Complete the DS-160** — Online nonimmigrant visa application at
   ceac.state.gov; must be completed before scheduling the interview
5. **Pay the MRV fee** — $315 USD visa application fee (as of 2025–2026)
6. **Schedule the consulate interview** — At the US Consulate General in Toronto
7. **Assemble the application binder** — 11 tabbed sections (Tabs A through L),
   50-page limit per tab
8. **Prepare for the interview** — Practice answering the 5 core officer questions
9. **Attend the interview** — At the Toronto consulate window
10. **Await decision** — On-the-spot approval, 221(g) administrative processing,
    or denial
11. **Receive passport/visa** — Returned by Canada Post if approved; stamp valid
    for 5 years

  Pre-Interview Compliance Calendar

**Q118. What is the DS-160 form and when do I fill it out?**

The DS-160 is the online Nonimmigrant Visa Application form required for all
nonimmigrant US visa categories, including the E-2. It is completed online at
ceac.state.gov — not on paper. The DS-160 collects personal information,
travel history, employment history, and security questions. You must complete
and submit it before you can schedule your consulate interview appointment.
The DS-160 confirmation page (with barcode) becomes Tab A of your application
binder. The DS-160 now also requires disclosure of social media accounts used
in the past 5 years — do not omit accounts intentionally, as this is a federal
disclosure requirement.

**Q119. What is the MRV fee and how much is it?**

The Machine Readable Visa (MRV) fee is the nonimmigrant visa application fee
paid to the US Department of State before scheduling the interview. As of
2025–2026, this fee is **$315 USD** for E-visa categories (E-1 and E-2). This
fee is non-refundable regardless of whether the visa is approved or denied.
It is paid separately from any other fees (such as the USCIS filing fee for a
Change of Status application).

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Compliance Calendar item: "Pay MRV visa
  application fee $315 USD"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "What is the application fee?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q121. Does the physical application binder need to be mailed in advance, or
do I bring it to the interview?**

You bring the physical application binder with you to the interview at the
Toronto consulate. The consulate officers review the package before and during
your interview. After the interview, if approved, your passport is returned to
you by Canada Post with the visa stamp — the binder remains with the consulate.

**Q122. What is the cover letter (Tab D) and why is it the most important
document in the package?**

The cover letter is the investor's personal narrative addressed to the consular
officer — it serves as the officer's primary roadmap through the entire
application package. A well-structured cover letter means the officer arrives
at the interview window having already answered most of their own questions,
typically resulting in an approval interview of under 10 minutes. A poorly
structured cover letter forces the officer to search through 11 tabs for answers,
creating uncertainty and inviting scrutiny. Your knowledge base states explicitly:
"The cover letter is the most important document in the package."

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "The Cover Letter Structure
That Wins Approvals"

**Q123. What are the 8 paragraphs of a winning E-2 cover letter?**

Based on Toronto consulate-specific guidance, a winning cover letter mirrors
the officer's five mental questions and is structured as follows:

1. **Introduction & Treaty Eligibility** — Who the applicant is, Canadian
   citizenship, E-2 treaty basis, one-sentence business overview
2. **The Business: Real and Operating** — What the business does, where, current
   operational status (lease signed, licenses applied for, equipment ordered)
3. **The Investment: Substantial and At Risk** — Total USD invested, allocation
   breakdown, wire dates, confirmation funds are at risk
4. **Source and Path of Funds** — Where investment funds came from, reference
   to Tab H for full chronological trail
5. **Non-Marginality** — Year 1, 3, and 5 revenue projections vs. household
   income need; hiring plan with specific employee counts and timeline
6. **The Investor's Role: Develop and Direct** — Specific title, specific
   responsibilities, decisions the investor will make; must show active manager,
   not passive investor
7. **Investor Qualifications** — Relevant experience, education, prior business
   ownership, industry knowledge
8. **Non-Immigrant Intent** — Canadian ties maintained; Canadian bank accounts
   kept open; family in Canada; provincial health coverage maintained; E-2
   acknowledged as nonimmigrant visa

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "The Cover Letter Structure
That Wins Approvals: Paragraphs 1–8"

**Q124. What goes in Tab F (Proof of Investment)?**

Tab F is the investment evidence tab — it proves that funds have been

- Signed commercial lease or property purchase agreement

- Lease deposit receipts
- Business bank account statement showing funds received
- Template 3 (Business Asset Register) listing all assets purchased with costs

**Q126. What goes in Tab H (Source and Path of Funds)?**

Tab H is the complete, chronological source of funds narrative — the document
the officer spends the most time reviewing. It includes:
- Template 1 (Source and Path of Funds narrative) — AI-generated from the
  investor's fund history data
- Chronological table: date, event, amount CAD/USD, account/institution —
  tracing every step from origin to US business account
- Supporting exhibits: bank statements for all accounts involved, tax returns
  (if used), property sale agreement and settlement statement, RRSP redemption
  confirmation, wire transfer confirmations
- Gift letter (if applicable) signed by the donor with documentation of the
  donor's source of wealth

**Q127. What is the EIN and do I need it before the interview?**

The EIN (Employer Identification Number) is the US federal tax identification
number for a business entity — the equivalent of a Canadian Business Number.
It is required for: LLC/corporation tax filing, hiring employees, opening a US
business bank account, and applying for business licenses. For E-2 purposes,
having an EIN before the interview is important — it is evidence the business
entity is real and in process. Your knowledge base states: "EIN must be obtained
before the interview in most cases — it demonstrates the business is real and
in process." Canadian applicants can obtain an EIN without a US Social Security
Number (SSN) by calling the IRS International Line: 1-267-941-1099.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.11 — "EIN: Required for LLC/
corporation tax filing, hiring employees, opening business bank accounts.
Canadian applicants can obtain an EIN without an SSN by calling the IRS
International Line."

**Q128. Does my business need to have a website before the interview?**

A business website is not a strict legal requirement but it is strongly
recommended. A live professional website is evidence the business is real,
in operation, and being taken seriously. Your knowledge base notes: "A
professional website strengthens the 'real and operating enterprise' argument,
particularly for new businesses. Consider having a basic website live before
your interview." At minimum, a basic site showing the business name, location,
services, and contact information is advisable.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3C-13: "A professional website
strengthens the real and operating enterprise argument."

**Q129. Do I need to have employees already hired before the interview?**

No — employees do not need to be hired before the interview for an initial E-2
application. However, your hiring plan must be credible: you need a documented
Year 1 hiring plan with specific roles, hire dates, and salary figures in your
business plan (Tab K / Template 5). Having even one employee hired or actively
recruiting before the interview strengthens the application. A business with
absolutely no staffing plan and no intention of hiring raises immediate
marginality concerns.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3E-01: "A business with no
employees is at high risk of being deemed marginal. We strongly recommend
including at least 1–2 full-time employee positions in your Year 1 plan."

**Q130. What photos should I bring of the business?**

Include photos of:
- The exterior of the business location (storefront, building, signage)
- The interior (office space, retail floor, service area — depending on business
  type)
- Any equipment on-site
- Signage (interior and exterior if available)
- The space under renovation or build-out (if not yet complete — shows progress)
These are included in Tab G. Photos establish that the business has a physical
presence beyond a registration certificate.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — Officer Question 2 checklist:
"Photos of the physical location"

**Q131. What is the 50-page tab limit and how do I manage it?**

The Toronto consulate imposes a 50-page limit per tab (per section of the
application binder). If any single tab exceeds 50 pages, it should be condensed.
For example: Tab H (source of funds) can become very long if you include years
of bank statements — include only the most relevant pages (statements showing
the key transactions in the fund chronology, not every monthly statement for
10 years). Use an exhibit summary sheet to reference additional documents rather
than including every page. Quality and relevance matter more than volume.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.2 — "50-page limit per tab at
Toronto consulate"

**Q132. What is the Change of Status (COS) path vs. consular processing?
Which should I choose?**

| Factor                  | Consular (Toronto)           | Change of Status (USCIS)     |
|-------------------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Who uses it             | Most Canadians               | Those already in US on valid  |
|                         |                              | nonimmigrant status           |
| Form                    | DS-160                       | Form I-129                   |
| Interview               | Required                     | Usually NOT required          |
| Validity granted        | 5-year visa stamp            | 2-year I-94 increment         |
| Travel during process   | Normal                       | Cannot leave US               |
| Typical processing      | 60–90 days (incl. wait)      | 3–6 months                   |
| MRV/filing fee          | $315 USD                     | $730 USD (I-129 fee, 2026)   |

**Key note on COS:** A USCIS Change of Status approval does NOT give you an
E-2 visa stamp. If you leave the US after a COS, you must apply for the E-2
stamp at the Toronto consulate before re-entering.

**Q133. What is the USCIS Form I-129 used for in the E-2 context?**

Form I-129 (Petition for a Nonimmigrant Worker) is the USCIS form used to:
(1) apply for a Change of Status to E-2 from inside the US; or (2) extend
E-2 status (2-year increment) from inside the US without traveling to Toronto.
It is filed with USCIS — not at the consulate. It requires an E Classification
Supplement specific to the E-2 category. Do not travel internationally while
a Form I-129 is pending — USCIS may consider the petition abandoned if you
depart the US.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Path B USCIS Status Extension:
"Do not travel internationally while an I-129 is pending"

**Q134. What happens after I submit my binder at the interview — how long does
it take to get my passport back?**

For approvals at the Toronto consulate, the visa stamp is placed in your passport
and the passport is returned to you by Canada Post — typically within approximately
5 business days after the approval interview. You should not plan international
travel (especially entering the US) until you have the passport with the visa
stamp in hand. Track the passport return timing in your compliance calendar.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Post-interview calendar:
"Interview +5 days: Canada Post returns visa"

**Q135. How long is the E-2 visa stamp valid for?**

The E-2 visa stamp issued at the Toronto consulate is valid for **5 years** for
Canadian citizens — one of the longest E-2 validity periods granted globally
(a benefit of the Canada-US relationship). This means you can enter and exit
the US multiple times over 5 years without returning to Toronto for a new stamp.
Each time you enter the US on the E-2 stamp, the CBP officer at the port of
entry determines how long you can stay — typically up to 2 years per entry
(noted on your I-94).

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Consular processing: 5-year stamp"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q136. What is an I-94 and how is it different from my visa stamp?**

Your **visa stamp** is in your passport — it allows you to apply for entry into
the US at the border. Your **I-94** is your actual record of admission — it
records the date you entered and how long you are authorized to stay. The two
are separate: a 5-year E-2 visa stamp allows you to travel to the US border
repeatedly, but each actual authorized stay is governed by your I-94, which is
typically set at 2 years per entry for E-2 holders. If your I-94 expires while
you are in the US, you are out of status — regardless of whether your visa
stamp is still valid.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Compliance Calendar: "I-94
expiry tracking" as a static compliance trigger

**Q137. Can I enter the US before the business is fully open?**

Yes — but you should have substantial operational progress in place. The E-2
allows entry "to develop and direct" the business, which includes the pre-opening
setup phase. However, entering on the E-2 and then doing nothing to operate the
business for an extended period creates compliance risk. You should be actively
working on the business from the point of entry.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "entering solely to develop and
direct the enterprise"

**Q138. What is the compliance calendar and what does it track?**

The compliance calendar is a personalized, dynamic timeline that tracks every
deadline, action item, and milestone in the E-2 journey — from 6 months before
the interview through 5 years of post-approval compliance and renewal. It is
built from three layers: (1) the target interview date as an anchor (all
pre-interview items work backward from it); (2) static triggers that apply to
all applicants (DS-160, MRV fee, interview scheduling); and (3) dynamic triggers
generated from the applicant's specific profile (state licensing timelines for
their business type, FBAR filing deadlines, child age-out alerts, renewal window).

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Compliance Calendar Architecture

**Q139–Q155. Application Process — Condensed Set**

**Q139. What social media accounts do I need to disclose on the DS-160?**
All social media platforms used in the past 5 years must be disclosed on the
DS-160. This is a federal requirement — do not omit accounts intentionally. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3A-09

**Q140. Do I need a medical exam for the E-2 visa?**
A medical exam (Form I-693) is generally NOT required for nonimmigrant visa
categories including the E-2 — it is required primarily for green card (immigrant
visa) applications. Confirm with the US Embassy Canada for any current-year
requirement changes. [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q141. Can a family member or lawyer attend the interview with me?**
The interview takes place at a consulate window — it is an individual interview.
Attorneys do not typically attend alongside the applicant at the window, though
they may accompany you to the consulate building. Your knowledge base describes
the physical setup: "Interview takes place at a window — not a private room.
The officer has a glass partition between them and the applicant." [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13

**Q142. What language is the interview conducted in?**
The E-2 interview at the Toronto consulate is conducted in English. French-
speaking Canadians may request an interpreter — this is permitted at the Toronto
consulate. The need for an interpreter should be indicated in advance so it can
be noted in preparation materials. Non-English fluency does not affect eligibility.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3F-09

**Q143. How many times can I be rejected and reapply?**
There is no legal limit on the number of E-2 applications you can file.
However, each denial is on your immigration record and may require explanation
in subsequent applications. A denial based on 214(b) immigrant intent is
particularly serious and must be directly addressed if you reapply. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — Common Denial Reasons

**Q144. Can I use a lawyer to prepare my E-2 application?**
Yes — and for complex situations (prior visa issues, unusual fund sources, F-1
status, removal proceedings), it is strongly recommended. An immigration
attorney provides legal advice beyond what a preparation tool can offer. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Outcome 6: Attorney Referral Required

**Q145. Is the application binder assembled in order from Tab A to Tab L?**
Yes — the binder is assembled sequentially, Tab A through L, with each section
clearly tabbed and labeled. Officers review in a specific order (cover letter
first, then business plan, then source of funds), but the binder should be
complete and orderly so the officer can navigate any tab directly. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — How an Officer Reads the Package

**Q146. What is the Visa Category Confirmation Letter (Tab C)?**
Tab C is a brief letter (typically 1 page) confirming that the applicant is
applying for the E-2 Treaty Investor visa category, referencing the treaty basis
(Canada-US treaty), and confirming that all E-2 requirements are met. It is
generated from the application data and serves as a category confirmation
document for the officer's reference. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.2 — Tab C

**Q147. What is Tab J (Applicant Qualifications) and what does it contain?**
Tab J documents the investor's qualifications to operate the specific business.
It contains: a professional resume (work history, education, certifications),
an organizational chart showing the investor's management position, a
qualifications narrative explaining why the investor's background makes them
credible to develop and direct this particular enterprise, and evidence of prior
business ownership if applicable. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.4 — Template
4 Organizational Chart

**Q148. What is Tab L (Dependent Family Information)?**
Tab L contains documentation for the investor's spouse and dependent children
who will be accompanying them to the US on E-2 dependent status. This includes:
each dependent's personal information (name, date of birth, relationship), their
own DS-160 confirmation pages, passport copies, and any supporting documentation.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.2 — Tab L

**Q149. Do children under 14 need to attend the interview?**
Applicants under 14 years of age are not required to appear in person at the
US consulate in Canada for their visa interview. [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada
E-Visa FAQs — "Do I have to come to a consulate to apply in person? Applicants
under 14 years of age do not need to appear in person."
https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q150. What happens if my application binder is incomplete at the interview?**
An incomplete binder typically results in a 221(g) administrative suspension —
the officer will identify the missing items and request supplemental documentation.
This is not a denial but significantly delays the process. Ensure all 11 tabs
are complete and present before attending the interview. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.4 — 221(g) Administrative Suspension

**Q151. Do I need to bring original documents or are copies acceptable?**
The US Embassy Canada indicates that you should bring original documents where
possible, particularly for passports and vital records. For financial documents
(bank statements, wire confirmations, property sale records), certified copies
or clear photocopies organized in the binder are standard. Follow the US Embassy
Canada document guidance for the specific requirements at the time of your
application. [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q152. Can I submit additional documents after the interview if I forget
something?**
If you receive a 221(g) request, you can and must submit the requested documents.
If no 221(g) is issued (i.e., a decision is made at the interview), unsolicited
post-interview submissions are generally not effective. This is why thorough
pre-interview preparation is so important — you cannot easily supplement an
already-decided application. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — 221(g)
Administrative Suspension

**Q153. What is the Application Confidence Score and how is it calculated?**
The Application Confidence Score is an 8-dimension readiness assessment generated
after Module 3 is complete. It scores the application package on: (1) Investment
Substantiality (25 pts); (2) Source/Path of Funds (20 pts); (3) Non-Marginality
(20 pts); (4) Active Direction/Development (15 pts); (5) Business Plan Quality
(10 pts); (6) Investor Qualifications (5 pts); (7) Real Operating Enterprise
(5 pts); and (8) Immigrant Intent Risk (modifier, not scored). Score bands:
90–100 Excellent; 80–89 Strong; 70–79 Good; 60–69 Needs Work; below 60 Not Ready.
It is an educational tool — not a prediction of visa outcome. [KB] E2Pathway
Vol 3, Section 7.5

**Q154. How far in advance should I start preparing for the E-2 interview?**
Your knowledge base builds a 6-month preparation timeline working backward from
the target interview date. The 6-month window allows for: state licensing
applications (which can take 2–6 months); entity formation and EIN; opening the
US business bank account; deploying investment funds; building the business plan
and application documents; completing the DS-160; and running multiple interview
simulations. Starting with less than 3 months before the interview creates
serious risk of being unprepared or having licensing not yet in process. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Pre-Interview Compliance Calendar

**Q155. What is the Organization Chart (Template 4) and why does it matter?**
The Organization Chart (Tab J) is a visual diagram showing the investor's
management position within the business hierarchy. It demonstrates that the
investor occupies an apex, active management role — not a passive or peripheral
one. For partnership applications, it shows both investors' distinct management
lanes. The chart is accompanied by a narrative confirming the investor's day-
to-day responsibilities and decision-making authority. An officer who sees "Owner"
in a box with nothing beneath it has no evidence of genuine management structure.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.4 — Template 4 Organizational Chart

# PART 6 — INTERVIEW PREPARATION (Questions 156–195)

**Q156. What are the 5 questions the consulate officer is mentally asking
throughout the interview?**

Every Toronto consulate officer reviewing an E-2 application works through
five questions in this specific order:

1. **Is this person a Canadian citizen from a treaty country?** (Passport check —
   easiest question; no complexity for Canadian applicants)
2. **Is this a real business — not a shell or scheme?** (Looking for operational
   evidence beyond paper)
3. **Is the investment substantial and at risk?** (Proportionality + at-risk
   documentation)
4. **Will this business generate more than a marginal living?** (Revenue
   projections + hiring plan)
5. **Does this person intend to return to Canada?** (Non-immigrant intent —
   the most subjective question; lives in the interview, not the package)

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "The Officer's Mental Checklist:
Five Questions in Order"

**Q157. What does the Toronto consulate interview actually look like?**

Key physical and procedural facts from documented Toronto consulate experiences:
- The interview takes place at a **window** — not a private room
- There is a **glass partition** between the applicant and the officer
- The officer is **looking at their screen** reviewing the file while asking questions
- They may **type notes** while the applicant is speaking
- Typical duration: **5–10 minutes** for straightforward approvals; **10–20
  minutes** for more complex cases; cases resulting in 221(g) may be cut short
  once the officer identifies the issue

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "The Interview Itself: What to
Expect at Toronto"

**Q158. What is the very first question the officer typically asks?**

Based on documented Toronto consulate experiences, officers typically begin with
an open-ended overview question: **"Tell me about your business."** This is
followed by specific questions about investment and funds, then the hiring plan,
then the investor's specific role, and possibly "Why this location?" Officers
rarely ask highly technical legal questions — they are more interested in whether
the investor genuinely knows their own business.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "The Officer's Approach:
Toronto officers typically ask an open-ended overview question first."

**Q159. What are the 9 universal interview questions that every E-2 applicant
should be able to answer cold?**

These questions appear in every simulation regardless of business type:

1. Give me an overview of your business.
2. How much have you invested and how were the funds allocated?
3. Where did your investment funds come from?
4. What is your specific role in the business?
5. How many employees do you currently have, or plan to hire?
6. What are your revenue projections for Year 1 and Year 3?
7. Is the business currently operational?
8. Why did you choose this business in this location?
9. **What happens to the business if your visa is not approved today?**

Question 9 is particularly important — your answer must demonstrate that the
investment is genuinely at risk and that business planning is serious, not
contingent on visa approval. A strong answer: "The business entity and
investment are already committed. We would need to wind down operations if
the visa is not approved, but the investment funds are already deployed."

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.6 — Universal Questions UQ-01
through UQ-09

**Q160. What does the officer observe beyond the words of your answers?**

Beyond the content of the answers, the officer observes:
- Does the investor speak about the business with **genuine knowledge and
  confidence**?
- Does the investor **hesitate when asked about financial figures**? (suggests
  they did not prepare their own package)
- Are the answers **consistent with what is written in the package**?
- Does the investor seem like someone who will **actually run this business**?

The goal of interview preparation is not to memorize numbers — it is to speak
about your own business as if you built it yourself. An investor who confidently
explains their business, investment, and hiring plan in plain language will
always outperform one who nervously recites memorized statistics.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "What the Officer is Observing"

**Q161. What triggers an on-the-spot approval?**

On-the-spot approvals occur when all of the following are true:
- The cover letter answers all five officer questions clearly
- The source of funds chronology is complete with no gaps
- The investment is clearly at risk (receipts, contracts, bank statements)
- The business plan projections are realistic and supported by market data
- The investor can answer every question about their own business confidently
- The officer finds no reason to doubt the legitimacy of any element

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "What Triggers a 221(g) vs.
an On-the-Spot Approval"

---

**Q162. What triggers a 221(g) administrative suspension?**

A 221(g) is not a denial — it is a request for additional information or
processing. It is typically triggered by:
- Missing source of funds documentation for a specific deposit or asset sale
- Business plan projections that appear unrealistic without explanation
- Lease or franchise agreement not yet executed (business not in process)
- Prior US immigration issues flagged during processing
- Security or background checks (administrative processing — no documents
  requested; applicant simply waits)
- Local market research missing for the specific city/county
- Questions about the business model that require additional evidence

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "What Triggers a 221(g)"

---

**Q163. What triggers an outright denial?**

Outright refusals are most commonly triggered by:
- **214(b) Immigrant Intent:** Sold Canadian home before visa approval; surrendered
  all Canadian ties; answers suggesting permanent intent
- **Investment not substantial:** Amount too low, poorly documented
- **Business deemed marginal:** Projections too low, no hiring plan, solo operator
- **Source of funds unclear:** Unexplained cash deposits, incomplete trail
- **Disqualifying business category:** Passive real estate, cannabis, non-profit

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "Outright Refusals: Most
Commonly Triggered By"

**Q164. How do I answer the "Why are you maintaining Canadian ties?" question?**

This is one of the most strategically important interview questions. Your
knowledge base coaches: the most accepted answer format is: "I intend to
operate this business for as long as my E-2 visa permits. If the business is
no longer viable, or if I no longer hold E-2 status, I will return to Canada.
I maintain strong ties to Canada including [family / property / bank accounts /
professional connections]." The key principle: acknowledge that the E-2 is a
nonimmigrant visa and express that return to Canada is your genuine long-term
intent if the business basis ends.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3H-08 Tooltip: "The most
common answer officers accept"

---

**Q165. How should I answer "What happens to the business if your visa is not
approved today?"**

This question tests whether the investment is genuinely at risk and whether
the business commitment is real. A strong answer: "The business entity is
already formed, the investment has already been deployed, and the lease/franchise
agreement is signed. If the visa is not approved, the business would need to
wind down and I would return to Canada — but the investment is already committed
and at risk." Avoid any answer that implies the investment will simply be
refunded or that the business plan was contingent on visa approval.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.6 — Universal Question UQ-09

---

**Q166. Do I need to memorize my financial projections word-for-word?**

No — and in fact, nervously reciting memorized numbers is a red flag for officers.
The goal is to speak about your projections with the natural confidence of someone
who built the plan themselves. You should know: your total investment amount; the
major allocation categories (franchise fee, build-out, working capital, etc.);
Year 1, Year 3, and Year 5 revenue projections; your household income need and
how the projections compare; and the number of employees you plan to hire and
when. Know these as context — not as a script.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "The simulator must train users
to speak about their business from memory — not to recite numbers they memorized
the night before."

**Q172. What questions are triggered if I have a high immigrant intent risk

If you have sold your Canadian home, have no Canadian family remaining, or have
other high-risk intent signals:
1. What ties do you maintain to Canada?
2. What would cause you to return to Canada?
3. Do you intend to apply for US permanent residence?

These questions are among the hardest to prepare for because they are subjective.
Your answers must be consistent, genuine, and supported by the ties-to-Canada
evidence in your cover letter and package.

**Q173. How does the interview simulator work?**

The interview simulator is a personalized mock interview tool that generates
questions from your actual application data — not from a generic question library.
Every question references your specific business name, investment amount,
business type, source of funds, and risk profile. It operates in two modes:
- **Text Mode:** Type your answers; AI evaluates the written response
- **Voice Mode:** Speak your answers; Whisper API transcribes; same AI
  evaluation engine assesses the transcription

After each answer, immediate feedback is provided: what the officer was really
asking; what your answer addressed; what was missing; and a stronger answer
framework using your specific data. Voice mode also detects filler words (um,
uh, like) and flags answer pace.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.6 — Simulator Architecture and
In-Session Flow

**Q174. How many times should I run the interview simulator before the real

Your knowledge base builds three scheduled simulation sessions into the

tracks your score across sessions and shows your improvement trend. The goal is
not just to reach 11/11 "Strong" answers but to answer with natural confidence,
not rote recitation.
