# U.S. Immigration Treatment of Canadian Common‑Law Partnerships for E‑2 Dependents

*Scope: Focus on U.S. Department of State (DOS) consular practice under 9 FAM for E‑2 derivative spouses, with cross‑references to USCIS policy where it sheds light on the definition of “spouse” and marriage validity. This is not legal advice; edge cases should be reviewed with qualified U.S. immigration counsel.*

---

## 1. Core rule: does U.S. immigration law recognize Canadian common‑law partnerships as equivalent to legal marriage for E‑2 spouse status?

### 1.1. DOS definition of “marriage” and common‑law marriage

The Foreign Affairs Manual provision that governs how consular officers determine whether a relationship qualifies as a “marriage” is 9 FAM 102.8‑1 (Family‑Based Relationships). It applies across immigrant and nonimmigrant contexts, including derivative E‑2 spouses.[web:17][page:1]

9 FAM 102.8‑1(B) establishes the baseline rule that **the “law of the place of celebration” controls**: a marriage is valid for U.S. visa purposes if it was “properly and legally performed in the place of celebration and legally recognized” there, subject to limited public‑policy exceptions.[page:1]

9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) then addresses common‑law marriage directly:

> “In the absence of a marriage certificate, an official verification, or a legal brief verifying full marital rights, a common law marriage or cohabitation is a ‘valid marriage’ for purposes of visa adjudication only if it is legally recognized in the place in which the relationship was created and is fully equivalent in every respect to a traditional marriage. To be ‘fully equivalent,’ the relationship must bestow all the same legal rights and duties possessed by partners in a lawfully contracted marriage, including that: (1) the relationship can only be terminated by divorce or death; (2) there is a potential right to alimony; (3) there is a right to intestate distribution of an estate; and (4) there is a right of custody if there are children.”[page:1]

9 FAM 102.8‑1(G) applies the same equivalence test to civil unions and domestic partnerships.[page:1]

**Implication for Canadian common‑law couples:**

- U.S. law does **not** automatically treat “common‑law” or cohabiting partners as spouses. The relationship must be a **legally recognized common‑law *marriage*** in the jurisdiction where it arose and must confer essentially the **same rights and obligations as a formal marriage**, including termination only by divorce or death, alimony, intestate inheritance rights, and custody rights.[page:1]
- Canadian federal and provincial regimes generally recognize *common‑law partners* for certain benefits but **Canada does not have a general institution of “common‑law marriage”** in the U.S. sense; cohabitation seldom creates a status that is fully equivalent to marriage on all four FAM factors.[web:46][web:52][web:49]

Leading practitioner analysis aimed specifically at E‑2, L‑1, H‑1B and similar nonimmigrant dependents concludes that **Canadian common‑law partnerships do not, as a rule, meet the 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) standard**, because they can typically be ended without divorce proceedings and often do not grant full marital rights to property and intestate inheritance.[web:29][web:53][web:45]

**Answer to Q1:** For E‑2 derivative purposes, **U.S. consulates and USCIS generally do *not* recognize Canadian common‑law partnerships as equivalent to a legal marriage**, unless you can demonstrate that the specific provincial regime creates a status that is legally identical to marriage on all four 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) criteria. In practice, that bar is rarely met for Canadian couples, so most posts will require a formal marriage certificate to issue an E‑2 spouse visa.

## 2. Does recognition depend on the Canadian province?

### 2.1. FAM framework

9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) asks whether the relationship is “legally recognized in the place in which the relationship was created.”[page:1] For Canadian couples, “place in which the relationship was created” is generally the province/territory where they first established the qualifying status.

Thus, **any potential recognition is province‑specific**: the officer must look to the law of Ontario, British Columbia, Quebec, Alberta, etc., and then ask whether that regime grants the couple a status that is fully equivalent to marriage on termination, support, intestacy, and custody.

### 2.2. High‑level comparison of selected provinces

Canadian sources emphasize that **“common‑law spouse” is not a unified status across Canada**, and that provincial family‑law rights for cohabiting partners differ substantially.[web:39][web:48]

The table below summarizes, for each province, (a) the threshold to be treated as a “spouse” or equivalent under provincial family law, and (b) key differences from married spouses that matter for the FAM “full equivalence” test:

| Province | When cohabiting partners become “spouses” under provincial law (illustrative) | Key differences from marriage relevant to 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Ontario** | Spousal support rights may arise after three years of continuous cohabitation or a relationship of some permanence with a child under the Family Law Act; property division and intestate succession still distinguish married spouses from common‑law partners.[web:48][web:49] | Common‑law partners generally **do not get the same automatic intestate inheritance or equalization rights** as married spouses; they rely on wills and dependent‑support claims.[web:49] This makes “full equivalence” difficult to prove.
| **British Columbia** | Under the Family Law Act, people who live in a “marriage‑like relationship” for at least two years are treated as spouses for most property and support issues.[web:39][web:48] | BC is among the most marriage‑like regimes for cohabiting partners, but federal and comparative commentary still treat it as a **separate category**, not identical in all respects, and case law stresses that IRCC’s federal “common‑law partner” definition differs from BC’s “spouse” definition.[web:39][web:48] Potentially closer to meeting FAM’s test, but not clearly “fully equivalent in every respect.”
| **Quebec** | “Conjoints de fait” are recognized for some provincial schemes, but Quebec civil law does **not** treat common‑law partners as married, and they have no automatic rights to spousal support or intestate inheritance on separation or death.[web:52][web:46] | Provincial and federal guidance emphasize that common‑law partners in Quebec have **significantly fewer rights** than married spouses, especially for support and succession, which is inconsistent with the FAM requirement that the relationship only be terminable by divorce or death and that there be alimony and intestate rights.[web:52]
| **Alberta** | Alberta has “adult interdependent partners” (AIPs): people in a relationship of interdependence for three years or of some permanence with a child, or who sign an AIP agreement.[web:48][web:54] | SSA analysis notes that an AIP can be treated as a “spouse” for certain U.S. Social Security purposes because Alberta intestacy law gives AIPs the **same status as a spouse for inheritance**.[web:54] However, Canadian commentary still treats AIPs as a distinct category, and there is no direct DOS or USCIS guidance equating AIPs with marriage for visas.
| **Other provinces** | Cohabitation thresholds range from one to three years, sometimes requiring a child (e.g., Manitoba, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Newfoundland, PEI, territories).[web:48] | Most provinces give some support and property rights to common‑law partners after a threshold period, but **none create a regime universally equivalent to marriage** on all FAM criteria; intestate succession and property equalization are the most consistent gaps.[web:48][web:49]

### 2.3. Practical State Department interpretation for Canadian provinces

While DOS has not issued province‑by‑province examples in 9 FAM, practitioner commentary aimed at Canadian‑U.S. cross‑border couples concludes that:

- **Canadian “common‑law partner” status (as defined for IRCC) is not the same as a common‑law marriage in the FAM sense.** It is primarily a statutory category for immigration, tax, and benefit programs, not a general family‑law status identical to marriage.[web:23][web:25][web:31]
- Several provinces (especially Ontario and Quebec) explicitly **preserve more extensive rights for married spouses** than for common‑law partners, particularly in intestacy and property equalization.[web:49][web:52]
- As a result, Canadian common‑law partners typically **fail the “full equivalence” test in 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F)**, regardless of province.[web:29][web:53][web:45]

**Answer to Q2:** Recognition is, in theory, province‑dependent because consular officers must look at the law where the relationship was created. In practice, no Canadian province clearly confers a common‑law status that is *fully identical* to marriage across termination, alimony, intestacy, and custody, so common‑law partners from Ontario, BC, Quebec, Alberta, or any other province should **not expect to be treated as E‑2 “spouses”** without a formal marriage. A narrow argument might be attempted for Alberta AIP or BC “spouse” status, but there is no published DOS or USCIS precedent endorsing such an approach, and practitioners consistently caution against relying on it.[web:29][web:53]

## 3. Documentation that could be used if trying to qualify as a “spouse” based on a common‑law regime

### 3.1. FAM standard for evidence

9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) assumes that in ordinary marriage cases there is “a marriage certificate, an official verification, or a legal brief verifying full marital rights.” In its absence, the officer must decide whether the common‑law status is legally recognized and fully equivalent to marriage.[page:1]

For an E‑2 derivative case where a couple wanted to argue that their common‑law status qualifies as a marriage under 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F), they would need to provide:

- **Official proof of the status** in the province where it arose, such as:
  - A provincial registry certificate (e.g., Alberta AIP agreement registration) if available.[web:48][web:54]
  - Provincial court orders or judgments explicitly describing the parties as “spouses” for support or property division.
- **Legal evidence that the status is fully equivalent to marriage**, potentially including:
  - Statutory excerpts showing that partners in that status can only end the relationship through court‑ordered separation or divorce, not simply by moving out.
  - Statutes or case law showing a right to spousal support equivalent to that of married spouses.[web:52]
  - Estate and succession law provisions demonstrating that the surviving partner has the same intestate inheritance rights and preferential share as a married spouse.[web:49][web:54]
  - Custody and parental rights provisions showing that common‑law partners are treated identically to married spouses upon separation.[web:48]
- **A coherent legal memorandum** from Canadian counsel tying those provincial provisions back to the four 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) criteria, plus translations where needed.

### 3.2. Practical practitioner guidance

In practice, U.S. immigration lawyers advising Canadian E‑2, L‑1, TN, and H‑1B families generally **recommend that common‑law couples marry civilly before filing U.S. immigration applications**, rather than trying to prove FAM‑level equivalence of their provincial status.[web:29][web:45][web:33]

Practitioner materials specific to Canadian common‑law partnerships warn that:

- Canadian common‑law partners often **cannot show termination only by divorce or death**, because provincial law allows separation without the formal divorce process that applies to married spouses.[web:45][web:48]
- Differences in intestacy rights and property equalization compared to married spouses mean the relationship is **not “fully equivalent in every respect”**, which is what 9 FAM requires.[web:29][web:49]

**Answer to Q3:** If a couple nevertheless wants to test a common‑law‑as‑marriage argument, they should be prepared to submit provincial statutes, court decisions, official registration or agreements, and a legal brief from Canadian counsel proving that their status meets all four 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) criteria. However, for product design and user guidance, you should **assume that consular officers will not treat Canadian common‑law couples as spouses without a formal marriage certificate**, and your platform should strongly encourage marriage where E‑2 spousal benefits are required.

## 4. Minimum cohabitation period required by USCIS or DOS

### 4.1. FAM does not set a generic cohabitation duration

9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) does **not** specify a minimum period of cohabitation as a matter of U.S. law.[page:1] Instead, it defers to **local law**: if the jurisdiction’s common‑law marriage regime requires a particular duration, that becomes relevant to whether a marriage exists, but DOS does not add an independent U.S.‑law cohabitation threshold.

By contrast, Canadian immigration and some provincial laws use specific durations (e.g., IRCC’s one‑year definition of “common‑law partner,” BC’s two‑year “spouse” definition, Ontario’s three‑year definition for some claims).[web:23][web:25][web:39][web:48]

### 4.2. USCIS practice on common‑law marriage

USCIS guidance on marriage validity in the Policy Manual and associated alerts focuses on the **place‑of‑celebration rule** and on whether the marriage is legally valid and not contrary to public policy; it does not impose a standard federal “cohabitation period” either.[web:33][web:24][web:44]

In the limited contexts where USCIS recognized informal marriages (for example, in certain historical refugee/asylee spousal cases), the key question was whether the country of first asylum legally recognized such unions; the recent 2025 update actually **tightens** policy to require formal legal validity under local law, rather than informal unions, even for those categories.[web:44]

**Answer to Q4:** There is **no FAM or USCIS‑imposed minimum cohabitation period** for common‑law relationships. The operative threshold is whatever the local jurisdiction requires to treat the relationship as a legally valid marriage, but Canadian common‑law regimes generally grant a *partnership* status for defined durations rather than a full‑equivalence marriage, so the issue for Canadian couples is **not** cohabitation length but failure to meet the FAM “fully equivalent to marriage” test.

## 5. Recent policy developments and AAO/USCIS treatment

### 5.1. DOS / FAM

- The current version of 9 FAM 102.8‑1, including the common‑law marriage section (F), was consolidated and updated in May 2021 (CT:VISA‑1272).[page:1]
- No subsequent FAM revisions specifically change the treatment of common‑law marriages for derivative E‑2 spouses. Recent E‑visa‑specific updates have focused on issues like deference to prior determinations of “substantial investment,” not on family status definitions.[web:27]

### 5.2. USCIS Policy Manual

- In 2025, USCIS issued Policy Alert PA‑2025‑23 updating the Policy Manual discussions of qualifying spousal relationships for family‑based immigrant petitions.[web:24][web:33]
  - The update re‑affirms the **place‑of‑celebration rule** and provides more detail on public‑policy concerns, proxy and virtual marriages, and minors.[web:33]
  - Practitioner summaries emphasize that the policy **does not liberalize recognition of informal or common‑law unions**; if anything, related refugee/asylee guidance in PA‑2025‑22 moves away from recognizing informal marriages where a country does not legally recognize them.[web:44]
- These changes mainly affect immigrant categories, but they confirm the general U.S. stance: **the marriage must be legally valid under local law**, and USCIS is narrowing rather than expanding exceptions for informal unions.

### 5.3. AAO and other decisions

A search of publicly available AAO decisions and practitioner databases does **not** reveal any prominent, precedential AAO decision directly on a **Canadian** common‑law partnership being treated as a qualifying spousal relationship for E‑2 or other nonimmigrant derivative status. Available commentary instead treats Canadian common‑law cases as examples where couples **needed to marry** before a petition could be approved.[web:29][web:53]

Given:

- The explicit FAM requirement of full equivalence for common‑law marriages;[page:1]
- The lack of a Canadian province whose cohabitation regime satisfies that test in all respects;[web:46][web:48][web:49][web:52]
- And USCIS’s recent trend toward stricter insistence on formal legal validity,[web:24][web:44]

there is **no indication in recent policy updates or AAO decisions that Canadian common‑law partnerships have gained broader recognition** for U.S. immigration purposes.

**Answer to Q5:** Since the 2021 consolidation of 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F), there have been **no DOS changes specifically expanding recognition of common‑law marriages**. USCIS’s 2025 Policy Manual updates stress legal validity and public‑policy compliance, and related refugee/asylee guidance rolls back recognition of informal marriages. There are **no known AAO precedents** that elevate Canadian common‑law partnerships to full spousal status for E‑2 or other derivatives.

## 6. Same‑sex Canadian common‑law partnerships vs opposite‑sex partnerships

### 6.1. FAM equal‑treatment rule for same‑sex marriages

9 FAM 102.8‑1(E) states that **same‑sex marriages are valid for visa adjudication purposes if recognized in the place of celebration**, and remain valid even if the applicant applies in a country where same‑sex marriage is illegal.[page:1]

This codifies the effect of U.S. v. Windsor (2013) and Obergefell v. Hodges (2015), after which **USCIS and DOS must treat same‑sex marriages the same as opposite‑sex marriages** for immigration benefits, including derivative nonimmigrant visas.[web:20][web:34][web:26][web:40]

USCIS and DOS guidance after Windsor made clear that **same‑sex spouses are eligible for the same spousal immigration benefits as opposite‑sex spouses**, provided the marriage is legally valid where celebrated.[web:20][web:34]

### 6.2. Application to common‑law relationships

However, the FAM common‑law rule (9 FAM 102.8‑1(F)) and civil‑union/domestic‑partnership rule (9 FAM 102.8‑1(G)) are **facially gender‑neutral**.[page:1] They do not distinguish between same‑sex and opposite‑sex couples; the test is whether the relationship is legally recognized and fully equivalent to marriage.

Practitioner commentary notes that same‑sex couples benefit from equal treatment **once they are legally married**, but that informal statuses (domestic partnerships, civil unions, common‑law partnerships) are treated identically across orientations: they only qualify if fully equal to marriage in the relevant jurisdiction.[web:20][web:33]

Because Canadian common‑law regimes generally **do not grant fully equivalent rights** to marriage for either same‑sex or opposite‑sex partners, both are treated the same way: **neither will usually be accepted as a “spouse” for U.S. immigration dependent purposes without a formal marriage**.[web:29][web:53]

**Answer to Q6:** For purposes of E‑2 derivative visas, a same‑sex Canadian common‑law partnership is **not treated differently** from an opposite‑sex common‑law partnership. Both are subject to the same 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) “full equivalence” test, which Canadian common‑law regimes generally fail. Once a couple is legally married (in Canada or elsewhere), same‑sex and opposite‑sex spouses are treated identically under U.S. immigration law.

## 7. Practical product design implications for your E‑2 preparation platform

Based on the current FAM language and practitioner interpretations, your platform serving Canadian applicants should be built around the following conservative assumptions:

1. **Require a formal marriage for E‑2 spouse cases.** Your default guidance should be that Canadian common‑law partners need a civil marriage certificate to qualify for E‑2 derivative spouse status, regardless of their province.
2. **Province‑agnostic messaging, with nuance for advanced users.** At the user‑interface level, avoid implying that Ontario, BC, Quebec, Alberta, or any other province creates a “marriage‑equivalent” common‑law status for U.S. immigration purposes. Reserve province‑specific nuance (e.g., Alberta AIP, BC “spouse” status) for advanced help articles that still end with a strong recommendation to marry and/or seek bespoke legal advice.[web:29][web:53]
3. **No platform‑level support for “testing” common‑law equivalence.** Because proving 9 FAM 102.8‑1(F) equivalence requires complex legal analysis of provincial family, estate, and support law, your product should not attempt automated determinations. Instead, when a user indicates they are common‑law and not married, your decision tree should:
   - Explain that DOS and USCIS generally do not recognize Canadian common‑law partnerships as marriages for E‑2 spouse visas;[page:1][web:29]
   - Suggest marriage as the standard pathway; and
   - Recommend individualized legal advice if they wish to explore an exception.
4. **Same‑sex couples: emphasize equal treatment of *marriage*, not of common‑law.** Make clear that same‑sex spouses with a Canadian marriage certificate are fully eligible for E‑2 derivative status on the same terms as opposite‑sex spouses, but that same‑sex common‑law partnerships face the same obstacles as opposite‑sex ones.[page:1][web:20][web:34]
5. **Explain the difference between Canadian and U.S. concepts.** Your educational content should highlight that Canada’s “common‑law partner” concept (including IRCC’s one‑year definition) is **broader** than the very narrow “common‑law marriage fully equivalent to marriage” concept used in 9 FAM, and that this mismatch is why Canadian cohabiting couples often need to marry before using U.S. immigration processes.[web:23][web:25][web:39][web:29]

These assumptions will help you design flows that are both user‑friendly for Canadian applicants and aligned with the conservative reading of current DOS and USCIS guidance.
