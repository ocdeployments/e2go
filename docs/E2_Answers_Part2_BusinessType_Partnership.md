# PART 3 — BUSINESS TYPE & FRANCHISE (Questions 53–90)

---

**Q53. What types of businesses work best for an E-2 application?**

Based on Toronto consulate patterns, the strongest E-2 business types combine:
(1) a clear investment figure; (2) an active management role; (3) a hiring plan
that demonstrates U.S. job creation; and (4) revenue projections that exceed
3–4x the investor's household income need. Top categories for Canadian applicants:

| Business Category              | E-2 Compatible? | Min. Investment (USD) | Notes                                       |
|-------------------------------|-----------------|----------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Senior home care (non-medical)| Strong          | $100K–$200K          | Top Canadian choice; recurring revenue      |
| Commercial cleaning/janitorial| Strong          | $75K–$150K           | Low licensing; easy job creation story      |
| Childcare/daycare             | Strong          | $150K–$350K          | Strong job creation; franchise options      |
| Staffing/recruitment agency   | Strong          | $100K–$200K          | Job creation is built into the model        |
| Food franchise (QSR)          | Strong          | $200K–$500K          | Strong brand; strong business plan evidence |
| Tutoring/learning centre      | Strong          | $100K–$250K          | Franchise options available                 |
| Retail franchise              | Strong          | $150K–$350K          | Must show active management                 |
| IT/tech consulting (with staff)| Compatible     | $100K+               | Must have employees; not solo consulting    |
| Real estate management company| Conditional     | $150K+               | Active management ONLY; not passive rental  |
| Home-based service business   | Conditional     | $75K+               | Must show client interaction and workspace  |
| Solo consulting (no staff)    | Risky/Disqualify| N/A                  | Marginal; no job creation                   |
| Passive rental portfolio      | Disqualified    | N/A                  | Not a real and operating enterprise         |
| Cannabis business             | Disqualified    | N/A                  | Federally illegal in the U.S.               |
| Non-profit/charity            | Disqualified    | N/A                  | Must be for-profit                          |

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — Business Category Compatibility Matrix

**Q54. Is a franchise the best option for an E-2 application?**

For most Canadian applicants, a franchise is widely considered the strongest
business model for an E-2 application — though it is not the only option. A
franchise provides: (1) a proven business model with documented unit economics
that reduce marginality risk; (2) a clear, documented investment figure (franchise
fee + build-out); (3) a built-in business plan via the Franchise Disclosure
Document (FDD); and (4) franchisor training and support that strengthens the
"will develop and direct" argument. The FDD's Item 19 (Financial Performance
Representation) serves as the gold standard for supporting revenue projections.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "The Four Business Acquisition Models:
Model 1 Buying a Franchise"

---

**Q55. Can I do a franchise in any industry, or are some franchises not eligible?**

Any franchise in a qualifying business category can work, provided the investor
actively manages and directs it. Disqualifying categories apply regardless of
franchise status — a cannabis franchise, for example, is still federally illegal.
Passive-style franchise investments where the investor has no management role
also do not qualify. Franchises in senior care, cleaning services, food service,
tutoring, staffing, childcare, and retail are all commonly approved. Licensing
complexity varies by franchise category and state (see Q67–Q72 for licensed
industry specifics).

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Sections 6.7 and 6.9

---

**Q56. What is the Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) and why does it matter
for E-2?**

The FDD is a federally mandated disclosure document that all franchisors must
provide to prospective franchisees in the U.S. under the FTC Franchise Rule.
For E-2 purposes, the FDD matters because:
- **Item 7** documents the estimated initial investment range — supporting
  the substantiality and proportionality analysis
- **Item 19** (Financial Performance Representation) provides documented
  revenue and profit data from existing franchise units — the strongest possible
  support for revenue projections in the business plan
- **Item 21** provides audited financial statements from the franchisor —
  evidence of the franchisor's stability
- The executed Franchise Agreement itself is included in Tab F as evidence that
  funds are irrevocably committed

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Sections 7.2 and 7.3 — Q3D-08: "Franchisor
Item 19 data is the gold standard for franchise applicants."

---

**Q57. Do I need to have the franchise agreement signed before applying?**

The franchise agreement does not need to be fully executed at the time you
submit your DS-160 online, but it must be signed — and the franchise fee paid —
by the time of your consulate interview. At the interview, officers check whether
funds are irrevocably committed. An executed franchise agreement with a paid
franchise fee is the clearest evidence of this commitment. A franchise agreement
that is merely "agreed in principle" but not yet signed does not satisfy the
at-risk requirement.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — Module 2 franchise question: "Have you
  signed the Franchise Agreement? Yes / No / In process"
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — officer checklist: "Franchise agreement
  executed (if franchise)" as evidence of real business

**Q58. What is the difference between buying a new franchise territory vs. buying
an existing franchised location?**

- **New franchise territory:** You purchase the rights to open a new unit from
  the franchisor. Investment covers franchise fee + build-out + equipment +
  working capital. No prior operating history — projections are based on Item 19
  data from comparable units.
- **Existing franchised location (resale):** You purchase an already-operating
  franchise unit from a current franchisee. The prior owner's revenue history
  is available — the strongest non-marginality evidence possible. The franchise
  agreement must be transferred to your name (franchisor consent required), and
  all franchise documents must be updated to reflect you as the new owner.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "Franchise resales must update
all franchise docs to reflect buyer's name"

---

**Q59. Can I buy an existing non-franchise business for the E-2?**

Yes. Buying an existing operating business is the second most common E-2
acquisition model for Canadians. Key advantages: the business already has
operating revenue (strongest non-marginality evidence), existing employees
(demonstrates job creation already in place), and a track record. Required
documentation includes: the purchase agreement, last 3 years of profit and loss
statements from the seller, and a transition plan demonstrating that the buyer
(you) will take over active management — not just collect passive income from
an existing operation.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "Model 2: Buying an Existing
Business"

---

**Q60. Can I build a business from scratch (greenfield) for the E-2?**

Yes, but greenfield businesses face the highest scrutiny of the four models
because there is no operating history — only projections. For a greenfield
application to succeed, the business must be demonstrably "in process" before
the interview: lease signed, EIN obtained, bank account opened, licenses applied
for, equipment purchased or ordered. A greenfield business existing only as an
LLC registration and a business plan — with no money spent — is not sufficient.
The business plan quality is the primary approval factor in a greenfield case.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "Model 3: Starting a New Business
(Greenfield): Highest scrutiny — no operating history, projections only"

---

**Q61. Can I invest in a business that is already owned and operated by my
American friend or business partner?**

Yes — Model 4 in your knowledge base covers this scenario. However, it receives
heightened scrutiny. The Canadian investor must: (1) own exactly 50% of the
restructured business; (2) have a clearly defined, active management role
distinct from the U.S. partner's role; and (3) be investing their own documented
funds — not simply lending money or making a passive capital contribution. The
U.S. partner's existing ownership must be restructured to a 50/50 split, and a
new Operating Agreement documenting this structure is required.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "Model 4: Investing in a Partner's
Existing Business: High scrutiny — officer must be convinced the Canadian is
not a passive investor"

---

**Q62. Can a consulting business qualify for the E-2?**

Only if it is structured as a genuine enterprise — not a personal services
arrangement. If the business model is "I consult with clients and they pay me
for my expertise," the investor IS the business. There is no job creation, and
this fails both the marginality test and the real enterprise test. A consulting
business can qualify when: it employs at least 2 full-time staff (developers,
analysts, support) in Year 1; it has existing client contracts beyond the
investor's personal network; revenue comes from client work performed by
employees, not just the investor's billable hours; and the investor's role is
management, not front-line service delivery.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — "IT/Technology Consulting With
Staff: The solo consultant disqualifier"

---

**Q63. Can a home-based business qualify for the E-2?**

Conditionally — with significant caveats. A home-based business faces lower
scrutiny on premises costs but must still demonstrate: a dedicated workspace
(not a kitchen table); documented client interaction; real operations with
measurable revenue; and a hiring plan. The investment threshold still applies.
Home-based businesses face a higher burden to prove they are not marginal
(sole-operator). A home-based cleaning company with a vehicle, equipment,
contracts, and employees is very different from a home-based "consultant" with
a laptop.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — Business Category Matrix:
"Home-based service business: Conditional — must show client interaction,
physical workspace"

---

**Q64. Can I run an e-commerce business on an E-2?**

Yes, conditionally. An e-commerce business can qualify if it: has real
operations (inventory, fulfillment, customer service); employs at least some
US-based staff or contracts; and generates revenue from genuine commercial
activity — not just the investor operating a laptop from home. Officers are
skeptical of pure dropshipping or passive income models. The investment must
still be substantial relative to the total business cost, and the business plan
must show how this qualifies as a non-marginal enterprise with a hiring plan.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "Online e-commerce with
operations: Conditional — must show real operations, inventory, staff"

**Q65. Is a cannabis dispensary or marijuana business allowed for E-2?**

No. Cannabis remains federally illegal in the United States under the Controlled
Substances Act. Regardless of state legalization, the E-2 visa is a federal
immigration benefit. A business that violates federal law cannot serve as the
basis for a federal immigration visa. This is an absolute disqualifier — not
a judgment call.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — "Businesses that CANNOT be used
for E-2: Cannabis/marijuana — federally illegal" and Section 6.9 — Business
Matrix: Disqualified

---

**Q66. Can a real estate business qualify for E-2?**

Only if it is an active real estate services company — not a passive investment
portfolio. The clear line:
- **Disqualified:** Buying rental properties and collecting rent (passive income,
  no real enterprise)
- **Qualifies (conditionally):** A property management company that manages
  properties for third-party owners, providing maintenance, tenant management,
  and leasing services — where the investor actively directs operations and
  employees are hired to perform the work

Revenue must come from management fees, not passive rental income. The investor
must be actively directing the operation.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — "Real Estate Management:
Conditional — Active management only, not passive rental"

**Q67. What are the licensing requirements for a senior home care business?**

Non-medical senior home care (companionship, personal care, homemaking —
NOT skilled nursing) is one of the most popular E-2 business choices for
Canadians. Licensing requirements vary significantly by state:

|---------------|-------------------------------------------|--------------|-----------------|---------------------|

|               |                                           |              |                 | administrator       |

|               |                                           |              |                 | administrator req'd |

**Q68. What is the difference between non-medical senior care and skilled
nursing/medical home care for E-2 purposes?**

- **Non-medical senior care:** Companionship, personal care (bathing, grooming,
  meal prep), homemaking. Does NOT include medication administration, wound care,
  or skilled nursing. Licensing is moderate and manageable. Recommended for
  first-time E-2 applicants.
- **Medical/skilled nursing home care:** Requires Medicare/Medicaid-certified
  Home Health Agency (HHA) license in most states. Processing time: 6 months to
  over 2 years. Some states (California, New York) have active moratoriums on
  new HHA licenses — meaning you cannot get a new license at all in those states.
  Your knowledge base explicitly warns: "This business type is generally not
  recommended for first-time E-2 applicants due to the licensing timeline
  creating a mismatch with your interview schedule."

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — Senior Home Care (Medical):
"High Complexity" alert

**Q69. What licenses are required to open a daycare or childcare centre?**

Childcare is a strong E-2 business (job creation, recurring revenue) but
requires state licensing. Key requirements:

| State      | License Required               | Issuing Body | Processing Time | Key Requirement                        |
|------------|-------------------------------|--------------|-----------------|----------------------------------------|
| Florida    | Child Care Facility License    | DCF          | 60–90 days      | Background checks, facility inspection |
| Texas      | Child-Care Center License      | HHSC         | 90–120 days     | Director must have CDA credential      |
| California | Community Care Facility License| CDSS         | 4–8 months      | Extensive facility and staff req'mts   |
| Arizona    | DHS Child Care License         | ADHS         | 60–90 days      | Background checks, CPR certification   |

**Critical for Canadian investors:** Most states require the childcare facility
director to hold US-recognized credentials (CDA — Child Development Associate —
or equivalent degree). A Canadian investor without these credentials must hire
a qualified director from Day 1. This must be reflected in the org chart and
business plan with an associated salary.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — Childcare/Daycare: Licensing

**Q70. Why is commercial cleaning a popular E-2 business for Canadians?**

Commercial cleaning (B2B janitorial services) is one of the most E-2-compatible
business types available for the following reasons: (1) low licensing complexity
— only a standard business license, general liability insurance ($1M minimum),
workers' compensation, and bonding are required in most states; (2) investment
threshold ($75K–$150K) is accessible; (3) easy job creation story — cleaners
are hired quickly as the client base grows; (4) franchise options available
(Jan-Pro, Coverall, Anago, Stratus); and (5) contract-based clients provide
predictable recurring revenue to support projections. No industry-specific state
license is required in most states.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — Commercial Cleaning/Janitorial
Services

**Q72. What is a Franchise Discovery Day, and should I attend one before
applying?**

A Franchise Discovery Day is an invitation-only event hosted by the franchisor
at their headquarters, where prospective franchisees meet the management team,
tour operations, and undergo mutual evaluation before the franchise agreement
is signed. For E-2 purposes, attending a Discovery Day is significant evidence
that the investor has conducted genuine due diligence — strengthening the
"real investor" narrative. Your knowledge base notes: "Taking at least one
concrete step to research the business before your interview strengthens the
argument that you are a genuine investor." Attending a Discovery Day is among
the strongest such steps.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3F-10: "Attended franchise
discovery day" as evidence of genuine investor intent

**Q73. Can I buy a franchise in the US without going through a broker?**

Yes. There is no legal requirement to use a franchise broker. You can research
and contact franchisors directly through their websites, the International
Franchise Association (IFA), or industry publications like Entrepreneur's
Franchise 500. However, E-2-specialized franchise brokers can help identify
brands that: (1) qualify for E-2 (investment level, active management
requirements); (2) have territory availability in your target state; and (3)
have FDD Item 19 data that supports strong revenue projections. Broker services
are typically free to the buyer — brokers are compensated by the franchisor
upon placement.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.2 — Franchise Referral Integration:
"Their service is free to you — they are compensated by the franchisor when a
placement is made."

**Q74. I have a specific business idea that is not a franchise. Can I still
apply?**

Absolutely. Franchises are common but not required. A greenfield (start-from-
scratch) business or an acquisition of an existing independent business both
qualify — provided all E-2 requirements are met: substantial at-risk investment,
real operating enterprise, non-marginal projections, and active management by
the investor. Greenfield and independent acquisition businesses require a
stronger, more independently documented business plan than a franchise, since
there is no franchisor-provided documentation like an FDD.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — Models 2 and 3

---

**Q75. What is the "marginality test" and how do my revenue projections need to
look?**

The marginality test evaluates whether the business has the capacity to generate
income beyond a minimal living for the investor and family — and to make a
meaningful economic contribution (i.e., create US jobs). A business projecting
only enough to cover the investor's household expenses fails this test. Your
knowledge base specifies that projections must show at least 3–4x household
income need AND include a clear hiring plan. For example: if your family of 4
needs $85,000/year to live, your Year 3 revenue projection should be at or above
$255,000–$340,000, and your plan should show employees being hired.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — The Marginality Test; Section
7.3 — Q3D-07 Non-marginality Assessment (auto-generated in the app at
3–4x household income threshold)

---

**Q76. Does the business need to be profitable before I renew my E-2?**

Yes — at renewal, the consulate or USCIS evaluates whether the business has
moved beyond marginality. Red flags at renewal include: revenue not exceeding
the investor's household income by 3–4x; no employees hired despite the original
hiring plan; and significantly missed projections across multiple years. The
business does not need to be wildly profitable — but it must be operating,
growing, and creating jobs consistent with the original plan. A variance
narrative explaining any gaps between projected and actual performance is
essential at renewal.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.5 — "Marginality Risk at Renewal:
The 5-Year Window"

**Q78. Can I run two different businesses simultaneously on one E-2 visa?**

The E-2 visa is tied to one specific enterprise. If you wish to invest in a
second US business, you would need to file a separate E-2 application for the
second enterprise, demonstrating all E-2 requirements independently for that
business as well. Your primary E-2 business must remain the principal basis of
your visa status. Consult an immigration attorney for dual-enterprise structures.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "entering solely to develop and
direct the enterprise" — singular enterprise basis of the visa

---

**Q79. What happens if my franchise fails or closes — do I lose my visa status
immediately?**

If the business ceases to operate, your E-2 basis is technically extinguished —
the visa is premised on an operating investment enterprise. However, you do not
lose status the instant the business closes; your visa has a validity period.
You would need to either: (1) reinvest in a new qualifying enterprise and apply
for a new E-2; or (2) transition to another visa category or depart the US. This
is why your knowledge base emphasizes renewal readiness and tracking actual vs.
projected performance — a declining business should be course-corrected before
renewal, not allowed to fail at the renewal stage.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.5 — Renewal Process; Section 6.4 —
Common Denial Reasons

**Q80. What is an FDD Item 19 and why is it so important for the business plan?**

Item 19 of the Franchise Disclosure Document is the Financial Performance
Representation — the section where the franchisor voluntarily discloses actual
financial performance data from existing franchise units (average revenues, gross
profit, and sometimes net income). Not all franchisors include an Item 19 (it is
optional but common), but when available, it is the single most powerful document
for supporting E-2 revenue projections because it is third-party, audited data
from real operating units in the same system. Your knowledge base explicitly
calls it "the gold standard for franchise applicants."

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3D-08: "Franchisor Item 19
data is the gold standard for franchise applicants."

---

**Q81. What states have no licensing requirement for non-medical senior care?**

Arizona and Georgia have no state-level licensing requirement for non-medical
senior home care businesses. This makes them attractive E-2 destinations for
investors pursuing senior care because: (1) no licensing timeline creates
interview scheduling flexibility; (2) no state license application is needed
before the interview; and (3) only standard county/city-level business permits
are required. Note: local (county/city) permits and insurance are still required
in all states.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — Senior Home Care Licensing
Table: "Arizona: No state license required for non-medical — county/city permits
only. Georgia: No state license required for non-medical — county/city permits only."

---

**Q82. If I choose California for my E-2 business, what should I know about
licensing and taxes?**

Two major warnings for California:
1. **Licensing timeline:** For senior care businesses, California's Home Care
   Organization (HCO) license takes 6–12 months to process. You must begin the
   licensing application at least 8 months before your target interview date to
   demonstrate the business is "in process."
2. **State income tax:** California has the highest state income tax in the US —
   up to 13.3%. This significantly reduces after-tax income compared to Florida
   or Texas (which have no state income tax). A cross-border CPA can model the
   after-tax impact on your projected income before you commit to a California
   location.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.10 — California alert for senior
care licensing; Section 6.11 — State Income Tax Considerations: "California:
up to 13.3% state income tax — highest in U.S."

**Q83–Q90. Franchise Specifics — Condensed Set**

**Q83. Do I need to complete franchise training before my E-2 interview?**
Franchise training should be scheduled (if not yet completed) before the
interview. Completing or scheduling training is evidence that the investor is
actively engaged with the business system. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 —
Q3C-09: "Have you completed or scheduled franchise training? Yes / Scheduled /
Not yet"

**Q84. Does the franchisor need to know I am applying for an E-2 visa?**
Yes — the franchisor typically knows because you are entering into a legal
agreement with them, and they may need to provide documentation (FDD, franchise
agreement, territory confirmation) as part of your application package. Most
established franchise systems are familiar with E-2 applicants. [KB] E2Pathway
Vol 3, Section 6.9 — Franchise documentation for Tab F

**Q85. Can I change my franchise brand after the visa is approved?**
Changing to a different franchise after approval would materially change the
basis of your E-2. At a minimum, a new E-2 application or amendment would
likely be required. Consult an immigration attorney before making any such
change. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3 — general E-2 continuity requirements

**Q86. Can I open multiple franchise locations on one E-2?**
The E-2 is tied to the specific enterprise. Opening additional locations within
the same franchise system (same LLC or corporation) and managed actively by you
is likely acceptable, but any material change to the investment enterprise should
be reviewed with an immigration attorney before execution. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.1

**Q87. What happens if the FDD changes between when I sign and when I apply?**
Franchisors update their FDDs annually. The version you sign under is the
controlling agreement. Consulate officers review the executed Franchise Agreement
and the FDD version in effect at signing. If the FDD updates materially before
your interview, consult with your attorney about whether amendments are needed.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9

**Q88. Is seller financing on a business acquisition allowed for E-2?**
Seller financing complicates the "at-risk" requirement. If the seller finances
part of the purchase price and the investor personally guarantees repayment (puts
personal assets at risk), the financed portion may count. If the seller financing
is secured only against the business assets with no personal guarantee, the
financed portion generally does not count. Document the full structure carefully
and have an attorney review it. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.9 — "Seller
financing can complicate funds at risk requirement — must be addressed"

**Q89. Can the franchise fee be refunded if the visa is denied?**
Franchise fees are typically non-refundable once paid. This is precisely why the
fee counts as at-risk capital — it is irrevocably committed. Some franchisors
may have limited refund policies for specific circumstances, but do not assume
any refund right. Review your Franchise Agreement's refund provisions before
paying. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — At-Risk Requirement

**Q90. How do I find E-2-compatible franchises in my budget?**
E-2-specialized franchise brokers can identify franchises compatible with your
investment budget, target state, professional background, and management
preference at no cost to you (they are paid by the franchisor upon placement).
Alternatively, the International Franchise Association (IFA) at franchise.org
provides a searchable directory of franchise opportunities. Filter by investment
range and industry category. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.2 — Franchise
Referral Integration

# PART 4 — PARTNERSHIP & CO-INVESTORS (Questions 91–115)

**Q91. Can two people apply for the E-2 together on the same business?**

Yes — the E-2 allows a maximum of two principal investors per business. Both
investors must independently meet all E-2 requirements: each must be a citizen
of a treaty country (they can be from different treaty countries), each must own
exactly 50% of the business, each must have their own independently documented
source of funds, and each must have their own active management role that is
distinct from their partner's role. The partnership generates two complete,
separate application packages from the shared business.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Maximum two E-2 investors per
business"

---

**Q92. Does the ownership split have to be exactly 50/50?**

Yes — for a two-partner E-2 application where both partners are applying for
E-2 status, the ownership must be exactly 50/50. A 49/51 split disqualifies
the minority (49%) partner. A 60/40 split means the 40% partner cannot qualify
for their own E-2. There is no flexibility on this point for dual E-2
applications.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Each must own exactly 50% —
no more, no less. A 49/51 split disqualifies the minority partner."

---

**Q93. What if my partner owns 60% and I own 40%? Can I still get an E-2?**

No — if you own only 40%, you cannot qualify as a principal E-2 investor. Only
the 60% partner meets the 50% minimum ownership requirement. If you wish both
partners to hold E-2 status, the business must be restructured to a 50/50 split
before filing. If only the 60% partner needs E-2 status, they may qualify
independently; the 40% partner would need a different visa category.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "6/40 split: the 60% partner
may qualify but the 40% partner cannot apply"

---

**Q94. My business partner is a US citizen. Do they need an E-2 visa?**

No. A US citizen (or US permanent resident) does not need any visa to live or
work in the US. Only the Canadian partner(s) need to apply for the E-2. However,
the 50% ownership rule still applies: the Canadian partner must own at least 50%
of the business (or have documented operational control if their ownership is
structured differently). The business must still meet all other E-2 requirements
based solely on the Canadian partner's investment.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "The U.S. Citizen Partner
Scenario: Only the Canadian partner applies for the E-2. The 50% ownership
rule still applies."

---

**Q95. Can two people from different countries both get E-2 visas for the same
business?**

Yes — two partners from different treaty countries can both hold E-2 visas for
the same business, provided each independently meets the E-2 requirements for
their respective treaty country. Each applies to their own country's US
consulate, each uses their own citizenship. The 50/50 ownership and independent
source of funds requirements still apply to each.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — Independence Requirements; the
citizenship test is per individual

---

**Q96. Does each partner need their own source of funds, or can we pool our
money?**

Each partner must independently demonstrate their own source of funds — you
cannot pool or combine investments to meet one shared threshold. The officers
will review each partner's source of funds documentation separately and
independently. Each partner's investment must be separately documented from its
origin through to the US business account.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Each partner must independently
demonstrate their own source of funds — cannot share or combine to meet one
threshold."

---

**Q97. Does each partner need their own cover letter and DS-160?**

Yes. Each partner files their own DS-160 (the online nonimmigrant visa
application form), has their own cover letter independently proving their
management role and investment, and prepares for their own interview. While some
documents are shared (the business plan, operating agreement, organizational
chart), the personal documents are separate and independent for each partner.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Two DS-160 reference sheets,
two interview prep sheets" and "Dual cover letters: each independently proves
their management role."

---

**Q98. What documents are shared between partners vs. what needs to be
separate?**

| Shared Documents                     | Separate for Each Partner          |
|--------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Business plan (dual-investor language)| DS-160 application form            |
| Operating agreement (50/50)          | Individual cover letter             |
| Organizational chart (both roles)    | Source of funds chronology          |
| Business asset register              | Individual investment narrative     |
| Lease, franchise agreement           | Individual interview preparation    |
|                                      | Individual passport and ID documents|

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "What Gets Shared in the
Partnership Application" and "Partnership-Specific Documents Required"

---

**Q99. What is an Operating Agreement and why is it critical for a partnership
E-2?**

An Operating Agreement is a legal document (for an LLC) that governs the
relationship between partners — specifying ownership percentages, voting rights,
management responsibilities, profit distributions, and decision-making authority.
For a partnership E-2 application, the Operating Agreement is essential because
it: (1) proves the 50/50 ownership split; (2) documents each partner's distinct
management role (critical to proving each partner actively develops and directs
the business); and (3) establishes the governance structure of the enterprise.
A generic template Operating Agreement is insufficient — it must reflect the
specific roles and responsibilities of each E-2 applicant.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Operating agreement: new
document explaining 50/50 split, voting, management" listed as a
partnership-specific required document

---

**Q100. Can partners have different job titles and management areas?**

Yes — in fact, they must. Each partner must have a distinct and clearly
defined management role that is genuinely separate from the other partner's
role. The cover letter and organizational chart must document these distinct
roles explicitly. For example: Partner A as CEO (responsible for financial
oversight, investor relations, and strategic direction) and Partner B as
Director of Operations (responsible for staffing, client service, and day-to-day
operations). Roles that appear identical or overlap significantly will trigger
scrutiny about whether both partners are genuinely developing and directing
the enterprise.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3C-18: "Describe your business
partner's specific title and responsibilities — how are your roles distinct from
each other?"

---

**Q101. Do both partners need to be interviewed at the same time?**

No. Each partner has their own independent interview at the US consulate. The
interviews may be scheduled on the same day or different days — this is
logistical, not a legal requirement. Both partners should prepare individually
and be able to answer all questions about their own role, investment, and the
business independently. Officers do not jointly interview E-2 partners.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Their own DS-160, their own
cover letter, their own interview prep" — individual interview implied

---

**Q102. What if one partner's visa gets approved and the other's is denied?**

This is a serious scenario. If one partner's E-2 is denied, the approved partner
may proceed — but the business and ownership structure may need to be reviewed.
If the approved partner now owns 50% of a business without an active second
manager, they may need to document that they can develop and direct the enterprise
independently (essentially reverting to a solo investor profile). An immigration
attorney should be consulted immediately if one partner's application is denied
while the other is approved.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — general partnership structure
and independence requirements; Outcome 6 attorney referral

---

**Q103. Can a married couple both be E-2 investors in the same business?**

Yes, provided they meet all partnership requirements independently — 50/50
ownership, independent sources of funds, distinct management roles. A spouse
who is not an investor can instead travel to the US as an E-2 dependent, which
allows them to apply for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD) and work
for any US employer. Whether to structure a spouse as a co-investor or dependent
depends on whether both genuinely have independent funds and distinct management
roles — not simply to share the application.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — Independence Requirements;
Section 7.1 — Q8: Partnership advisory; Section 7.1 — Q13: Spouse EAD advisory

---

**Q104. Can more than two people invest in the same E-2 business?**

Only two investors can hold E-2 status per business. A third or fourth investor
could hold equity in the business as a minority owner (via an LLC operating
agreement) but would not be eligible for their own E-2 visa based on that
investment if their ownership is below 50%. They would need a different visa
category if they wish to live and work in the US.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Maximum two E-2 investors per
business"

---

**Q105. What is the minimum investment for a partnership vs. a solo application?**

Each partner must independently meet the substantiality threshold for the
business type — there is no "shared" or "split" minimum. If the total business
cost is $200,000, each partner is expected to contribute approximately $100,000
(50%) from their own documented funds. You cannot have Partner A invest $180,000
and Partner B invest $20,000 and claim both qualify.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Each partner must independently
demonstrate their own source of funds"

---

**Q106. Can we split the investment unevenly — like one partner pays more
because they have more money?**

The investment split should correspond to the ownership split, which must be
50/50. Significant deviation from a 50/50 investment allocation while maintaining
50/50 ownership creates inconsistency that officers will question. The investment
allocation narrative must explain how the total investment is divided between
the two partners, and it should closely reflect the 50/50 ownership structure.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Investment allocation narrative
explains how total investment is split"

---

**Q107. Can one partner be a silent investor and the other run the business?**

No. A silent or passive investor does not qualify for an E-2 visa. Both partners
must actively develop and direct the enterprise in distinct management capacities.
An investor who simply contributes capital and receives distributions — without
any genuine management role — fails the "develop and direct" requirement
regardless of ownership percentage.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Each must independently
demonstrate their own active management role in the business — their own
operational responsibilities not just passive equity"

---

**Q108–Q115. Partnership Edge Cases — Condensed**

**Q108. My partner is a Canadian permanent resident but not a citizen. Can they
still be an E-2 co-investor?**
If your partner holds citizenship in another E-2 treaty country (e.g., UK,
Australia, France), they may apply using that citizenship. Canadian permanent
residency alone does not qualify. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q1
eligibility routing

**Q109. Can we each have different accountants and lawyers for our separate
packages?**
Yes — each partner can use their own advisors. What matters is that each package
is independently complete and internally consistent. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.3 — Independence Requirements

**Q110. What happens to the business if my partner leaves or sells their share?**
A material change in ownership (one partner exiting) may affect E-2 status for
both partners and should be reviewed with an immigration attorney immediately.
Ownership changes should also be reflected in an updated Operating Agreement.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.5 — Renewal and continuity requirements

**Q111. Can my partner be on an existing visa (like L-1 or TN) while I apply
for E-2?**
Each applicant's visa category is independent. Your partner's existing visa
status does not affect your E-2 application directly, but they should not be
simultaneously pursuing two conflicting immigration paths without legal guidance.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q10 routing; attorney referral conditions

**Q112. Does the operating agreement need to be notarized?**
While notarization is not a strict federal requirement for an E-2 application,
a professionally drafted, executed, and (ideally) notarized operating agreement
carries more evidentiary weight than an informal or unsigned document. Use a
US-licensed attorney to draft the operating agreement. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.3 — Operating Agreement as partnership-specific required document

**Q113. Can we apply with a verbal partnership agreement and formalize later?**
No. The operating agreement must be executed (signed) before the interview. An
informal or verbal partnership arrangement does not prove the 50/50 ownership
structure required by E-2. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3

**Q114. Does my partner and I need to appear at the consulate on the same day?**
Not required — each partner's interview is scheduled independently. However,
coordinating interview dates close together is practical so you can begin
operating the business as partners at approximately the same time. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — Independent applications

**Q115. If we both get approved, do we share one I-94 entry record or have
separate ones?**
Each traveler receives their own independent I-94 record tied to their own
entry into the US. Your immigration records are always individual — never shared
between two people regardless of business partnership. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.3 — Independent application process
