## The Age-Out Issue

E-2 dependent status (E-2D) is available to the **unmarried children of
the principal E-2 investor who are under 21 years of age** at the time
of the visa application.

When an E-2 dependent child turns 21, they are no longer eligible for
E-2D dependent status. This is called **aging out.**

> "For immigration purposes, a 'child' is an unmarried person under 21
> years of age."
> — INA Section 101(b)(1)

**When aging out happens:**
- The child's current E-2D status does not automatically terminate on
  their 21st birthday — it expires with the current visa period
- However, the child cannot renew or extend E-2D status after turning 21
- The child must take action before or immediately after their 21st birthday
  to maintain lawful status in the United States

## E-2 Specific Age-Out Dynamics

### The CSPA Does NOT Apply to E-2 Dependants

The **Child Status Protection Act (CSPA)** was designed to protect children
in family-sponsored and employment-based **immigrant visa** proceedings —
not nonimmigrant visa categories.

E-2 is a nonimmigrant visa. **CSPA does not apply to E-2 dependent children.**
There is no formula to freeze the child's age or extend E-2D eligibility
beyond the child's 21st birthday.

This is a critical distinction from immigrant visa proceedings where CSPA
may provide protection.

### The Practical Timeline

A family that arrives in the U.S. when their child is 17 has approximately
4 years before the age-out issue becomes critical. A family that arrives
when the child is 19 has approximately 2 years.

**Planning must begin at least 12–18 months before the child turns 21.**

## Options When a Child Approaches 21

### Option 1 — F-1 Student Visa

If the child is enrolled or planning to enroll in a U.S. university or
college, changing status to **F-1 (Student)** is the most common solution.

**How it works:**
- Child obtains admission to a U.S. Student and Exchange Visitor Program
  (SEVP)-certified school
- Child files Form I-539 (Change of Nonimmigrant Status) from E-2D to F-1,
  OR departs the U.S. and obtains a new F-1 visa at a consulate
- F-1 status is valid for the duration of the student's full-time enrollment

**Important considerations:**
- **Nonimmigrant intent:** F-1 requires demonstration of nonimmigrant intent.
  If the family is pursuing or considering an immigrant pathway (EB-5, EB-1C, NIW),
  the child having a pending immigrant petition can complicate an F-1 application.
  Consult an immigration attorney if an immigrant petition is pending.
- **Financial support:** The child must demonstrate financial support for
  the duration of studies
- **I-20 and SEVIS:** The school issues a Form I-20; the child pays the
  SEVIS fee and obtains the F-1 visa or files I-539 for change of status
- F-1 spouses of F-1 holders (F-2) cannot work; but a child aging out of
  E-2D is typically transitioning to their own independent status

**Timing:** File I-539 or apply for F-1 visa well before the child's 21st
birthday. Do not wait until the birthday.

### Option 2 — H-1B (Specialty Occupation)

If the child has completed a U.S. degree and has a qualifying job offer
from a U.S. employer, **H-1B** may be an option.

**Significant obstacle:** H-1B is subject to an annual numerical cap
(65,000 regular cap + 20,000 U.S. advanced degree exemption) with a lottery
selection process. Demand far exceeds supply; selection is not guaranteed.

**H-1B cap registration:** Takes place each March for employment starting
October 1. A child who turns 21 in, say, July may face a timing gap
between E-2D expiration and H-1B commencement — careful status management
is required.

**Cap-exempt employers:** Universities, research institutions, and certain
nonprofits are cap-exempt — H-1B positions at these employers can be filed
at any time without lottery risk.

### Option 3 — O-1 (Extraordinary Ability)

If the child has demonstrated extraordinary ability in science, arts,
education, business, or athletics, O-1 may be available.

The standard is high — "sustained national or international acclaim."
This option is relevant for child athletes, musicians, researchers, or
others with demonstrable exceptional achievement.

### Option 4 — L-1 (Intracompany Transferee)

If the child has worked for the family's Canadian business (or an affiliated
foreign entity) for at least 1 year in the past 3 years in a managerial,
executive, or specialized knowledge capacity, L-1 may be available.

**This requires the child to have genuine, documented employment in a
qualifying role** — not merely being a family member associated with the business.

### Option 5 — TN Status (Canada-Specific)

If the child is a Canadian citizen and qualifies in one of the TN
professional categories (engineer, accountant, scientist, etc. — there are
63 listed professions), they can apply for TN status at the U.S. port of
entry with a job offer from a U.S. employer.

**Advantages:** No annual cap; no lottery; processed at the border; relatively
low cost. **Disadvantages:** Limited to listed occupations; does not lead
directly to a green card.

### Option 6 — Marriage to a U.S. Citizen or LPR

If the child marries a U.S. citizen, they can potentially adjust status to
lawful permanent resident through the marriage petition process (IR-1
immediate relative — no wait if citizen spouse). This is a personal life
decision, not a strategic planning tool — but it is a legal pathway that
exists.

### Option 7 — Return to Canada

If no qualifying immigration option is available or practical, the child
departs the United States before their E-2D status expires and returns to
Canada. They can later apply to return to the U.S. under any appropriate
visa category for which they qualify.

## Action Plan — Age-Out Timeline

| Months Before 21st Birthday | Action |
|---|---|
| 18–24 months | Consult immigration attorney; assess all options; determine most likely path |
| 12–18 months | If F-1: research schools; apply for admission; obtain I-20 |
| 12 months | File I-539 (change of status to F-1) if not departing for consular F-1 |
| 6–12 months | If H-1B: ensure employer registration in March lottery |
| 3 months | Confirm new status is in place or travel plan is arranged |
| Before 21st birthday | Child must be in valid nonimmigrant status |
| After 21st birthday | E-2D status cannot be renewed — do not let E-2D expire without a plan |

## Maintaining Continuous Lawful Status

The most critical risk in an age-out situation is an **unlawful presence**
gap — a period between the expiration of E-2D status and the commencement
of a new nonimmigrant status.

Unlawful presence consequences:
- **180 days of unlawful presence:** 3-year bar on admission
- **365+ days of unlawful presence:** 10-year bar on admission
- These bars can permanently disqualify the child from U.S. immigration
  options for years

**The only acceptable outcome:** The child transitions to a new valid status
**before** the E-2D period expires. There must be no gap.

## If the Family Is Pursuing a Green Card Path

If the principal E-2 investor is in the process of pursuing an immigrant
visa (EB-5, EB-1C, or NIW), the age-out issue becomes more complex because:

1. CSPA applies to **immigrant** visa proceedings — the child's age in the
   green card process may be protected by CSPA even if E-2D ages out first
2. The child maintaining a nonimmigrant status during the green card process
   is essential
3. A pending immigrant petition affects the child's ability to obtain certain
   nonimmigrant visas (particularly F-1)

In these situations, engage an immigration attorney specializing in both
family immigration and investor visas. Do not try to manage this cross-over
without professional guidance.
