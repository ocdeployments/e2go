# E-2 Visa — Non-Marginality Standard
**kb/e2-visa-marginality.md**
*Sources: 9 FAM 402.9-6(E) — State Department Foreign Affairs Manual (fam.state.gov, updated 02-17-2026),
INA 101(a)(15)(E)(ii), Matter of Kung (BIA 1978)*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## The Legal Standard

A business is **marginal** — and therefore disqualifying for E-2 purposes —
if it:

> "does not have the present or future capacity to generate enough income to
> provide more than a minimal living for the treaty investor and their family."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(E)

There is an exception:

> "An enterprise that does not have the capacity to generate such income but
> that has a present or future capacity to make a significant economic
> contribution is not a marginal enterprise."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(E)

And a critical timing rule for future capacity:

> "The projected future capacity should generally be realizable within five
> years from the date the applicant commences normal business activity of
> the enterprise."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(E)

---

## What the Standard Actually Means

The non-marginality requirement has **two separate pathways** to satisfy it:

### Pathway 1 — Income Capacity

The business must have the present or projected capacity to generate income
that exceeds a "minimal living" for the investor and their family.

**What is "more than a minimal living"?**

The FAM does not define a specific dollar threshold. However, in practice:
- The income must clearly exceed what would be needed for bare subsistence
- It should be sufficient to support the family at a level consistent with
  the investor's lifestyle and the local cost of living
- Most practitioners interpret this as the business needing to generate
  income in excess of approximately 3× the federal poverty line — often
  called the **"3× rule"** in informal practice

**Federal Poverty Guidelines (2025 — as reference):**
- 1 person: $15,060/year
- Family of 4: $31,200/year

A rough "3× rule" minimum would therefore be approximately:
- $45,000–$95,000/year net business income depending on family size

> Note: The "3× rule" is a practitioner heuristic, not a codified standard.
> What matters is the officer's assessment that the business clearly exceeds
> minimal living — a business projecting $150,000+ in year 2 is on very
> strong ground.

### Pathway 2 — Significant Economic Contribution

Even if the business cannot demonstrate income above minimal living,
it is **not marginal** if it has the present or future capacity to make
a **significant economic contribution.**

What constitutes significant economic contribution:
- **Job creation** — hiring U.S. workers is the strongest signal
- **Economic multiplier effect** — the business stimulates other local
  economic activity
- **Services to underserved communities**
- **Technology or innovation with broad economic impact**

**Practical application:** Most applicants rely on Pathway 1 (income capacity)
because it is easier to quantify and document. Pathway 2 (economic contribution)
is typically used when the business is in a capital-intensive or employment-
generating sector where early-stage profits are modest.

## What Officers Look For

### In the Business Plan

Officers evaluate the business plan's financial projections with specific
non-marginality questions in mind:

1. **Year 1–5 revenue projections** — are they realistic and based on
   local market data?
2. **Net income to owner** — what does the owner actually take home after
   operating expenses?
3. **Job creation plan** — how many U.S. workers will be hired and when?
4. **Salary detail** — are the projected wages for employees consistent with
   the Bureau of Labor Statistics data for the role and region?

> ⚠️ Officers are trained to identify business plans with inflated projections
> that appear designed to pass the marginality test rather than reflect genuine
> business reality. A plan projecting $500,000 revenue in year 1 for a
> single-location service business in a mid-size city will raise red flags
> if it is not supported by local market data.

### Signs of a Marginal Business

These characteristics increase marginal business risk:
- Business that generates income sufficient for only one person (the investor),
  with no capacity to hire
- "One-person consulting shop" with no employees, minimal overhead, and all
  revenue dependent on the investor's personal labor
- Business in a sector with very low barriers to entry and thin margins
  (e.g., Uber driving, personal services at minimum wage)
- Very low total investment inconsistent with generating significant income
- No concrete hiring plan and vague statements about "potential future employees"
- Revenue projections derived entirely from the investor's own personal labor

### Signs of a Non-Marginal Business

These characteristics support non-marginality:
- **Concrete hiring plan with specific job titles, timelines, and wages**
- Revenue projections based on local market data, comparable businesses,
  or franchise disclosure document FDD data
- A franchise with a known track record of generating $300,000+ in annual
  revenue at comparable units
- Multiple revenue streams that do not all depend solely on the investor's
  personal effort
- Capital-intensive business model where significant revenue is generated
  from assets (equipment, inventory) rather than personal labor alone

## The Hiring Plan — Most Important Non-Marginality Evidence

The single most powerful evidence of non-marginality is a **specific, credible
hiring plan** that shows the business will create U.S. jobs within a defined
timeframe.

A strong hiring plan includes:
- Specific job titles (not generic "employees")
- Number of positions and employment type (full-time vs. part-time)
- Wage rates (consistent with BLS Occupational Employment Statistics for
  the specific occupation and state)
- Timeline (month 1, month 3, month 6, year 2, etc.)
- Brief rationale for why each position is needed (customer volume, hours,
  operational function)

**The 5-year window matters:** If your business cannot demonstrate non-marginality
at opening but has a credible path to generating significant economic contribution
within 5 years, you can still meet the standard. Document this timeline explicitly
in the business plan.

## At Renewal — Marginality Is Re-Evaluated

At your first and subsequent renewals, the officer will compare:
- Your actual business results (revenue, net income, employees hired,
  wages paid, taxes paid) against
- Your original projections from the initial application

A business that has failed to meet its hiring projections or has generated
income below projections will face marginality scrutiny at renewal.

This is why Template 7 (Actual vs. Projected) in the E2Pathway renewal module
is critical — it shows the trajectory of the business and explains any variance.

## Marginality FAQ

### "Can I use income from my Canadian investments to pass the marginality test?"

This is a contested area of law. The 2009 revision to the FAM eliminated
language that previously allowed "other factors" (including outside income)
to overcome marginality. Most practitioners and the IRS Sourcebook on
immigration law treat the current standard as focusing on the enterprise
itself — not the investor's personal wealth.

The conservative and safest position: your business plan should demonstrate
that the enterprise itself — independent of any outside income — will generate
more than a minimal living.

### "My business plan shows low profit in Year 1 but strong growth by Year 3. Is that enough?"

Yes, if the projections are credible and achievable within 5 years. Officers
accept that start-up businesses take time to become profitable. The key is:
- Year 1–2 losses or modest income must be explainable (build-out period,
  ramp-up time, initial marketing spend)
- Year 3–5 projections must be realistic and supported by data
- Hiring must be on a clear trajectory even if early hiring is limited

### "What if I own multiple businesses?"

Each E-2 application is evaluated on the qualifying enterprise — the specific
business the applicant is investing in. Income from other businesses is generally
not counted toward the marginality analysis for the qualifying enterprise.
