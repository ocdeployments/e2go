# PART 10 — GREEN CARD PATHWAYS BEYOND E-2 (Questions 276–315)

---

**Q276. Does the E-2 visa itself lead directly to a green card?**

No. The E-2 is a **nonimmigrant visa** and does **not directly lead to permanent residency** on its own. It can be renewed indefinitely as long as the business continues to operate, but the visa itself does not convert into a green card after a certain number of years. That said, many E-2 investors use the E-2 as a platform and later transition to a green card strategy through a separate immigrant category. The key point is that the green card process is a **different process**, with different eligibility rules, filings, and timelines.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — "The E-2 visa is temporary and does not directly lead to a green card. However, practical pathways exist."

---

**Q277. What are the main green card pathways mentioned for E-2 holders?**

The three main pathways referenced in your knowledge base are:

1. **EB-1C Multinational Manager or Executive**
2. **EB-5 Immigrant Investor**
3. **EB-2 National Interest Waiver (NIW)**

These are very different pathways. EB-1C is typically the best fit for E-2 holders who establish a real Canadian-U.S. multinational structure. EB-5 is the pure investor route but requires much more capital. NIW is not based on the E-2 business itself — it is for people with exceptional ability, advanced degrees, or work of substantial national importance.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — Green Card Pathway Beyond E-2

---

**Q278. Which green card route is usually considered the best fit for an E-2 investor?**

Based on your source material, **EB-1C** is presented as the strongest practical option for many E-2 holders who can build a genuine cross-border company. It has no quota backlog for Canadians in the way many immigrant categories do, and the required investment is often lower than EB-5 because the focus is on a qualifying multinational management structure, not a minimum investment threshold. The tradeoff is that EB-1C requires real corporate structure and real management activity in both Canada and the U.S. — it is not available to a simple single-country small business.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-1C: "Best option for E-2 holders who establish genuine Canadian operations"

---

**Q279. What is EB-1C in plain English?**

EB-1C is the green card category for a **multinational manager or executive**. In plain terms, it allows someone who has worked for a company **outside the U.S.** for at least one year in a managerial or executive role to be transferred to a related U.S. company in a managerial or executive role and then pursue permanent residence. For an E-2 investor, this means the E-2 business can sometimes become the U.S. arm of a broader Canada-U.S. corporate structure. The immigrant petition is not based just on investment — it is based on qualifying multinational company relationships and management duties.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-1C Multinational Manager or Executive

---

**Q280. What are the main requirements for EB-1C from your knowledge base?**

Your source identifies these core EB-1C requirements:
- The applicant must have **1 year of employment by the company outside the U.S.**
- The person must then transfer to the **U.S. business as a manager or executive**
- The E-2 business must be part of **multinational operations in Canada and the U.S.**
- The corporate relationship must be genuine and documented

This means EB-1C generally works best where the investor has an active Canadian company and a related U.S. company, rather than a one-country E-2 business with no foreign parent, affiliate, or branch.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-1C requirements summary

---

**Q281. How long does EB-1C take, according to your materials?**

Your knowledge base gives an EB-1C processing time of approximately **6–18 months** for Canadians. That is materially faster than EB-5 in your materials, which is shown at roughly **2–5 years**. Actual government processing times vary, but within your content framework EB-1C is clearly positioned as the faster and more strategic permanent residence route for many established E-2 families.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-1C processing time; EB-5 processing time

---

**Q282. Can I pursue EB-1C while keeping my E-2 active?**

Yes. In practical planning, the E-2 can continue to function as the status that lets you operate in the U.S. while a separate green card strategy is pursued. Your source material frames the E-2 as a platform from which later permanent residence pathways can be launched. What matters is that the E-2 application itself must still be presented as a nonimmigrant visa application, especially at the interview and renewal stages. This is why your interview prep materials repeatedly warn not to express immigrant intent in the E-2 process itself.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — practical pathways beyond E-2
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 / 3H-06 — green card intention is not disqualifying, but must not be expressed during the E-2 interview

---

**Q283. What is EB-5 in plain English?**

EB-5 is the **immigrant investor** green card category. Unlike E-2, it is specifically designed to lead to permanent residence. In broad terms, the investor contributes a qualifying amount of capital to a qualifying U.S. business and must create at least **10 full-time U.S. jobs**. It is the most direct investor route to a green card in your knowledge base, but also the most capital-intensive and often the slowest.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-5 Immigrant Investor

---

**Q284. How much investment does EB-5 require, based on your source?**

Your knowledge base states the current EB-5 threshold as:
- **$800,000 USD** in a Targeted Employment Area (TEA)
- **$1.05 million USD** in other areas

It also states that the investment must create **10 full-time U.S. jobs** and that the process can run concurrently with E-2 renewal. This makes EB-5 an option for families with substantial capital who want a direct permanent residence route while maintaining E-2 operations in the meantime.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-5 investment thresholds and job creation rule

---

**Q285. How long does EB-5 take, according to your source?**

Your knowledge base gives an EB-5 processing timeline of approximately **2–5 years**. That timing matters a lot for families with older dependent children, because the real planning issue becomes whether the green card process can move fast enough before a child ages out at 21. In your materials, this is described as a race between green card timing and the child’s 21st birthday.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-5 timing; Child age-out problem

---

**Q286. What is an EB-2 National Interest Waiver (NIW)?**

The NIW is an immigrant category for individuals with **advanced degrees, exceptional ability, or work of national importance**, where the applicant can often self-petition without needing a traditional employer sponsor. Your knowledge base describes it as particularly relevant for some E-2 applicants who have strong academic, scientific, professional, or industry credentials. Unlike EB-1C or EB-5, NIW is not fundamentally tied to owning a multinational company or investing a large set minimum amount. It is a profile-driven green card route.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — National Interest Waiver EB-2 NIW

---

**Q287. Which E-2 investors might be realistic NIW candidates?**

Based on your source, NIW may be relevant for E-2 applicants who have:
- Advanced degrees
- Strong professional credentials
- Exceptional ability in their field
- Work that can be framed as serving U.S. national interest

In practice, that may include physicians, researchers, engineers, certain founders in high-value sectors, or professionals with a strong record of impact. It is generally **not** the default route for an ordinary small-business investor unless their personal credentials independently support NIW eligibility.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — NIW: "Relevant for some E-2 applicants with academic or professional backgrounds"

---

**Q288. Can I apply for a green card and still renew my E-2?**

Your knowledge base says EB-5 can be run **concurrently with E-2 renewal**, and the broader structure of the materials treats green card planning as something that can happen alongside continued E-2 operations. The caution is not that the strategies are mutually exclusive; the caution is that the **E-2 filing itself** still must be handled as a nonimmigrant process, with careful interview preparation and maintenance of Canadian ties. In other words, green card strategy may exist in the background, but the E-2 package should not be framed as an immigrant filing.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-5 can run concurrently with E-2 renewal
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — nonimmigrant intent coaching

---

**Q289. What is the age-out issue when planning a green card for my child?**

The age-out issue is that E-2 dependent children lose derivative status on their **21st birthday**, while most green card pathways take significant time. Your knowledge base specifically warns that if a child is **17** when the parents apply, planning should begin **immediately**, because the family is racing the child’s age. This is one of the biggest reasons families consider EB-1C or EB-5 early rather than waiting until the child is already 19 or 20.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — Child age-out problem

---

**Q290. When should a family start green card planning if a child is older?**

Your materials indicate that age-out alerts fire at **age 20.0** and **20.5**, but the strategic planning warning appears much earlier: if a child is **17** at the time the parent applies, the family should begin a separate pathway **now**. For practical planning, that means families with children in the 16–18 range should treat permanent residence analysis as an early-stage issue, not a Year 4 renewal issue.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — begin planning now if child is 17
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — age 20 and 20.5 alerts

---

**Q291. Which green card route is most direct for pure investors?**

According to your source, **EB-5** is the most direct investor route to permanent residency because it is explicitly structured as an immigrant investor program. EB-1C can also work very well, but it depends on multinational management structure rather than just investment. NIW depends on the applicant’s credentials and national-interest case, not on investor status by itself.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-5: "Expensive but the most direct investor route to permanent residency"

---

**Q292. Which green card route is usually fastest in your source material?**

Within your materials, **EB-1C** is presented as the fastest major route discussed, at approximately **6–18 months**, compared with **2–5 years** for EB-5. NIW timing is not specified in the excerpts you provided, so the content does not support a hard comparative claim there. Based strictly on your sources, EB-1C is the speed leader among the listed pathways.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-1C and EB-5 timing

---

**Q293. If I only have one U.S. business and no Canadian company, is EB-1C realistic?**

Usually no, or at least not yet. Your source emphasizes that EB-1C requires **multinational operations in Canada and the U.S.** If the investor only has a U.S. E-2 business with no genuine Canadian parent, branch, subsidiary, or affiliate structure, then the core multinational requirement is missing. In that scenario, EB-5 or NIW may be more realistic than EB-1C unless the corporate structure evolves.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-1C multinational operations requirement

---

**Q294. Can a solo small business owner use NIW instead of EB-5 or EB-1C?**

Possibly, but only if the person’s own qualifications support it. Your source frames NIW as relevant for applicants with strong academic or professional backgrounds, not as a default fallback for every small-business owner. So a solo business owner with no advanced credentials, no exceptional ability evidence, and no clear national-interest case should not assume NIW is available just because EB-1C or EB-5 is difficult.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — NIW relevance for certain academically or professionally strong applicants

---

**Q295. What should families understand before starting a green card strategy from E-2?**

Families should understand three things clearly:
- The **E-2 itself does not become a green card** automatically
- The best long-term route depends on the family’s **capital, business structure, and timeline**, especially children’s ages
- The E-2 interview and renewal still require careful handling of **nonimmigrant intent**, even if long-term permanent residence is part of the family’s private strategy

That is why the best planning usually happens early, before a child is close to 21 and before business structure choices become difficult to change.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Sections 6.6 and 7.3

# PART 11 — CANADIAN CROSS-BORDER TAX & FINANCIAL PLANNING (Questions 316–355)

---

**Q316. What is "departure tax" for Canadians moving to the U.S. on E-2?**

When a Canadian becomes a **non-resident for Canadian tax purposes**, the CRA may treat many of their capital assets as if they were sold at **fair market value** on the date of departure. This is often called a **deemed disposition** or departure tax. In plain English, it can trigger Canadian tax on unrealized gains even though you did not actually sell the asset. This is one of the most important pre-move tax issues for an E-2 family.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — "CRA deems all capital property disposed of at fair market value — deemed disposition tax is owed on unrealized gains"

---

**Q317. Does every asset trigger departure tax?**

Your source says the CRA deems **all capital property** disposed of at fair market value when a Canadian becomes a non-resident, but in real tax practice there are exceptions and special categories. Because your source material gives a high-level summary rather than a detailed asset-by-asset rule set, the safe guidance is this: do **not** assume you can assess departure tax yourself from a checklist. Any meaningful portfolio — shares, private company interests, investment assets, rental property interests, trusts, or large unrealized gains — should be reviewed with a cross-border CPA before departure.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — departure tax overview

---

**Q318. What happens to my RRSP if I move to the U.S. on E-2?**

According to your knowledge base, the RRSP can generally be **kept deferred**, which is often the preferred approach. However, withdrawals as a non-resident are generally subject to **25% non-resident withholding tax**, with possible reduction to **15%** under the Canada-U.S. tax treaty in some situations. Your materials specifically advise that a cross-border tax specialist should review RRSP withdrawals before you take them.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — RRSP can be kept deferred; 25% non-resident withholding on withdrawals
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — RRSP withdrawal advisory in Q5

---

**Q319. Should I cash out my RRSP to fund the E-2 investment?**

Your materials do **not** suggest that cashing out an RRSP is automatically a good idea. They allow RRSP funds as a possible source of investment capital, but they also warn about withholding tax and recommend cross-border tax review first. In many cases, preserving tax-deferred retirement funds may be better than triggering tax leakage to raise E-2 capital. The right answer depends on the investor’s broader financial picture.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — RRSP withdrawals as a source of funds, with treaty withholding warning
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — RRSP deferral and withholding summary

---

**Q320. What happens to my TFSA when I become a non-resident of Canada?**

Your knowledge base says that once you become a non-resident, the TFSA **ceases to earn contribution room**, and new contributions made as a non-resident can trigger a **1% per month penalty**. This makes the TFSA a common trap for Canadians who assume they can keep contributing after moving. The account may still exist, but contribution behavior has to change once non-residency begins.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — TFSA non-resident rule summary

---

**Q321. What happens to CPP and OAS if I move to the U.S.?**

Your source says CPP and OAS can continue to be received by non-residents, but **25% withholding** may apply unless reduced by treaty. The knowledge base states the Canada-U.S. treaty reduces the withholding rate to **15%** in this context. Because pension taxation can vary by type of payment and treaty treatment, this is another area where cross-border tax advice matters.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — CPP/OAS non-resident withholding summary

---

**Q322. What happens to OHIP or provincial health coverage when I leave Canada?**

Your materials say provincial health insurance such as **OHIP** is typically cancelled upon departure and that it is **critical to maintain it until U.S. health coverage is active**. This matters both practically and strategically: practically because you do not want a healthcare gap, and strategically because surrendering provincial coverage **before** visa approval can weaken your nonimmigrant intent case by making it look like you have already abandoned Canada.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — OHIP cancellation timing
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Sections 6.4 and 6.13 — surrendering OHIP before visa approval is an immigrant-intent risk signal

---

**Q323. Should I close my Canadian bank accounts before the E-2 is approved?**

No. Your materials are explicit on this point: **do not close Canadian accounts before visa approval**. Those accounts help demonstrate continuing ties to Canada for E-2 nonimmigrant intent, and the departure checklist specifically warns against closing them too early. After approval and relocation, you can update the institutions to your non-resident status and new address.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — pre-departure checklist; Sections 6.4 and 7.3 — Canadian financial ties as nonimmigrant intent evidence

---

**Q324. What is Form NR73 and do I have to file it?**

Your knowledge base includes **Form NR73 — Determination of Residency Status** as part of the pre-departure checklist. The materials frame it as something to file when evaluating your Canadian non-residency status. Whether filing NR73 is always strategically advisable in real life can depend on the facts and professional advice, but within your content set it appears as a checklist item for Canadians leaving under E-2 planning.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — pre-departure checklist item 1

---

**Q325. What are the main pre-departure tax checklist items in your materials?**

The pre-departure checklist in your source lists:
1. File **Form NR73** with CRA
2. Arrange **departure tax filing** for the year of departure
3. Update all financial institutions with **non-resident status**
4. Do **not** close Canadian accounts before visa approval
5. Do **not** surrender provincial health coverage before U.S. health coverage starts

This is a strong example of why immigration planning and tax planning have to be coordinated, not done separately.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.8 — pre-departure checklist implications

**Q326. When should I notify Canadian financial institutions that I am a non-resident?**

Your compliance calendar places this in the **post-approval** phase, not before visa issuance. Specifically, it says to update Canadian bank accounts to non-resident status and notify financial institutions of the address change at about **1 month after visa approval**. That sequencing aligns with the nonimmigrant-intent concern: maintain ties until approval, then complete the financial housekeeping after the move becomes real.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — post-approval calendar items at Visa +1 month

**Q327. When should I handle departure tax and CRA non-residency steps?**

Your compliance calendar places CRA non-residency registration and departure tax work very soon after approval — around **2 weeks after visa issuance**. Specifically, it lists:
- Register with CRA as non-resident
- File departure tax return or engage a CPA

This makes clear that the tax transition should happen promptly once the move is actually underway, not casually months later.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Visa +2 weeks calendar items

**Q328. What U.S. tax filing appears in the compliance calendar?**

**Q329. What is FBAR and when does it matter for E-2 holders?**

Your materials treat FBAR as an ongoing compliance obligation where applicable. The compliance calendar says that if U.S. foreign accounts exceed **$10,000 USD**, FBAR filing becomes required and is flagged beginning in Year 1 and continuing afterward. The broader lesson is that banking in both countries creates compliance duties that many E-2 families do not expect at the start.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — FBAR trigger in the compliance calendar

A cross-border CPA helps with source-of-funds issues, RRSP withdrawal analysis, departure tax, first-year U.S. filing, and account-status changes.
