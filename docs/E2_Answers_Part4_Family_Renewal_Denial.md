# PART 7 — FAMILY & DEPENDENTS (Questions 176–215)

---

**Q176. Can my spouse come with me to the US on the E-2 visa?**

Yes. The spouse of an E-2 principal investor is eligible for **E-2 dependent
status** (sometimes called the E-2S visa). The spouse must be legally married
to the principal investor (or in a recognized common-law partnership, depending
on USCIS/consulate recognition). The spouse does not need to be a Canadian
citizen — they may hold any nationality. They apply as a dependent on the
investor's E-2 application and are included in Tab L of the application binder.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Module 1 Q12–Q13: "Will your
spouse be joining you in the US on E-2 status? Advisory about spouse EAD."

---

**Q177. Does my spouse need to be Canadian to qualify for E-2 dependent status?**

No. The nationality of the spouse does not matter for E-2 dependent status.
The E-2 treaty nationality requirement applies only to the **principal investor**
— the person who invested in and will manage the business. A spouse of any
nationality may receive E-2 dependent status as long as the principal investor
qualifies. For example, a Canadian investor married to a French, Indian, or
American national can still include their spouse in the E-2 application.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q13 routing logic; Section 6.1
— "Treaty nationality requirement applies to the principal investor."

---

**Q178. Can my spouse work in the US on E-2 dependent status?**

Yes — and this is one of the most significant practical benefits of the E-2
dependent visa. E-2 spouses are eligible to apply for an **Employment
Authorization Document (EAD)**, also called a work permit (Form I-765). Once
the EAD is granted, the spouse may work for **any US employer** in any role —
they are not restricted to working for the E-2 business. This is a major
advantage over many other visa categories where spousal work authorization
is restricted or unavailable.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q13: "Advisory about spouse EAD
  (Employment Authorization Document)"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q179. When and how does my spouse apply for the EAD?**

The EAD (Form I-765) is filed with USCIS — it is a separate application from
the E-2 visa itself. The spouse can file Form I-765 after arriving in the US
on E-2 dependent status. The EAD must be renewed when the underlying E-2 status
is renewed. Processing times for the EAD vary — check the USCIS website for
current processing times. The spouse may not begin working until the EAD is
physically in hand (or an electronic approval notice confirms it, depending on
current USCIS policy).

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — "spouseapplying: EAD module
activation"; Compliance Calendar post-approval items

---

**Q180. Can my children come with me to the US on the E-2 visa?**

Yes. Unmarried children **under the age of 21** are eligible for E-2 dependent
status alongside the principal investor. They are included in Tab L of the
application binder. Each child's date of birth must be documented (birth
certificate). Children on E-2 dependent status may attend US public schools
but cannot work unless they independently obtain their own work authorization.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q14: "Do you have dependent
children who will be joining you in the US? Collect child ages."

---

**Q181. Do my children need to be Canadian citizens to come on the E-2?**

No. Like the spouse, the nationality of dependent children does not matter.
Children may hold any nationality and still accompany the Canadian E-2 principal
investor as dependents, provided they are unmarried and under 21.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — Treaty nationality requirement
applies to the principal investor only.

---

**Q182. What is the "age-out" problem for E-2 dependent children?**

The age-out problem is one of the most time-sensitive immigration risks for
E-2 families with older children. **E-2 dependent status ends on a child's
21st birthday.** On that date, the child is no longer eligible to remain in
the US as an E-2 dependent and must either: obtain independent immigration
status, depart the US, or be included in a green card process that was started
early enough for them to qualify before their 21st birthday. This is not
automatic — it requires proactive planning. If a child is 17 when the parent
applies, the window to act is dangerously short.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — "The Child Age-Out Problem:
E-2 dependent status ends on a child's 21st birthday. Critical planning trigger
— if child is 17 when parent applies, begin planning a separate pathway NOW."

---

**Q183. At what age should I start planning for my child's age-out?**

Planning should begin no later than when the child turns **20 years old** — that
is, 12 months before the age-out deadline. Your knowledge base states the
compliance calendar fires an alert the moment the child turns 20, and a second
attorney referral alert at age 20.5 (6 months before age-out). Realistically,
if a green card process (EB-5 or EB-1C) is involved, planning should begin
even earlier — at age 16 or 17 — because green card processing timelines can
run 2–5 years.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Compliance Calendar:
"Dependent age 20: Child age-out planning alert (Critical); Dependent age 20.5:
Attorney referral — child pathway options (Critical)"

---

**Q184. What are my options when my E-2 dependent child turns 21?**

Options to explore at age 20 (12 months before age-out):
1. **F-1 Student Visa** — if the child is enrolled or will enroll in a US
   college or university; requires acceptance at a SEVP-certified school
2. **Their own E-2 visa** — if the child invests in their own qualifying US
   business (same requirements as the principal investor)
3. **O-1 or other work visa** — if the child has qualifying talent or employment
   in a US company willing to sponsor them
4. **Green card petition** — if an EB-5 or EB-1C process was started early
   enough; the child may qualify as a derivative beneficiary before turning 21
   (subject to the Child Status Protection Act)

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — "Options at age 21: F-1 student
visa, their own work visa, their own E-2, or green card if EB-5/EB-1C process
started early enough"; Section 7.7 — Child age-out alert notification text

**Q186. Does my spouse need to attend the consulate interview?**

For consular processing, the E-2 principal investor must attend the interview.
Whether dependents (spouse and children) are also required to attend varies —
the US Embassy Canada guidance indicates that applicants under 14 do not need
to appear in person. For spouses and older children, check current US Embassy
Canada requirements as this can depend on individual circumstances and current
consulate procedures.

**Sources:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "Do I have to come to a consulate
  to apply in person? Applicants under 14 years of age do not need to appear
  in person." https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Tab L generation covers dependent
  documentation

---

**Q187. What happens to my spouse's E-2 status if we divorce?**

E-2 dependent status is derivative — it depends entirely on the principal
investor's status and the marital relationship. In the event of divorce, the
spouse's E-2 dependent status is at risk. The spouse would need to transition
to an independent immigration status (their own E-2, an employer-sponsored
visa, student visa, etc.) or depart the US. This is a situation that requires
immediate consultation with an immigration attorney.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — RQ-12: "Have there been any
changes to your family composition? ... spouse status change" — flagged for
update and attorney review at renewal

---

**Q188. Can a common-law partner qualify as an E-2 dependent spouse?**

This depends on USCIS and consulate recognition of common-law partnerships.
US immigration law generally recognizes legal marriages. Common-law partnerships
may or may not be recognized depending on the jurisdiction where the relationship
was established and current USCIS policy at the time of application. This is an
area where an immigration attorney's guidance is important — do not assume a
common-law relationship will automatically qualify.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q12: "Are you married or in a
common-law partnership?" — flagged for attorney referral in complex cases

---

**Q189. What is Tab L and what does it contain?**

Tab L is the dependent family information section of the application binder.
It contains documentation for each accompanying dependent (spouse and children):
- Full legal name, date of birth, relationship to the principal investor
- DS-160 confirmation page for each dependent
- Passport copy (bio page) for each dependent
- Birth certificate for children
- Marriage certificate for spouse (if applicable)

The 50-page per-tab limit applies to Tab L as well.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.2 — "Tab L: Dependent Family
Information for spouse/children — Tab L generator"

---

**Q190. What if I have a newborn or adopt a child after my E-2 is approved?**

A new child born or adopted after the E-2 is approved would need to be added
as a dependent through a separate USCIS or consular process. This is addressed
at renewal — the renewal module (RQ-12) specifically asks: "Have there been
any changes to your family composition? New child, child aged out, spouse status
change." Any new dependent after the initial application requires updating the
E-2 record. Consult an immigration attorney for the specific process for adding
a dependent mid-status.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Renewal Module RQ-12

---

**Q191. Can my spouse start a separate business while on E-2 dependent status
with an EAD?**

Yes. Once the spouse holds a valid EAD, they are authorized to work in the US
without restriction — including starting their own business, working as a
contractor, or taking employment with any US employer. The EAD provides
unrestricted work authorization. However, the EAD is tied to the underlying
E-2 status — if the principal investor's E-2 expires or is revoked, the EAD
is also at risk.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q13 EAD advisory: spouse may
work "for any US employer in any role — not restricted to the E-2 business."

**Q192–Q215. Family & Dependents — Condensed Set**

**Q192. Can my dependent child work in the US on E-2 dependent status?**
No — E-2 dependent children under 21 are not authorized to work. They may
attend school. To work legally, they would need their own independent work
authorization (such as an F-1 OPT, their own E-2, or an employer-sponsored
visa). [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q14; Section 6.6 — age-out
options at 21

**Q193. Does my spouse's EAD need to be renewed at the same time as my E-2?**
Yes — the EAD is tied to the underlying E-2 status. When you renew your E-2,
your spouse should also renew their EAD simultaneously. A lapse in E-2 status
creates a lapse in EAD validity. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 —
Post-approval compliance calendar; Section 7.8 — Renewal Module RQ-12

**Q194. Can we have children born in the US — will they be US citizens?**
Children born on US soil are US citizens under the 14th Amendment (birthright
citizenship), regardless of the parents' immigration status. This is a factual
legal note — it does not affect the E-2 application itself but has long-term
implications for family planning. [KB] Section 6.6 — Green card pathways context

**Q195. What happens to my family's status if my E-2 business closes?**
If the E-2 business ceases to operate, the principal investor loses the basis
for their E-2 status and must either start a new qualifying business, transition
to another visa, or depart the US. All dependents' status is derivative — they
lose status when the principal investor loses status. Orderly wind-down and
transition planning should involve an immigration attorney immediately. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "renewable indefinitely as long as the business
continues operating"

**Q196. If my spouse finds a full-time job in the US, does that affect my E-2
status?**
No — the spouse's employment under an EAD does not affect the principal
investor's E-2 status. The spouse's EAD is work authorization only; it does
not create a new visa category. The principal investor must continue to operate
the qualifying E-2 business regardless of the spouse's employment situation.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q13 EAD advisory

**Q197. Can my parent or sibling come to the US on my E-2?**
No. E-2 dependent status is limited to the investor's **spouse and unmarried
children under 21**. Parents, siblings, and other extended family members are
not eligible for E-2 dependent status. They would need their own independent
immigration pathway. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q12–Q14 dependent
scope

**Q198. How is family composition documented at the interview?**
All dependents are documented in Tab L. The officer reviews Tab L briefly —
it is not typically the focus of detailed interview questioning. However,
inconsistencies between what is declared on the DS-160 and what is in Tab L
can trigger follow-up questions. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 —
"Tabs B, C, E, L: supporting personal documents — reviewed briefly"

# PART 8 — RENEWAL & EXTENSION (Questions 216–255)

---

**Q216. How long is the E-2 visa valid and when do I need to renew it?**

The E-2 visa stamp issued at the Toronto consulate is valid for **5 years**.
Your compliance calendar triggers renewal preparation at **Year 4** — giving
you a full year to prepare before the 5-year expiry. The renewal preparation
module should be started no later than **6 months before the visa expiry date**
(Year 4.5 on the compliance calendar). Waiting until the final month before
expiry is a serious risk — if the renewal is delayed, you may fall out of status.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.5 — "5-Year Consular Renewal
Toronto"; Section 7.7 — Post-approval compliance calendar: "Year 4: Renewal
preparation begin renewal module (Critical)"

---

**Q217. What are the two paths for renewing my E-2 visa?**

| Factor               | Path A: Toronto Consulate         | Path B: USCIS Form I-129           |
|----------------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Form                 | DS-160                            | I-129 with E Classification Supp.  |
| Interview            | Required at consulate             | Usually NOT required               |
| Validity granted     | 5-year visa stamp                 | 2-year I-94 increment only         |
| Travel during process| Normal                            | Cannot leave US                    |
| Processing time      | 60–90 days + interview wait       | 3–6 months                         |
| Fee (2026)           | $315 MRV fee                      | $730 I-129 filing fee              |
| Package format       | 11-tab binder                     | USCIS cover sheet + supporting docs|

**Recommendation:** Most Canadian E-2 holders renew at Toronto for the full
5-year stamp. USCIS extension is used when the applicant cannot travel or
wants to avoid an interview, but the 2-year limit means another renewal is
needed sooner.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Path A vs. Path B comparison
table; Section 6.5

**Q220. What is the marginality risk at renewal?**

At renewal, the consulate/USCIS evaluates whether the business has moved **beyond
marginality** — i.e., that it is generating substantially more than a living wage
and creating jobs. Red flags that increase denial risk at renewal:
- Revenue not exceeding the investor's household income need by 3–4x
- No employees hired despite the original hiring plan
- Business projections significantly missed across multiple years
- Solo operator with no staff whatsoever — the most dangerous renewal profile

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.5 — "Marginality Risk at Renewal:
The 5-Year Window"

---

**Q221. What if my business revenue missed projections in Year 1 or 2?**

Missing projections in the early years is common and is not automatically a
denial trigger — **if you explain it credibly.** Officers understand that new
businesses face ramp-up delays, market challenges, and unexpected costs. The
variance narrative (Template 6) must: (1) acknowledge the shortfall honestly;
(2) explain the specific reason (not vague excuses); (3) show the trajectory
is improving; and (4) demonstrate the business remained viable and the investor
remained active. A business that missed Year 1 but exceeded Years 3–5 is in
a strong renewal position.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — RQ-05 Tooltip: "Officers
understand that businesses face challenges — what matters is that the business
remained viable and you remained actively managing it." Template 6 variance
narrative example

---

**Q222. What is the highest-risk renewal scenario?**

The highest-risk renewal scenario is an investor who:
- Has hired **no employees** (solo operator throughout)
- Has **missed revenue projections** in multiple years
- Shows revenue only slightly above their household income need
- Cannot demonstrate active management (changed role, rarely on-site)

Your knowledge base states: "A renewal application with no employees hired is
at significant risk of being denied on marginality grounds. The officer will
compare your current staffing to your original projections. If you have not
hired staff, your renewal cover letter must address this directly with a
compelling explanation and a credible forward-looking hiring plan."

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — RQ-10 High Risk Advisory

---

**Q223. Do I need to redo the source of funds chronology at renewal?**

No — the source of funds chronology (Template 1) does not need to be repeated
at renewal if the investment is the same investment from the original application.
However, if you have made **additional investments** since the original
application, those new funds should be documented in the renewal cover letter
(demonstrating ongoing commitment to the business).

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — "NOT required: New source of
funds chronology if same investment." RQ-06: "What additional investment have
you made in the business since your original application?"

---

**Q224. What renewal questions does the consulate/USCIS focus on?**

The renewal interview (or USCIS adjudication) focuses on the investor's
**actual business performance** — not the plan. Key renewal questions:

1. Tell me about the growth of your business over the past 5 years.
2. How many people do you currently employ?
3. Your Year 1 revenue was below projections — can you explain that? (if applicable)
4. What changes have you made to the business since your original visa was granted?
5. What are your plans for the business over the next 5 years?
6. Are you still actively managing the business?

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Renewal Interview Simulator
Updates: Universal Renewal Questions RUQ-01 through RUQ-05

**Q225. What is the renewal cover letter structure?**

The renewal cover letter follows the same 8-paragraph structure as the original
but with emphasis shifted from *plan* to *achievement*:

1. Introduction — applicant name, visa history, renewal request
2. Business description update — what the business is today
3. Financial performance — summary of actual vs. projected results
4. Job creation — employees hired, payroll generated, economic impact
5. Investor's ongoing active role — what they do day-to-day now
6. Future plans — next 5 years of growth
7. Canadian ties maintained — non-immigrant intent reaffirmation
8. Conclusion — respectful renewal request

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — "Renewal Cover Letter Structure"

**Q226. Can I change the ownership structure of my business before renewal?**

Any change in ownership structure must be approached very carefully and with
legal guidance. Your knowledge base states: "Changes to ownership structure must
be carefully documented. If your ownership percentage has changed, please consult
an immigration attorney before filing your renewal." An ownership change — even
a small shift in percentage — can affect the E-2 eligibility basis and must
be disclosed and documented in the renewal package.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Renewal Module RQ-08

**Q227. What if I cannot travel to Toronto for renewal — can I renew from
inside the US?**

Yes — this is Path B (USCIS Form I-129 extension). It provides a 2-year
I-94 increment without requiring a Toronto interview. However:
- You **cannot travel internationally** while the I-129 is pending
- If your E-2 status has already **lapsed**, you cannot use I-129 — you must
  return to Toronto (consult an attorney immediately in that case)
- The I-129 filing fee is $730 (2026)
- You only get a 2-year extension vs. a 5-year stamp at Toronto

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Path B USCIS Status Extension;
RQ-B01: "If your E-2 status has lapsed, you cannot extend from inside the US."

---

**Q228. What happens if my E-2 status lapses before I file for renewal?**

If your E-2 status lapses — meaning you remain in the US after your I-94
expiry without filing for an extension or renewal — you are **out of status**.
This is a serious violation. You cannot file an I-129 extension if out of
status. You must depart the US and apply at the Toronto consulate for a new
E-2 visa. An out-of-status period may affect your ability to re-enter and
must be disclosed on future immigration applications. Consult an immigration
attorney immediately.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — RQ-B01 URGENT advisory:
"If your E-2 status has lapsed, you cannot extend from inside the US. You must
depart and apply at the Toronto consulate. Consult an immigration attorney
immediately."

---

**Q229. Is there a limit on how many times I can renew the E-2?**

There is **no statutory limit** on E-2 renewals. The E-2 can be renewed
indefinitely as long as the qualifying business continues to operate. This is
one of the most appealing features of the E-2 — there is no clock ticking
toward expiry of the program itself. The visa remains available as long as
the business remains operational, the investor continues to develop and direct
it, and the treaty between Canada and the US remains in effect.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "renewable indefinitely as
long as the business continues operating"

**Q230–Q255. Renewal — Condensed Set**

**Q230. When should I start preparing for my renewal?**
Start the renewal preparation module no later than **6 months before expiry**
(the Year 4.5 compliance calendar item). Begin gathering actual performance
data, payroll records, and business bank statements from Year 4 onward so the
Actual vs. Projected table can be populated accurately. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 7.7 — "Year 4.5: Toronto renewal interview scheduling window (Critical)"

**Q231. What is the Renewal Readiness Score?**
At Year 3–4, the app generates a Renewal Readiness Score assessing the business's
performance against the original projections and the non-marginality standard.
It flags risks (e.g., no employees hired, revenue shortfalls) and gives the
investor time to address them before renewal. This is analogous to the original
Application Confidence Score but focused on actual business performance. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.5 — "Renewal Readiness Score at Year 3–4"

**Q232. Do I need to update my business plan for renewal?**
Yes. The renewal package requires an updated business plan with real P&L data.
For the Toronto consulate renewal, this is a full updated plan. For the USCIS
I-129 path, a condensed 5-page version is sufficient. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 7.8 — Path A and Path B document requirements

**Q233. What documents do I need to prove job creation at renewal?**
The primary documents for job creation evidence at renewal are: payroll records
or payroll summary (W-2s, quarterly payroll reports, or payroll processor
reports); current employee count and roles; any W-2 or 1099 filings with the
IRS; and a narrative describing each employee's role and hire date. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — "NEW document: Evidence of job creation —
payroll records summary"

**Q234. Can I add a new business line or expand locations before renewal?**
Yes — and doing so is actually a positive indicator for renewal. Business growth,
new service lines, additional locations, and new client contracts all strengthen
the non-marginality case. Document these in the renewal cover letter's business
accomplishments section (generated from RQ-04). [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section
7.8 — RQ-04 prompts: "New services or products added, New locations, New clients,
Major contracts won"

**Q235. What if my role in the business has changed since the original application?**
A changed role is not disqualifying — but it must be documented and explained.
The renewal module asks: "Are you still actively managing the business in the
same role described in your original application? — Changed role: Describe your
current role (update cover letter)." What is disqualifying is a role that has
become purely passive. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.8 — RQ-09

**Q236. Can I switch from a Toronto renewal to a USCIS extension at any point?**
Generally yes — as long as you are in valid E-2 status and meet the criteria
for the chosen path. However, if you have international travel booked, you
should not file an I-129 (travel may abandon the petition). Use the routing quiz
in the renewal module: "Am I currently inside the US? Do I have international
travel planned? Can I travel to Toronto in the next 6 months?" [KB] E2Pathway
Vol 3, Section 7.8 — Renewal Module Entry routing quiz

**Q237. What is the FBAR filing requirement for E-2 holders?**
If you have US bank accounts with an aggregate balance exceeding $10,000 USD
at any point during the calendar year, you are required to file an FBAR
(FinCEN Form 114 — Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts) annually.
This applies to non-US persons who have US financial accounts. The annual
FBAR deadline is April 15 (with an automatic extension to October 15). The
compliance calendar flags this at Year 1 (first filing) and every subsequent
year. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Compliance Calendar: "Visa 11 months:
File FBAR if US foreign accounts >$10,000 (Critical)"

# PART 9 — 221(g), DENIAL & REAPPLICATION (Questions 256–295)

---

**Q256. What is a 221(g) administrative suspension — is it a denial?**

A 221(g) is **not a denial** — it is an administrative hold requesting additional
information or documentation, or placing the case in administrative processing
(security/background checks). It means the officer cannot approve the application
on the day of the interview with the information available, but has not refused
it. You will receive a written notice (white, yellow, or blue slip) explaining
what is needed or that the case is pending. The most common type is a request
for additional documents — respond promptly and completely.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "221(g) Administrative
Suspension: Not a Denial." Most common supplemental requests listed.

---

**Q257. What are the most common documents requested in a 221(g)?**

The most common 221(g) supplemental document requests for E-2 cases:
1. **Additional source of funds documentation** — bank statements, tax returns,
   or explanations for a specific deposit or account transfer
2. **Updated business plan** — if projections appeared unrealistic or unsupported
3. **Local market research** for the specific city/county (not just general
   industry data)
4. **Lease/purchase agreement update** — if the original was not yet executed
5. **Evidence business is currently operating** — photos, licenses, bank statements

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "Most common supplemental
requests" under 221(g) Administrative Suspension

---

**Q258. How long does 221(g) administrative processing take?**

The timeline for 221(g) resolution varies significantly:
- **Document request:** If the officer is simply asking for additional documents,
  you can respond immediately (as soon as you gather the requested items). Once
  submitted, the case is typically adjudicated within days to a few weeks.
- **Administrative processing (security/background):** This type requires no
  documents — the applicant simply waits. Processing can take anywhere from a
  few weeks to several months with no predictable timeline. There is no standard
  deadline for the consulate to conclude administrative processing.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "Security or background checks:
administrative processing — no documents requested; the applicant simply waits."
Section 6.4 — 221(g) description

---

**Q259. Can I enter the US while my 221(g) is pending?**

No — you cannot receive the E-2 visa stamp until the 221(g) is resolved and
the visa is approved. You should not travel to the US expecting to enter on
E-2 status while administrative processing is pending. If you are already in
the US on another valid status (e.g., Change of Status was filed), the rules
are different — consult an attorney.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — 221(g) context; Section 7.7 —
post-interview compliance: "If 221(g): review emergency guide"

---

**Q260. What is an outright denial (refusal)?**

An outright refusal (as opposed to 221(g)) means the officer has determined
that the E-2 visa cannot be approved based on the information presented. The
refusal is typically stated at the interview window (or in writing afterward).
The most common bases for refusal are listed under denial reasons (see Q163
in Part 3). A refusal is not necessarily permanent — you may reapply — but
it is on your immigration record and must be disclosed on future applications.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "Outright Refusals: Most
Commonly Triggered By"

---

**Q261. What is a 214(b) denial and why is it the most dangerous?**

A 214(b) denial means the consular officer determined that the applicant **failed
to demonstrate non-immigrant intent** — i.e., the officer believes the applicant
intends to remain permanently in the US rather than returning to Canada. This
is described in your knowledge base as "the most dangerous denial" because:
- It is entirely subjective — based on the officer's judgment
- It can override an otherwise strong application (good investment, real business,
  solid source of funds)
- It is difficult to overcome on reapplication because the intent question
  is largely answered by circumstantial evidence (what ties remain in Canada)
- It requires a substantially different fact pattern or presentation to succeed
  on reapplication

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "214(b) Immigrant Intent:
Most Dangerous Denial"

---

**Q262. What triggers a 214(b) immigrant intent denial?**

The most common triggers:
- **Sold Canadian home before visa approval** — most common trigger; officer
  sees no housing tie to Canada
- **Surrendered provincial health card (OHIP)** before visa approval
- **Closed all Canadian bank accounts** before the interview
- **Close family ties in US with no Canadian family remaining** — all family
  members moving to US simultaneously
- **Answers during the interview suggesting permanent intent** — "I plan to
  stay as long as possible," or "I don't plan to go back to Canada"
- **Pending US immigration applications** — an adjustment of status or pending
  green card petition on file simultaneously

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "What triggers 214(b)
Immigrant Intent"; Section 6.13 — outright refusal triggers

---

**Q263. Can I reapply after a denial?**

Yes — there is no legal bar to reapplying after a denial. However:
- The denial is on your immigration record and **must be disclosed** on all
  future visa applications (including on the DS-160)
- You must disclose the prior denial **honestly** — misrepresentation is a
  separate and more serious immigration violation
- For a 214(b) denial, reapplication requires a **materially different fact
  pattern** — not just a better cover letter. If you were denied for selling
  your Canadian home, you cannot simply reapply with the same facts and hope
  for a different result
- An immigration attorney should be involved in any reapplication strategy
  following a denial

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.3 — Q3A-11 (prior visa denial
disclosure); Section 7.6 — RT-VD-01: "I see you had a prior visa denial —
can you explain the circumstances and what has changed since then?"

---

**Q264. What must I disclose about a prior denial on my new application?**

On the DS-160, you must accurately disclose any prior visa refusals. The
question asks: "Have you ever been refused a US visa, or been refused admission
to the United States, or withdrawn your application for admission at the port
of entry?" Answer truthfully. The interview simulator flags a specific risk
question for applicants with prior denials: RT-VD-01: "I see you had a prior
visa denial — can you explain the circumstances and what has changed since
then?" Prepare a clear, honest, forward-looking answer.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.6 — Risk-Triggered Question
RT-VD-01; Section 7.3 — Q3A-11 prior visa denial

---

**Q265. After a 214(b) denial, what changes would make a reapplication stronger?**

To overcome a prior 214(b) denial, the reapplication must show a materially
improved non-immigrant intent profile:
- **Retain or re-establish a Canadian property** — rent out the old home instead
  of selling, or rent a Canadian property after the denial
- **Maintain open Canadian bank accounts** with ongoing activity
- **Keep other Canadian financial ties** — pension accounts (RRSP), professional
  memberships, provincial health card (OHIP) maintained
- **Canadian family ties** — if family members remain in Canada, document this
  prominently
- **Do not file concurrent US immigration applications** — withdraw any pending
  green card petitions before reapplying
- **New interview coaching** — specifically addressing the intent question with
  a more credible and consistent narrative

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — What triggers 214(b); Section
7.3 — Q3H-01 through Q3H-08 non-immigrant intent assessment

---

**Q266. What is the "emergency guide" for 221(g) cases?**

The 221(g) emergency guide is an in-app module activated when the user reports
a 221(g) outcome in the outcome tracker (within 3 days post-interview per the
compliance calendar). It provides:
- A categorized checklist of the most common 221(g) requests and what documents
  satisfy each one
- Guidance on how to organize and submit the supplemental response
- A cover letter template for the supplemental submission
- Timeline guidance for how quickly to respond

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "App response: 221(g) emergency
guide module with document response checklists for each common request type";
Section 7.7 — "Interview +3 days: If 221(g) — review emergency guide"

---

**Q267. If my business fails after I get the E-2, what should I do?**

If the E-2 business fails or must close:
1. **Notify an immigration attorney immediately** — do not simply stop operating
   and assume nothing will happen
2. You have a **grace period** (typically 60 days) after loss of E-2 status to
   depart or transition to another visa
3. You may apply for a different qualifying business without leaving the US
   (consult an attorney about the process)
4. Your dependents' status is also at risk — they lose E-2 dependent status
   when you lose E-2 status
5. Do NOT simply stop operating while remaining in the US — this creates an
   overstay situation

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "renewable indefinitely as long
as the business continues operating" (implication: status ends if it doesn't);
Section 6.4 — business deemed marginal context

**Q268–Q295. 221(g), Denial & Reapplication — Condensed Set**

**Q268. If I get a 221(g) for "missing local market research," what do I submit?**
Submit a localized market analysis specific to your city and county —
not generic national industry data. Include: local population and demographics
from the US Census Bureau, local business listings and competitor analysis
(from Google Maps, Yelp, local business directories), and any local industry
association data. The more specific and local, the better. [KB] E2Pathway
Vol 3, Section 6.4 — 221(g) most common request: "Local market research for
the specific city"

**Q269. Can I appeal a visa denial?**
US nonimmigrant visa denials at the consulate level generally cannot be
formally appealed in the way a court decision can be appealed. Your option
is to **reapply** with a materially stronger application addressing the reason
for the denial. You may also request an advisory opinion from the Department
of State's Visa Office if the denial involved a legal interpretation question —
but this is rare and typically involves attorney representation. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — Common denial reasons context

**Q270. Is there a waiting period before I can reapply after a denial?**
There is no mandatory waiting period after a nonimmigrant visa denial.
However, reapplying immediately with the same facts is unlikely to succeed.
The reapplication should only be filed when there is a materially different and
stronger fact pattern — typically after addressing the specific reason for the
denial. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4; Section 7.6 — RT-VD-01

**Q271. What if the 221(g) request asks for something I don't have?**
If the consulate requests a document you cannot produce (e.g., a signed lease
when the business is not yet at that stage), contact an immigration attorney
before responding. In some cases, a sworn affidavit explaining why the document
is unavailable and providing alternative evidence of the same fact can satisfy
the request. Do not simply leave the request unanswered. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.4 — 221(g) emergency guide context

**Q272. Does a 221(g) affect my ability to reapply in the future?**
A 221(g) that is ultimately resolved favorably (visa approved after supplemental
submission) does not count as a "refusal" for future disclosure purposes —
it was an administrative hold, not a denial. A 221(g) that results in a
subsequent denial after supplemental review would need to be disclosed as a
refusal. Confirm current Department of State guidance with an attorney. [KB]
E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — 221(g) vs. denial distinction

**Q273. How do I respond to a 221(g) for "business plan projections appear
unrealistic"?**
Respond with: (1) a revised business plan section addressing the specific
projections with additional supporting data; (2) local market research
validating the revenue assumptions; (3) industry benchmarking data (average
revenue per unit for comparable businesses); (4) a statement from a CPA or
business consultant validating the projections; and (5) if you are a franchise,
Item 19 of the Franchise Disclosure Document showing franchisee performance data.
[KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "Weak or Generic Business Plan" denial
trigger context

**Q274. What is "administrative processing" in a 221(g)?**
Administrative processing means the consulate has placed your case in a
security or background check queue. No documents are requested — you simply
wait. This is triggered by factors outside the content of your E-2 application
(e.g., security databases, prior travel to certain countries, name matches).
There is no action the applicant can take to accelerate it. The applicant
cannot enter the US on E-2 status during this period. [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3,
Section 6.13 — "Security or background checks: administrative processing —
no documents requested; the applicant simply waits"

**Q275. Can I file a Change of Status simultaneously with my consular application?**
Filing a Change of Status (I-129) while also pursuing consular processing is
generally discouraged — a dual-intent filing can be used as evidence of
immigrant intent. Choose one path. If you are already in the US on a valid
status and prefer to avoid traveling, use COS only. If you are in Canada
preparing for the consulate, use consular processing only. [KB] E2Pathway
Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q11 routing: "Consular processing vs. Change of Status —
pick one path"
