# PART 12 — EDGE CASES & SPECIAL SITUATIONS (Questions 356–400)

---

**Q356. I am currently in the U.S. on a visitor visa (B-1/B-2). Can I apply for E-2 from here?**

Yes. If you are in the U.S. on a valid B-1/B-2, TN, or L-1 status, you have the option to file a **Change of Status (COS)** to E-2 with USCIS using Form I-129. You do not need to travel to Toronto. However, the COS path gives only a **2-year I-94 increment** rather than the 5-year stamp you would get from the Toronto consulate, it takes **3–6 months** to process, and you **cannot travel internationally** while the petition is pending without potentially abandoning it.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — Consular vs. COS comparison table
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q10 routing: B-1/B-2 coseligible true

**Q360. I sold my Canadian home already before applying. What now?**

This is a significant immigrant-intent risk. Your materials are explicit: selling the primary Canadian residence before visa approval is one of the most common triggers for a 214(b) immigrant-intent denial. The app displays a high-risk advisory and then focuses on mitigation: documenting every other Canadian tie that remains — bank accounts, family in Canada, professional memberships, pension accounts, business interests — and building the cover letter's Canadian ties paragraph around those factors instead of housing.

**Sources:**

**Q361. I already sold my home AND have no family remaining in Canada. Is my application hopeless?**

Not automatically, but the risk is elevated and the cover letter and interview preparation need to work much harder. Your source says that when there are no family ties remaining, the cover letter guidance shifts to emphasizing **every other Canadian connection** — bank accounts, professional licences and designations, RRSP and pension accounts, business interests, community ties, or any other documented link to Canada. The officer is looking for a coherent reason to believe the applicant would return. The application is harder, but not impossible, with the right documentation.

**Q362. I am a Canadian permanent resident but not a citizen. Can I use E-2?**

No. The E-2 visa requires citizenship in a treaty country. Canadian permanent residency alone does not qualify an applicant for the E-2 through Canada, even if the person has lived in Canada for many years. If the person holds citizenship in another treaty country (for example, the UK, Germany, or Australia), they may qualify through that citizenship.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q1: Canadian PR but not citizen = DISQUALIFY SOFT with treaty country check offer

---

**Q363. I have dual citizenship — Canadian and another country. Does that change anything?**

For E-2 purposes, you apply based on your treaty-country citizenship. If you are a Canadian citizen, you apply as a Canadian, and the Toronto consulate is your natural interview location. Dual citizenship with another treaty country typically does not provide any additional benefit for the E-2 process, but it also does not hurt. Your knowledge base does not treat dual citizenship as a complication.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — citizenship-based routing; Tab E proof of nationality/treaty eligibility

**Q364. I am located outside Canada and the U.S. — can I still apply?**

Your source says that applicants outside Canada and the U.S. are typically still routed to Toronto for the interview if they hold a Canadian passport. The app shows an advisory about consular options and routes the user accordingly. In practice, a Canadian citizen anywhere in the world can generally apply at any U.S. consulate, but Toronto is the most practical and most familiar with Canadian E-2 applications.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q9: location "other" routes to advisory; typically routes to Toronto if Canadian passport

**Q365. What if I am applying from within the U.S. but want a 5-year stamp, not a 2-year increment?**

Then the consular path is better for you. Your source notes that applicants who want a full 5-year visa stamp should use the Toronto consulate rather than COS. COS gives only a 2-year I-94 extension. If the person is inside the U.S. but not restricted from traveling, and they want the longer validity, they can still choose to travel to Toronto for the consular interview rather than file I-129.

**Q368. My business is home-based. Can I use it for E-2?**

Yes, but with important caveats. Your source lists home-based businesses as qualifying but requiring **extra documentation**: you must show a dedicated physical workspace, evidence of genuine client interaction, and real business operations. A business that exists only on paper with no physical workspace or demonstrable client activity will struggle to satisfy the officer's "real and operating" test.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — home-based businesses: can work but require extra documentation

---

**Q369. My business is entirely online. Does it qualify?**

Yes, but again with extra scrutiny. Online businesses must demonstrate **real revenue and operations** — they are not automatically disqualifying. However, a business with no physical presence, no employees, and no clear evidence of operating activity beyond a website will have difficulty with both the "real business" test and the marginality test (an online solo business where the only product is the investor's personal labor is disqualifying by your source).

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — online businesses: can work but require demonstrated real revenue and operations

---

**Q370. I am buying a franchise resale from an existing franchisee. Does that work?**

Yes. Franchise resales are explicitly addressed in your source as qualifying but requiring one important step: **all franchise documents must be updated to reflect the new buyer's name**. A resale where the original franchisee's name still appears throughout the franchise agreement and disclosure documents will create problems in the application.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — franchise resales: must update all franchise docs to reflect buyer's name

---

**Q371. I want to invest in a real estate rental portfolio. Can I use that for E-2?**

No. Passive real estate investment — rental portfolios, REITs, or any model where the investor's role is primarily collection of rent rather than active management — is explicitly disqualifying in your source. The E-2 requires active management, not passive investment.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — "Businesses that CANNOT be used for E-2: Passive real estate investment (rental portfolios, REITs)"

---

**Q372. What about a real estate management company — not just owning properties, but actively managing them?**

Your source treats a **real estate management company** (active management of other people's properties) as a qualifying business category that can work — but it distinguishes it clearly from passive real estate holding. The distinction is that the investor must be actively and genuinely managing operations, not simply collecting income from properties they own.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — real estate management companies: "active management not passive holding" listed under businesses that CAN work

**Q373. Can I use a cannabis business for E-2?**

No. Cannabis/marijuana remains **federally illegal** in the United States. Your source lists it as explicitly disqualifying, regardless of state-level legalization. Because E-2 requires a lawful business activity under federal law, cannabis is categorically excluded.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — "Cannabis/marijuana — federally illegal" listed as a disqualifying business type

---

**Q374. I am a consultant — my only product is my own expertise and labor. Can I use E-2?**

This is one of the clearest disqualifying patterns in your source: a **single-person consulting business where the only product is the investor's own labor** is listed as disqualifying. The issue is that such a business fails both the marginality test (no jobs created, no economic contribution beyond one person's income) and the "develop and direct" requirement (there is no enterprise to direct — the investor *is* the product). Adding employees, real infrastructure, or contractual delivery capacity can change the analysis, but a pure solo-labor consulting arrangement does not qualify.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — "Single-person consulting where the only product is the investor's labor" is listed as a disqualifying business type

---

**Q375. My business will not be fully operational by my interview date. Is that okay?**

This is an important nuance. The E-2 requires investment "in the process of investing," not a fully running business. But your source is clear that the officer needs to see **a business in motion** — money spent, contracts signed, licenses applied for, lease executed, equipment purchased or ordered. A business that is only an LLC registration and a business plan does not pass the "real and operating" test. Your pre-interview checklist (T-6 months through T-1 week) is structured around ensuring enough operational progress exists before the interview.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — officer's Question 2: "A business that exists only as an LLC registration and a business plan is not a real operating enterprise"
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — pre-interview checklist timeline
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — "Businesses that will not be real and operating by interview date" listed as disqualifying

**Q379. Can I get an E-2 visa and then let someone else run the business?**

No. The E-2 requires the investor to be entering the U.S. **solely to develop and direct** the enterprise. Passive investment where someone else fully manages the business does not satisfy the "develop and direct" standard. The officer's Question 6 (Investor's Role in the cover letter) and the interview questions both probe whether the investor is a genuine active manager. Hiring operational staff is fine and expected — but the investor must hold a genuine management role.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — eligibility requirement: "investor must be entering solely to develop and direct the enterprise"
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — cover letter paragraph 6: The Investors Role; officer observation criteria

**Q380. What if the business I am buying has been operating under a different owner for many years?**

Buying an existing operating business (an acquisition) is a valid E-2 route. Your source identifies this as one of the four main business acquisition routes (franchise, acquisition, greenfield, partner investment). The practical advantage is that an existing business often has documented revenue history, which helps with the non-marginality analysis. The practical challenge is that all documents — franchise agreements if applicable, licenses, insurance, leases — must be updated to reflect the new owner. The business also must still represent an investment at risk, not just payment of a purchase price from which the investor can theoretically exit.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q7: acquisition route
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — franchise resales: update all docs to buyer's name

**Q381. I don't qualify for E-2. What are my main alternatives?**

Your knowledge base addresses this in the soft-disqualify routing. The main alternatives mentioned are:
- **LMIA-based work permit** (Labour Market Impact Assessment) — a Canadian-issued work authorization path
- **EB-5 immigrant investor** — requires $800,000–$1.05M and creates 10 U.S. jobs, leads to green card
- **Other investor visas** — general reference to alternatives
- **Immigration attorney consultation** — to assess the full picture before any path is chosen

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Outcome 5: "provide alternative options: LMIA work permit, EB-5, other investor visas, offer attorney referral"

---

**Q382. What is a LMIA work permit and how is it different from E-2?**

Your knowledge base mentions LMIA as an alternative for applicants who do not qualify for E-2, but does not go into detailed LMIA guidance (which is a Canadian immigration process, not a U.S. one). In general terms, an LMIA is a document that a Canadian employer obtains to demonstrate they cannot fill a position with a Canadian worker, after which the foreign worker can apply for a Canadian work permit. It is fundamentally different from E-2 — it is a Canadian-side employment process rather than a U.S. investor visa. Your app flags it as an alternative direction but does not provide LMIA guidance, correctly routing users to other resources.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Outcome 5 alternatives list

---

**Q383. I want to invest in the U.S. but I don't want to manage a business. Is there any visa for that?**

Based on your source materials, there is no standard nonimmigrant visa for purely passive U.S. investment. The E-2 requires active management. EB-5 is an immigrant investor category but it leads to permanent residency and requires $800,000–$1.05M and job creation. There is no widely available U.S. visa category for a passive investor who simply wants to deploy capital without managing a business — and E-2 specifically excludes passive investment scenarios.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.7 — passive investment is disqualifying for E-2
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.6 — EB-5 as the immigrant investor alternative

---

**Q384. What if I have under $50,000 to invest? Is there any path?**

Your source soft-disqualifies investments under $50,000 with a direct message: *"An investment below $50,000 USD faces very high scrutiny and is difficult to approve for most business types."* The user is directed to attorney consultation. No specific E-2 path is identified for sub-$50K applicants in your materials. The alternatives — EB-5, other investor categories, or working with an attorney to find a genuinely low-cost business with a strong proportionality argument — are flagged without detailed guidance, because the app correctly treats this as outside its competency.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q4: under $50,000 soft disqualify with attorney referral

**Q385. I am not a Canadian citizen — I live in Canada but hold citizenship elsewhere. What should I do?**

If the person holds citizenship in **another treaty country**, they may qualify for E-2 through that citizenship. Your source says: *"If you hold citizenship in another treaty country, you may still qualify. See the full list of treaty countries."* The applicant would need to apply at a U.S. consulate appropriate to their treaty country, not necessarily Toronto. Your app routes non-Canadian citizens out of the main Canadian flow but offers the treaty-country redirect.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q1: not a Canadian citizen, soft disqualify with treaty country check offer

**Q416. What is the first thing the app asks a user?**

The very first question is: *"Are you a citizen of Canada?"* This is the primary eligibility gate — no other questions are worth asking if the fundamental treaty-citizenship requirement is not met. If the answer is no, the user is soft-disqualified but redirected to check whether their other citizenship may qualify them through a different treaty country.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q1, Module 1 Section A

---

**Q417. How does the app handle a user who is clearly ineligible?**

It uses a **soft disqualify** approach rather than a hard block. Users who are likely ineligible see a clear explanation of why, the alternatives available to them, and an offer to connect with an attorney. They are not prevented from accessing the app, but the communication is honest and direct. Users who override a soft disqualify do so explicitly and see risk advisories on every relevant subsequent screen.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Outcome 5: soft disqualify logic; Q4 override mechanism

**Q427. How does the compliance calendar know when to send alerts?**

The calendar is built from three layers: (1) the user's target interview date as the anchor for all pre-interview items, (2) static triggers that apply to all users, and (3) dynamic triggers generated from the specific answers in Module 2 and Module 3 — such as business category, state, dependent ages, entity formation status, lease date, and EIN status. Critical items cannot be dismissed without confirmation. Push notifications and emails fire at 7 days, 3 days, and 24 hours overdue.

**Source:** [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.7 — Calendar architecture: three layers; notification rules

**Q430. What is the Renewal Readiness Score?**

Red flags that reduce the score include: revenue not exceeding household income by 3–4x, no employees hired, significant missed projections across multiple years, and solo operator with no staff. The score is designed to give families time to course-correct before Year 5 renewal becomes urgent.

**Q441. What is the proportionality calculator?**
A built-in calculator in the investment section where the user enters total business cost and total personal investment. The outcome capture module records the result of the user's interview — approved, 221(g), denied — along with notes about questions asked. Attorney referrals are triggered by: F-1 status (COS), prior immigration violations, removal proceedings, pending immigrant petitions, unusual fund sources (large unexplained cash), ownership structure changes at renewal, child age-out at 20.5, and any situation explicitly flagged as outside the app's preparation scope. [KB] Vol 3, Sections 7.1, 7.3, 7.8
