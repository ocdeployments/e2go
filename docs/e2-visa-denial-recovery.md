## Understanding a 214(b) Denial

A refusal under **INA Section 214(b)** is a final denial of your visa
application at that interview. It means the consular officer determined
you did not satisfy the requirements of the E-2 classification.

**Key facts about 214(b) denials:**

1. **No appeal is available.** The officer's decision is final for that application.
   > "Today's decision cannot be appealed."
   > — U.S. Embassy Turkey — Visa Denial (tr.usembassy.gov)

2. **You may reapply immediately.** There is no mandatory waiting period.
   > "However, you may reapply at any time."
   > — U.S. Embassy Turkey — Visa Denial

3. **Reapplication requires new evidence or changed circumstances.**
   Simply reapplying with the same application is nearly certain to result
   in the same denial.
   > "If you decide to reapply, you should be prepared to provide information
   > that was not presented in your original application, or to demonstrate
   > that your circumstances have changed since that application."
   > — Travel.State.Gov — Visa Denials

4. **The standard MRV fee is payable again** for each new application.
   As of 2025–2026, the nonimmigrant visa application fee (MRV) is USD $185.
   The DS-156E (E visa supplement) requires a separate fee; confirm current
   fees at travel.state.gov.

## Step 1 — Identify the Reason for Denial

The officer will typically provide a brief verbal explanation at the interview
and may provide a written notice. The denial will be coded under 214(b) —
but this alone does not tell you which E-2 criterion failed.

**Immediately after the interview:**
- Write down everything the officer said, word for word
- Note the officer's demeanor, the questions asked, and your answers
- Note which documents the officer reviewed and which were not examined
- Note whether the officer expressed any specific concern or asked follow-up
  questions about a particular area

This contemporaneous record is essential for attorney review and for
identifying what to fix.

### Common Reasons for E-2 Denial Under 214(b)

| Reason | What It Means | What to Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Investment not substantial | Proportionality test not met | Increase investment amount; improve documentation of all expenditures |
| Funds not at risk / not irrevocably committed | Money not yet deployed into the business | Complete the investment before reapplying; document all expenditures |
| Business is marginal | Income/job creation projections not convincing | Strengthen business plan projections; add hiring commitments; open the business and show actual revenue |
| Immigrant intent | Officer not satisfied you will leave the U.S. | Strengthen Canadian ties; document property, family, financial connections |
| Develop and direct not shown | Officer not convinced you will actively manage | Clarify your management role; differentiate from employees |
| Source of funds insufficient | Funds trail not complete or not convincing | Fill documentation gaps; provide additional bank records; explain unexplained deposits |
| Business not a bona fide enterprise | Business doesn't appear real or operational | Provide more evidence of operations: lease, licenses, employees, inventory |
| Treaty nationality not established | Passport/citizenship documentation insufficient | Rare for Canadians — ensure Canadian citizenship documents are current |

## Step 2 — Consult an Immigration Attorney Before Reapplying

A 214(b) denial is a serious setback. Reapplying without professional
guidance and without addressing the specific deficiency that caused the
denial is the most common mistake applicants make.

> "The most important thing to do if you receive this E-2 visa denial is
> to write down exactly what was asked and answered at the E-2 visa interview.
> In addition, write down your impressions of the officer... Regardless of
> the type of denial, the most important thing is to speak with a lawyer."
> — Rupert Law Group — E-2 Visa Reasons for Denial

**What an attorney can do:**
- Review the original application and identify weaknesses
- Review your post-interview notes to understand what the officer focused on
- Contact the consulate (in some circumstances) for additional clarity
- Recommend specific changes to the application, business structure, or
  documentation approach
- In some cases, recommend a different consular post for reapplication

**When to engage an attorney (attorney referral triggers):**
- Any 214(b) denial — always
- A 221(g) that remains unresolved after 90 days
- A second 214(b) denial (especially at the same consular post)
- The denial appears to relate to immigrant intent concerns
- The denial involves questions about the source or legitimacy of funds
- You made material changes to the business after the original application

## Step 3 — Assess What Changed

A successful reapplication almost always involves one or more of:

### Option A — Complete the Investment

If denial was because the investment was not yet made or not at risk:
- Complete the full investment deployment into the business
- Obtain comprehensive documentation of all expenditures
- Open the business if possible — actual operations are the most compelling
  evidence that the business is real and the investment is at risk

### Option B — Strengthen the Business Plan

If denial related to marginality or viability:
- Obtain actual financial data (if the business is operating, use real numbers)
- Commission an independent market analysis
- Secure letters of intent from customers or clients
- Add concrete hiring commitments (signed employment offer letters)
- Revise projections using locally sourced data (not national averages)

### Option C — Address Immigrant Intent Concerns

If denial related to immigrant intent:
- Do not sell your Canadian home
- Retain Canadian financial accounts and investments
- Ensure your Canadian ties are thoroughly documented
- If your spouse or children are remaining in Canada (even temporarily),
  document this

### Option D — Increase the Investment Amount

If the proportionality test was not met:
- Additional investment in the business (equipment, inventory, expansion)
- Clearly document all new expenditures
- Ensure the investment is genuinely at risk (not withdrawable)

### Option E — Restructure the Business

If the business concept itself was questioned:
- In rare cases, the right response is to choose a different business
- A business with a more established track record (e.g., a franchise in
  a proven category) may present a stronger case than a novel concept

## Timeline for Reapplication

There is no mandatory waiting period between a 214(b) denial and reapplication.
However:

**Reapplying too quickly (within days or weeks) without addressing the
deficiency is almost certain to result in another denial.**

The officer database will show your prior denial. A new officer will know
you were previously denied and will look for what has changed.

Practical timelines:
- **Investment not yet complete:** Reapply when the investment is fully
  deployed and documented — typically 4–12 weeks after denial
- **Business needs to open to demonstrate non-marginality:** Reapply after
  opening and generating initial revenue — potentially 2–6 months
- **Immigrant intent concerns:** Address ties, potentially reapply in
  3–6 months with stronger tie documentation
- **Application required a complete rebuild:** 4–8 weeks minimum with
  professional assistance

## Second and Third Denials — Escalating Severity

A second denial at the same consular post on the same application concept
is a serious signal that either:
1. The fundamental business concept does not meet E-2 standards
2. The applicant's circumstances create inherent immigrant intent concerns
   that cannot be easily overcome
3. The application presentation itself is poor

**After a second denial, strongly consider:**
- Full review by an experienced E-2 immigration attorney (not just
  document preparation assistance)
- Evaluating whether a different business concept may be more viable
- Consulting on whether any changes to personal circumstances would
  strengthen the application
- Whether USCIS COS (Change of Status) inside the U.S. may be an
  alternative if you have lawful U.S. presence

## When Denial Is the Right Outcome — Recognizing a Non-Viable Application

Some E-2 applications are denied because the application genuinely does
not meet the legal standard — not because of a fixable documentation problem.

Indicators that the business concept itself may not be viable for E-2:
- A solo-operator service business where all revenue depends entirely
  on the investor's personal labor (haircut, personal coaching, etc.)
- Investment below what is needed to establish the business as non-marginal
  even after full deployment
- Business type in a sector the State Department has historically viewed
  skeptically (see `kb/franchise-e2-compatibility.md` for business categories)

If a qualified immigration attorney advises that the current concept is
not viable for E-2, treat this as valuable information — not a failure.
Choosing a business specifically designed to satisfy E-2 criteria (often
a franchise in a proven category) is the most reliable path to approval.
