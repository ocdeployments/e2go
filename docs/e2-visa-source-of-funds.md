# E-2 Visa — Source and Path of Funds
**kb/e2-visa-source-of-funds.md**
*Sources: 9 FAM 402.9-6(B) — State Department Foreign Affairs Manual (fam.state.gov);
22 CFR 41.51; RJ Immigration Law; Kuzmin Law; e2visalawyer.net*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## The Legal Requirement

The E-2 investment funds must be:
1. **Lawfully acquired** — from a legitimate source
2. **Traceable** — the path from origin to the U.S. business must be documented
3. **At risk** — committed to the enterprise, not sitting in a personal account
4. **Irrevocably committed** — not subject to retrieval if the visa is denied
   (unless held in escrow)

> "The treaty investor must demonstrate that the capital invested was
> obtained through lawful means."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(b)

The FAM explicitly states the funds do not need to originate outside the U.S.:
> "The statute does not require that the source of the funds be outside the U.S."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(b)

## Common Lawful Fund Sources

### 1. Personal Savings

The most straightforward source. Documentation required:
- Bank statements showing the accumulation of funds over time (typically
  12–24 months of statements)
- If funds were previously held across multiple institutions, statements
  for each account
- Evidence of the income source that generated the savings:
  - Employment: T4 slips, pay stubs, tax returns (Canadian T1)
  - Self-employment: business financial statements, T1 returns, T4A slips
  - Investment income: T5 slips, brokerage statements

**Common gap risk:** A large cash deposit appearing in statements without
explanation. If you received a lump sum (bonus, commission, lawsuit settlement,
business sale proceeds), document its source specifically.

### 2. Home Equity / Real Estate Proceeds

Many Canadian E-2 applicants fund their investment from the sale of a
Canadian home or from a home equity line of credit (HELOC).

**Sale proceeds:** Provide:
- Purchase agreement (original property purchase)
- Sale agreement or conveyancing records
- Lawyer's statement of adjustments showing net proceeds
- Bank deposit record showing proceeds entering your account

**HELOC or mortgage on Canadian property:**
Home equity loans are acceptable at-risk funds because they are collateralized
by the Canadian property — not by the U.S. business assets. You bear personal
risk if the business fails and you cannot repay the loan.

> Contrast: A loan collateralized by the U.S. business assets themselves
> does not count — if the business fails, the lender takes the business
> assets and you bear no personal risk. These loans cannot be counted.
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c)(1)

### 3. Business Sale Proceeds

If you sold a Canadian business to fund the E-2 investment:
- Asset purchase or share purchase agreement
- Closing statement showing net proceeds
- Bank deposit records
- Evidence of business ownership (corporate registration, financial statements)

### 4. Retirement Account Withdrawals (RRSP)

Funds withdrawn from Canadian RRSPs are lawful source funds after Canadian
withholding tax is paid. Documentation:
- RRSP withdrawal confirmation
- Canadian T4RSP slip
- Bank records showing net amount received

Note the Canadian and U.S. tax implications of RRSP withdrawals before
executing this strategy. See `kb/rrsp-tfsa-crossborder.md`.

### 5. Gifts

Gift funds are acceptable if properly documented.

> "A gift of investment funds may qualify as lawfully acquired capital if
> the gift itself was lawfully made."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(b)

**Documentation required:**
- Signed gift letter identifying:
  - The donor
  - The recipient
  - The amount
  - That no repayment is expected or required
  - The donor's relationship to the investor
- Bank records showing the transfer
- Evidence that the donor had the funds to give:
  - Donor's bank statements
  - Evidence of the donor's income or asset source

**Watch for:** A gift from a family member that closely resembles a loan
(with informal repayment expectations) will be scrutinized. If there is any
repayment agreement — formal or informal — it is a loan, not a gift.

**Gift taxes:** Receiving a gift in Canada does not generally create a
Canadian tax liability (Canada has no gift tax). However, U.S. gift tax
rules may apply to large gifts received from non-U.S. persons. Consult
a cross-border CPA.

### 6. Loans — Personal

A personal loan or line of credit from a bank or family member can be
qualifying investment funds if:
- The loan is **secured by personal assets** (not by U.S. business assets)
- You personally guarantee the loan
- You bear personal risk of loss

Documentation:
- Loan agreement
- Evidence of loan proceeds deposited to your account
- Evidence of collateral (if secured)

**Loans from family members:** These receive heightened scrutiny because
of the risk they are informal arrangements that do not constitute genuine
at-risk investment. Ensure the family loan has a written loan agreement,
market-rate interest, and clear repayment terms.

### 7. Inheritance

Inherited funds are lawful if documented:
- Probate or estate documents establishing entitlement
- Distribution records from the estate
- Bank records showing receipt

## The Tracing Requirement — Path of Funds

"Source" tells the officer where the money came from. "Path" tells the
officer how it got from the source to the U.S. business. Both must be
documented.

### The Funds Trail Must Be Unbroken

A consular officer should be able to follow a clear, documented chain:

**Example — clean funds trail:**
1. Employment income earned 2019–2024 → deposited to TD Bank savings account
2. TD savings account → transferred to RBC chequing (shown on statements)
3. RBC chequing → wire transfer USD $125,000 to U.S. business bank account (Wells Fargo)
4. Wells Fargo business account → payments to franchise franchisor ($45,000),
   equipment supplier ($38,000), landlord (first/last + security $22,000),
   signage contractor ($12,000)

Each link in this chain is documented with bank statements, wire transfer
confirmations, and receipts.

### Gaps in the Path

**Red flags that create path gaps:**
- Funds moving through an intermediary account not explained in documentation
- Cash withdrawals with no explanation of where the cash went
- Currency exchanges without documentation
- Transfers through cryptocurrency without clear conversion records
- Funds moving through a business account for personal investment purposes

**How to address gaps:** If your path has an unexplained step, document it
proactively in your Source of Funds narrative (Template 1 in E2Pathway).
A brief, honest explanation with supporting documents is far better than
leaving the officer to wonder.

## Source of Funds Template — Structure

E2Pathway Template 1 (Source of Funds) generates a chronological narrative
documenting:

1. **The source section** — where each dollar came from (employment, savings,
   real estate, RRSP, gift, loan), with amounts and dates
2. **The path section** — how funds moved from source to the U.S. business
   account, step by step
3. **The investment section** — how funds were deployed in the business
   (what was purchased, when, and for how much)
4. **Document index** — list of every supporting document, organized by
   exhibit letter

The narrative is supported by Tab H of the application binder, which contains
all source documentation.

## High-Risk Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Funds From Multiple Sources

Mixing funds from multiple sources (savings + RRSP + home equity) requires
documentation for each source and a clear narrative explaining the combination.
This is common and acceptable — just ensure every source is addressed.

### Scenario 2 — Funds Through a Foreign Intermediate Account

If funds passed through a third country (e.g., a Cayman Islands account,
a Hong Kong holding company), heightened documentation is required. Officers
will want to understand the legitimate business reason for the intermediate
structure.

### Scenario 3 — Funds From a Business With Complex Ownership

If your savings were generated by a business that you owned or operated,
provide business financial statements, corporate tax returns, and evidence
of dividends or salary paid to you that ended up in the personal savings
being invested.

### Scenario 4 — Recent Large Deposits

A large deposit appearing in the most recent 1–3 months of statements without
a clear documented source is a major red flag. Officers assume undocumented
large deposits may be proceeds of illegal activity. Pre-empt this by
documenting the source of any large deposit, regardless of when it occurred.

## Documentation Master Checklist — Source of Funds

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| 24 months personal bank statements (all accounts) | Establish savings history |
| Canadian T1 tax returns (3–5 years) | Corroborate employment/business income |
| T4/T4A/T5 slips | Income from employment or investments |
| Property sale closing documents | Real estate proceeds |
| RRSP withdrawal slip + T4RSP | RRSP source |
| Loan agreement + bank record | Loan source |
| Gift letter + donor bank statements | Gift source |
| Wire transfer confirmations | Funds movement to U.S. |
| Business bank account statements | Deployment of funds |
| Invoices/receipts for all expenditures | Proof of investment |
| Escrow agreement (if applicable) | Irrevocable commitment pre-approval |
