## MASTER LEGAL FRAMEWORK

Before addressing individual categories, the five core tests every E-2 application must satisfy are established in **9 FAM 402.9-6(A)** and **INA 101(a)(15)(E)(ii)**:

1. **Real and active commercial enterprise** — not a paper organization or speculative/idle investment [9 FAM 402.9-6(C)]
2. **Substantial investment** — proportional to total business cost [9 FAM 402.9-6(D)]
3. **Funds at risk** — irrevocably committed, at risk of total or partial loss [9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c)]
4. **Non-marginal enterprise** — present or future capacity to generate more than a minimal living, with significant economic contribution [9 FAM 402.9-6(E)]
5. **Develop and direct** — investor controls and actively manages the enterprise [9 FAM 402.9-6(F)]

A business fails E-2 eligibility if it fails *any* of these five tests — not just one.

# SECTION 1 — OFFICIALLY INELIGIBLE CATEGORIES

---

## 1. Passive Investment Vehicles
*(Holding companies, REITs, stock portfolios)*

### Official Government Position: INELIGIBLE ✅

**What makes an investment "passive" vs. "active":**

9 FAM 402.9-6(C) provides the controlling test:

> *"It cannot be a paper organization or an idle speculative investment held for potential appreciation in value, such as **undeveloped land or stocks held by an investor without the intent to direct the enterprise**."*

The State Department's E-2 overview page further states:

> *"Uncommitted or revokable funds in a bank account or similar security are generally not considered an investment."*

The key distinction is **intent to direct** combined with **actual operational activity**. Passive vehicles fail because:
- They have no entrepreneur actively developing and directing operations
- They produce no service or commodity (they hold or appreciate assets)
- There is no operational enterprise for the investor to manage

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(C) — https://fam.state.gov/fam/09FAM/09FAM040209.html  
**Source:** State Dept. E-2 Overview — https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/employment/treaty-trader-investor-visa-e.html

---

### Can a Holding Company Ever Qualify? ✅

**Yes, under specific conditions.** 9 FAM 402.9 does not categorically bar all holding company structures. The Walsh/Pollard decision discussed in **9 FAM 402.9-6(G)** confirms that a foreign entity creating a U.S. subsidiary to fulfill a contract can constitute an E-2 investment — the U.S. subsidiary was the investment vehicle.

The test is whether the holding company **actively directs real operating subsidiaries** rather than passively holding shares. If the investor controls and directs a holding structure that in turn runs real operating businesses, the "develop and direct" test may be met at the holding company level.

**However:** A pure holding company with no operational activity of its own — that simply holds stock and receives distributions — fails the "real and active commercial enterprise" test.

🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED** — Holding company structures in E-2 applications require careful legal structuring and are highly fact-specific.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(C); 9 FAM 402.9-6(G) (Walsh/Pollard)

---

### Official Definition of "Develop and Direct" ✅

The legal standard is in **9 FAM 402.9-6(F)(a)**:

> *"It must also be shown, in accordance with INA 101(a)(15)(E)(ii), that a national (or nationals) of the treaty country, through ownership or by other means, develops and directs the activities of the enterprise."*

Develop and direct is demonstrated through:
- At least 50% ownership **plus** active management, OR
- Operational control through a managerial position or other corporate device [9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(c)]

**Merely occupying a managerial position is not sufficient** if the applicant does not and will not control the enterprise. [9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(c)]

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

For passive investment: **PRIMARILY STRUCTURE.**  
A real estate company can be E-2 eligible if *actively managed*. The same assets held passively are not. The legal test focuses on what the investor *does* with the enterprise, not merely what the enterprise holds.

---

### Does a Franchise Version Change the Analysis?

No. A franchise holding company where the franchisee passively collects income and lets a manager run operations would still fail the "develop and direct" test. The franchise wrapper does not substitute for the investor's active management obligation.

---

## 2. Marginal Enterprises

### Official Government Position: INELIGIBLE ✅

**Official definition of "marginal" — 9 FAM 402.9-6(E):**

> *"A marginal enterprise is an enterprise that does not have the present or future capacity to generate enough income to provide more than a minimal living for the treaty investor and their family. An enterprise that does not have the capacity to generate such income but that has a present or future capacity to make a **significant economic contribution** is not a marginal enterprise."*

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(E) — https://fam.state.gov/fam/09FAM/09FAM040209.html

---

### The Two-Part Marginality Test ✅

9 FAM 402.9-6(E) creates a two-path test — a business passes the non-marginality requirement if it meets **either** of these:

**Path A — Income Test:**  
Present or future capacity to generate income that exceeds a minimal living for the investor and family.

**Path B — Economic Contribution Test:**  
Even if income is currently low, the enterprise has present or future capacity to make a **significant economic contribution** (e.g., job creation, substantial economic activity).

**Critical timeline:** The projected future capacity under Path B should generally be **realizable within five years** from the date the applicant commences normal business activity.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(E)

---

### What Evidence Satisfies Non-Marginality? ✅

9 FAM 402.9-6(E) does not specify an exhaustive list, but the State Department E-2 overview adds:

> *"The enterprise must generate more income than just to provide a living to you and family, **or it must have a significant economic impact in the United States**."*

Accepted evidence categories (drawn from the FAM framework):
- Financial projections showing revenue substantially exceeding household income need
- Job creation plan — U.S. workers hired or committed to be hired
- Market research supporting realistic projections
- Prior operating history if buying an existing business
- Franchise system's documented unit economics

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(E); State Dept. E-2 Overview

---

### Job Creation as Non-Marginality Evidence — How Many Jobs, What Timeline? ⚠️ UNVERIFIED (specific number)

**No specific job count or timeline is mandated in 9 FAM 402.9 or the Embassy FAQ.** The FAM says only that there must be "significant economic contribution" and that future capacity should generally be realizable within five years.

The requirement for a specific number of jobs appears in **EB-5** (10 full-time jobs) — not in E-2. The E-2 standard is qualitative, not a fixed count.

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — Any specific job count stated as an E-2 requirement (e.g., "you must hire 2 employees by Year 1") is not from an official source. The standard is whether the business makes a significant economic contribution, which can be demonstrated through a credible hiring plan without a fixed number.

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**STRUCTURE.** Marginality is determined by what the business *produces economically*, not by what type of business it is. A restaurant can be marginal; a restaurant with strong projections and a hiring plan is not.

---

### Does a Franchise Version Change the Analysis?

Yes, favorably. Established franchise systems with documented unit economics (via the Franchise Disclosure Document / FDD) provide officer-reviewable evidence of projected revenue and job creation across the system, which supports non-marginality arguments.

---

## 3. Non-Profit and Charitable Organizations

### Official Government Position: INELIGIBLE (absolute bar) ✅

**The official source is 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c) and 9 FAM 402.9-6(C):**

From 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c):
> *"E-2 investor status must not, therefore, be extended to non-profit organizations."*

From 9 FAM 402.9-6(C):
> *"The investment must be a commercial enterprise; it must be **for profit**, eliminating non-profit organizations from consideration."*

The bar is stated in absolute terms in the FAM. There is no exception language in 9 FAM 402.9 for non-profit organizations with partial commercial activity.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c); 9 FAM 402.9-6(C)

---

### Is This an Absolute Bar? ✅

**Yes.** The FAM language is unambiguous: "must not" and "eliminating non-profit organizations from consideration." There is no stated exception.

---

### What About a For-Profit Business with a Charitable Mission? 🔒

A **legally structured for-profit entity** (LLC or corporation) with a social or charitable mission is not automatically disqualified — the legal test is whether the entity is organized for profit and whether the investment is at risk with a return expected. A B-Corp, social enterprise, or mission-driven business organized as a for-profit entity may qualify, provided all other E-2 requirements are met.

A **hybrid** structure where part of the entity is non-profit and part is for-profit would require careful legal structuring.

🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — There is no official government guidance specifically addressing for-profit social enterprises in the E-2 context. The safe rule is: if organized as a for-profit entity, the bar does not apply; if organized as a non-profit, it does.

# SECTION 2 — HIGH SCRUTINY CATEGORIES

## 4. Home-Based Businesses

### Official Government Position: NOT AUTOMATICALLY DISQUALIFIED ✅

**9 FAM 402.9-4(D) — Physical Office Space:**

> *"An applicant does not necessarily need a physical office space to qualify for an E visa. Although having physical office space may be relevant in determining whether the requirements for an E visa have been met, it is **not a requirement** to qualify for the visa."*

This is the clearest official statement on this issue. Physical office space is **relevant but not required.**

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-4(D) — https://fam.state.gov/fam/09FAM/09FAM040209.html

---

### Does a Home Office Ever Qualify? ✅

**Yes, per 9 FAM 402.9-4(D).** However, the absence of commercial premises shifts scrutiny to whether the enterprise is genuinely real and active. The "real and active commercial enterprise" test under 9 FAM 402.9-6(C) still applies fully — the question is whether the business produces services or commodities with genuine commercial operations.

Evidence that strengthens a home-based application:
- Demonstrated client relationships and contracts
- Revenue history or credible projections
- Dedicated workspace (even within a home)
- Business licenses, EIN, business bank account
- Physical evidence of operations (equipment, inventory)

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**STRUCTURE.** A home-based business is scrutinized on whether it is genuinely operational, not automatically disqualified by location.

---

### Does a Franchise Version Change the Analysis?

Yes, somewhat. A home-based franchise with an established brand, documented FDD, and proven unit economics provides stronger evidence of a genuine commercial enterprise than a self-created home-based business.

---

## 5. Online-Only / E-Commerce Businesses

### Official Government Position: NOT AUTOMATICALLY DISQUALIFIED ✅

The same 9 FAM 402.9-4(D) language applies:

> *"An applicant does not necessarily need a physical office space to qualify for an E visa."*

The commercial enterprise test under 9 FAM 402.9-6(C) still requires the enterprise to be "real and active" and to "produce some service or commodity." An online business can satisfy this if it demonstrably does so.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-4(D); 9 FAM 402.9-6(C)

---

### Does a Registered U.S. LLC with No Physical Location Qualify?

**Potentially yes, but entity registration alone is insufficient.** 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(e) states:

> *"Moreover, for the applicant to be 'in the process of investing', the applicant must be **close to the start of actual business operations**, not simply in the stage of signing contracts...or scouting for suitable locations and property."*

An LLC registration without demonstrated operational activity — revenue, clients, contracts, inventory — does not meet the commercial enterprise test.

---

### What Evidence Is Required?

Based on 9 FAM 402.9-6(C) and 9 FAM 402.9-6(B):
- Actual revenue records or signed client contracts
- Documented business operations (orders, fulfillment, service delivery)
- Business bank account showing transactions
- Evidence of investment deployment (inventory, technology, software)
- Non-marginality evidence: projections beyond minimal living

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**STRUCTURE.** An online business that genuinely produces services or commodities for profit and is actively operated can qualify. The scrutiny is on whether operations are real, not whether there is a physical storefront.

---

## 6. Single-Person Service Businesses (Consulting, Coaching, Freelancing)

### Official Government Position: HIGH SCRUTINY — Two distinct problems ✅

**Problem 1: The Marginality Test**

9 FAM 402.9-6(E) requires capacity beyond "minimal living." A solo consultant generating income only sufficient to support one person and their family, with no hiring plan, typically fails this test.

**Problem 2: The "Develop and Direct" / Real Enterprise Test**

9 FAM 402.9-6(C) requires a "real and active commercial or entrepreneurial undertaking, producing some service or commodity." A single-person consulting arrangement where the investor *is* the product raises the question of whether there is an independent *enterprise* being directed, or simply self-employment.

9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(b)(4) is instructive:
> *"At the extreme opposite pole, the cost to purchase an ongoing commercial enterprise or to establish a service business, such as **a consulting firm**, may be relatively low."*

The FAM does not categorically bar consulting businesses — it acknowledges their low cost and notes that the proportionality test still applies.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(C); 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(b)(4); 9 FAM 402.9-6(E)

---

### What Makes a Consulting Business Non-Marginal?

Based on the 9 FAM framework:
- Revenue projections substantially exceeding investor's household income need
- Demonstrated plan to hire U.S. employees or contractors
- Substantial client base or contracts generating real economic activity
- Investment of sufficient capital in business infrastructure (not just personal labor)

🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED** — Solo consulting businesses are among the most difficult E-2 applications. Whether a specific consulting business qualifies depends heavily on the specific facts, revenue projections, and hiring plan.

---

### What Job Creation Evidence Is Required?

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED (specific number)** — As noted under Section 2, 9 FAM 402.9 does not mandate a specific job count for E-2. The standard is significant economic contribution. For a solo service business, a credible, documented hiring plan is the key evidence — but the FAM does not specify a minimum number.

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**PRIMARILY STRUCTURE.** A consulting *firm* with multiple employees, demonstrated revenue, and a genuine investment is different from a solo self-employment arrangement. The structure determines whether there is an enterprise to develop and direct.

---

## 7. Real Estate Businesses

### Official Government Position: Depends entirely on STRUCTURE ✅

**Passive real estate investment: INELIGIBLE**

9 FAM 402.9-6(C) explicitly uses undeveloped land as an example of a disqualifying speculative investment:
> *"It cannot be a paper organization or an idle speculative investment held for potential appreciation in value, such as **undeveloped land** or stocks held by an investor without the intent to direct the enterprise."*

The same logic applies to rental portfolios and REITs — the investor holds assets but does not direct an enterprise that produces services or commodities.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(C)

---

### Does a Property Management Company Qualify? ✅ / 🔒

**Potentially yes.** The critical distinction is whether the investor is:
- **Actively managing** a commercial service enterprise (property management company) — this involves hiring staff, managing client relationships, delivering management services, directing operations — potentially qualifies.
- **Passively collecting** rent from properties they own — does not qualify.

A property management company that provides management services *to third parties* is a genuine service business; the investment is in the management operations, not in the underlying properties.

🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED** — Fact-specific; the structure of the management company and the nature of the investor's role matter greatly.

---

### Does Real Estate Development Qualify? 🔒 ⚠️

**Potentially, but highly fact-specific.** A real estate development company that actively develops, constructs, and sells or leases properties is arguably an active commercial enterprise producing a commodity (developed real estate). However:
- The undeveloped land example in 9 FAM 402.9-6(C) suggests speculative land holding does not qualify
- Active development with genuine operations and staff is structurally different from passive holding

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — No official source reviewed explicitly addresses real estate development companies. This is a gap in the official guidance.  
🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

---

### Does a Real Estate Brokerage Qualify? 🔒

A real estate brokerage (active service business, licensed brokers, client transactions, business operations) is structurally different from passive investment — it produces services for profit. The core E-2 tests (commercial enterprise, substantiality, non-marginality, develop and direct) would still apply on their individual merits.

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — Official sources do not specifically address real estate brokerages. The analysis follows from applying the standard five-part E-2 test.  
🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**PRIMARILY STRUCTURE** — one of the clearest examples in the E-2 context. The same real estate assets can be part of a qualifying business (actively managed property management company) or a disqualifying one (passive rental portfolio), depending entirely on the business structure and the investor's role.

## 8. Businesses Requiring U.S. Professional Licensure
*(Medical, dental, legal, financial advisory)*

### Official Government Position on Licensing: ADDRESSED IN EMBASSY FAQ ✅

From the U.S. Embassy Canada FAQ:
> *"Licensing and permit requirements in the United States fall mainly under state and local law, and vary with the type of business you wish to operate. For specific information, please contact the appropriate government offices in the locality where you plan to start your business."*

**Source:** U.S. Embassy Canada FAQ — https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

### Can an Applicant Operate a Licensed Profession Without a U.S. License? ⚠️ 🔒

**9 FAM 402.9 does not address professional licensure specifically** as an E-2 eligibility requirement. However:

- State law typically prohibits practicing medicine, dentistry, law, or financial advisory without the applicable license
- An applicant may be able to *own and manage* a practice (as an investor/director) while licensed professionals perform the work — this is the model many E-2 medical practice investors use
- Whether the E-2 investor personally needs the license depends on whether the investor's role is managerial/investor rather than direct clinical or legal service delivery

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — The official sources reviewed do not address professional licensure as an E-2 qualification issue. This is a state-law and practice-structure question, not a federal immigration eligibility rule per se.  
🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED** — Both immigration counsel and the applicable state licensing board should be consulted.

---

### What Happens if Licensure Is Obtained After Visa Issuance?

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — The official sources reviewed do not address this scenario. Generally, the business must be real and operating, or close to operational, by the interview date. If a required license is a prerequisite to *any* operations, having it in-process (applied for but not yet granted) may be acceptable — but whether this satisfies the officer depends on the specific business type and the officer's discretion.

🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**STRUCTURE.** The question is whether the investor manages the practice as a business owner/director vs. personally delivering licensed professional services without a license.

## 9. Cannabis Businesses

### Official Government Position ✅

**No official source reviewed — 9 FAM 402.9, the State Department E-2 overview, or the Embassy Canada FAQ — directly addresses cannabis businesses by name.**

However, the legal framework makes the answer derivable from two established principles:

**Principle 1 — Investment must not be from illicit activities:**  
9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(b) states:
> *"The source of the investment must not...be the result of illicit activities."*

**Principle 2 — The enterprise must be a real and active commercial enterprise:**  
9 FAM 402.9-6(C) requires a commercial enterprise. Federal law (the Controlled Substances Act) classifies cannabis as a Schedule I controlled substance. A business that is illegal under federal law cannot be a lawful commercial enterprise for federal visa purposes.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(b); 9 FAM 402.9-6(C); Controlled Substances Act (21 U.S.C. § 811)

---

### Federal vs. State Law Conflict ✅

**Federal law governs.** Immigration law is federal. The E-2 visa is a federal classification administered by the U.S. Department of State and USCIS. State-level cannabis legalization does not affect the federal illegality analysis for immigration purposes.

---

### Official State Department / USCIS Position ⚠️

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — No explicit official policy statement from the State Department or USCIS specifically addressing cannabis businesses in the E-2 context was found in the official sources reviewed. The conclusion that cannabis businesses are disqualifying follows from application of existing principles (federal illegality + commercial enterprise requirement), not from an explicit policy statement.

🔄 **VERIFY CURRENT STATUS** — Federal cannabis policy is subject to potential change (rescheduling, enforcement policy shifts). This should be verified before any major release.  
🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**BUSINESS TYPE** — for as long as cannabis remains federally illegal. Unlike most other categories, the disqualification is not addressable through restructuring.

## 10. Staffing and Labor Businesses

### Official Government Position: HIGH SCRUTINY — Job Shop Warning ✅

**9 FAM 402.9-6(G) addresses this directly** in the Walsh/Pollard discussion:

> *"A job shop usually involves the providing of workers needed by an employer to perform pre-designated duties... The fact pattern of this decision is not that of a job shop, nor does it in any way **facilitate the creation of job shops** under the E-2 visa classification."*

The FAM distinguishes between:
- **A legitimate project-oriented enterprise** (like the Walsh/Pollard engineering firm) — a company providing a specific contracted commodity (design services) that the client cannot perform itself
- **A job shop** — simply placing workers into the client employer's vacant positions

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(G) — https://fam.state.gov/fam/09FAM/09FAM040209.html

---

### The "Develop and Direct" Requirement for Staffing Businesses ✅

9 FAM 402.9-6(G)(c)(2) confirms:
> *"The test of 'develop and direct' applies only to the investor(s), not to the individual employees."*

For a staffing agency, the investor must develop and direct the *agency itself* — its operations, client relationships, recruitment processes, business development — not merely supervise the workers placed at client sites.

Whether a staffing agency qualifies depends on whether:
- The agency is a genuine commercial enterprise with its own operations
- The investor actively directs the agency's business
- The business is non-marginal (generates significant economic contribution)

🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

---

### Does a Franchise Staffing Agency Version Change the Analysis?

Potentially yes — a franchise system with established operations, proven revenues, and documented job creation could more readily satisfy non-marginality and the "real enterprise" tests than a startup staffing arrangement.

---

### Is the Issue Business TYPE or Business STRUCTURE?

**PRIMARILY STRUCTURE.** A professionally operated staffing agency with real infrastructure differs from a bare job-shop arrangement. The key test is whether there is a genuine independent enterprise being directed.

## 11. What Franchise Characteristics Make the Strongest E-2 Applications?

### Official Government Position on Franchise Documentation ✅

9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)(3) addresses how costs for a new business are established:
> *"The actual cost can usually be determined by combining the cost of the assets the investor has already purchased with the cost estimates for the procurement of additional assets needed to run the business. For example, cost may be established through **invoices or contracts for substantial purchases of equipment and inventory**; appraisals of the market value of land, buildings, equipment, and machinery; accounting audits; and records submissions to various governmental authorities."*

Applied to franchises, the FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document) serves exactly this function — it documents the total cost of the franchise, the investment required, and the historical financial performance of similar franchise units.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)(3)

---

### FDD and What It Demonstrates ✅ / ⚠️

**Officially confirmed:** A franchise's documented investment costs satisfy the 9 FAM requirement to establish the "actual cost" of a new business for the proportionality test.

**Not officially stated but structurally supported:** Item 19 (Financial Performance Representations) in an FDD, where provided, gives officer-reviewable historical unit economics that support non-marginality arguments.

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — No official source specifically states that an FDD with Item 19 data is required or sufficient for E-2 purposes. However, the framework of 9 FAM 402.9-6(D) strongly supports its relevance.

---

### Prior E-2 Approvals for the Brand ✅ / 🔄

Under 9 FAM 402.9, once a company achieves **E-Visa Company Registration** (addressed in the Embassy Canada FAQ), subsequent employees can apply under a streamlined process. However, the FAQ clarifies:
> *"Unless there have been substantive changes to the enterprise that would jeopardize its E-visa status, subsequent applications can focus on whether and how a given employee qualifies."*

Applied to franchises: prior investor approvals for a specific franchise brand provide evidence that the brand itself has previously met E-2 standards, which may support the new investor's application. But prior approvals do not guarantee or substitute for the new investor's independent qualification.

**Source:** Embassy Canada FAQ — https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/  
🔄 **VERIFY CURRENT STATUS** — E-visa company registration status for specific brands should be verified at time of application.

---

## 12. Franchise Categories That Face Additional Scrutiny

### Home-Based Franchise Concepts ✅

Per 9 FAM 402.9-4(D), physical office space is not required. But the scrutiny on demonstrating real operations increases when there is no commercial premises. Home-based franchise concepts must work harder to demonstrate the business is real and active.

### Single-Operator Franchise Models ⚠️ 🔒

A franchise designed to be operated entirely by one person with no employees raises both the marginality question (significant economic contribution) and the "develop and direct" question (if the investor is also the sole labor provider, is there an enterprise being directed or just self-employment?).

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — No official source specifically addresses single-operator franchise models in the E-2 context.  
🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

### Low-Investment Franchise Tiers ✅ / ⚠️

9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(b) confirms there is no minimum dollar amount: *"No set dollar figure constitutes a minimum amount of investment to be considered 'substantial'."* However, the proportionality test means that low-cost franchises require a higher *percentage* investment. Below approximately $50,000–$75,000 total investment, most consulates apply heightened scrutiny.

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — The $50,000–$75,000 practical floor is based on consulate practice, not any official written threshold.

## 13. Partnership Structures

### Two Partners, One E-2 — Does Partner's Nationality Matter?

**If one partner is a U.S. citizen or permanent resident:** ✅

9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(e):
> *"A trader or investor with the nationality of a treaty country but who holds U.S. LPR status does not qualify to bring in employees under INA 101(a)(15)(E). Moreover, **stock shares owned by U.S. LPRs cannot be considered in determining the nationality of the business.**"*

This means shares owned by a U.S. citizen or LPR do not count toward the required 50% treaty-country ownership. The Canadian partner's own 50% must independently meet the ownership threshold.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(e)

---

### Minority Ownership — Official Minimum Percentage ✅

**22 CFR 41.51(b)(2)(ii)** (cited in 9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(c)):
> *"Nationals of the treaty country must own at least **50 percent** of the business in question when the investor is an organization, and the applicant is an employee."*

For the principal investor coming to develop and direct: per 9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(c), control is *normally* shown through **at least 50% ownership**, but can also be shown through *operational control* (managerial position or other corporate device).

A minority owner below 50% who cannot demonstrate operational control through other means does not qualify as a principal E-2 investor.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(c); 22 CFR 41.51(b)(2)(ii)

---

### Can a U.S. Citizen Be a Partner in an E-2 Business? ✅

**Yes.** Nothing in 9 FAM 402.9 prohibits a U.S. citizen from being a co-owner. The E-2 investor simply must independently meet the 50% ownership (or operational control) requirement. U.S. citizen ownership shares do not count toward the treaty-country nationality calculation, but they do not disqualify the business from having a qualifying investor.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-4(B)(c) and (e)

---

### The Negative Control Doctrine — Two Equal Partners ✅

Per 9 FAM 402.9-6(F)(b) (confirmed earlier):
> *"An equal share of the investment in a joint venture or an equal partnership of two parties, generally gives controlling interest, if the joint venture and partner each retain full management rights and responsibilities. This arrangement is often called 'Negative Control.'"*
>
> *"The Department of State has determined, however, that an equal partnership with **more than two partners** would not give any of the parties control based on ownership."*

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(F)(b)

---

## 14. Acquisition vs. Startup

### Buying an Existing Business — What Changes ✅

**Cost determination changes.** 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)(2):
> *"The cost of an established business is generally its **purchase price**, which is normally the fair market value."*

For a startup, cost is actual setup cost. For an acquisition, cost is purchase price/fair market value.

**Substantiality re-evaluation at acquisition:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(a) states:
> *"Once you determine that an applicant has invested a substantial sum, the applicant generally does not need to be evaluated under this criterion again **unless there has been a change in ownership** usually through a business acquisition."*

This means acquisition events trigger fresh substantiality review.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(a); 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)(2)

---

### Purchasing a Struggling Business to Revive It ⚠️ 🔒

9 FAM 402.9-6(E) uses a **two-part marginality test** — present capacity OR future capacity. A currently struggling business might qualify if it has genuine future capacity to generate more than minimal living or make a significant economic contribution, realizable within five years.

⚠️ **UNVERIFIED** — No official source specifically addresses "turnaround" or "struggling business" acquisition scenarios. The analysis applies the standard two-part marginality framework.  
🔒 **PROFESSIONAL ADVICE RECOMMENDED**

---

### What Documentation Is Required for Acquisitions? ✅

9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c) guidance on cost establishment applies:
- Purchase agreement establishing fair market value
- Audited financial statements or accounting records of the target business
- Evidence of the applicant's investment deployment (funds transferred, purchase completed)
- Evidence the applicant will develop and direct the acquired business (management plan, role description)

For an existing business, the prior operating history and financial performance become the primary non-marginality evidence, replacing or supplementing projections.

**Source:** 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c); 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(d)
