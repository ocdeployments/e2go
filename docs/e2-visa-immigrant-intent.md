# E-2 Visa — Immigrant Intent and 214(b) Refusals
**kb/e2-visa-immigrant-intent.md**
*Sources: INA Section 214(b); INA Section 101(a)(15)(E)(ii); 9 FAM 302.1-2;
9 FAM 402.9-4(B); Travel.State.Gov — Visa Denials; U.S. Embassy Turkey — 214(b)*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## The Legal Framework

Every nonimmigrant visa applicant — including E-2 — is presumed to be an
intending immigrant under **INA Section 214(b)** unless they can demonstrate
otherwise:

> "Every alien shall be presumed to be an immigrant until he establishes to
> the satisfaction of the consular officer, at the time of application for
> a visa, that he is entitled to a nonimmigrant status."
> — INA Section 214(b)

For E-2 applicants, the intent standard is slightly different from tourist
or student visas. The E-2 statute explicitly recognizes that the investor
intends to operate a business — and therefore allows for **indefinite
renewability** of the visa. However, the applicant must still demonstrate
that they will **depart when the visa expires or is no longer valid.**

> "The applicant must clearly demonstrate that at the time of application
> for E status, they have the present intention to depart the United States
> upon the expiration or termination of E status."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-4(B)

## What Officers Actually Ask

The officer is not looking for a promise. They are evaluating objective
circumstances to determine whether departure is plausible. The key question is:

**"Does this person have ties to their home country strong enough to compel
their eventual return — or have they structured their life to permanently
remain in the U.S.?"**

The officer considers the totality of the evidence. No single factor is
automatically disqualifying (except outright evidence of immigrant intent
such as a pending immigrant petition).

## Ties That Support Non-Immigrant Intent

### Strongest Ties (Most Persuasive)

**Property ownership in Canada:**
- Owning a home in Canada that you have not sold
- Owning investment or rental property in Canada
- This demonstrates an ongoing financial stake in Canada

**Family in Canada:**
- A spouse or children remaining in Canada
- Elderly parents you are responsible for
- Note: Family traveling with you to the U.S. is not disqualifying —
  the E-2 explicitly allows dependants to accompany the investor

**Active financial interests in Canada:**
- A Canadian business you continue to own (even if managed by others)
- Active Canadian investment accounts, pension plans
- Canadian real estate generating rental income

**Professional and community ties:**
- Canadian professional license or membership (e.g., CPA, P.Eng.)
- Long-standing memberships in Canadian organizations
- Ongoing consulting relationships with Canadian clients

### Moderate Ties

- Canadian bank accounts and credit cards
- RRSP and TFSA accounts (but these alone are not strong)
- A Canadian driver's licence (maintained, not surrendered)
- Family members (non-dependant) remaining in Canada

### Ties Officers Weigh Skeptically

- Vague references to "returning to Canada someday"
- Prior U.S. visa history showing long stays
- A history of multiple nonimmigrant visa applications
- Applicant age close to retirement (suggests no career-based reason to return)

## Actions That Create Immigrant Intent Risk

### High Risk — Can Trigger 214(b) Denial

**Selling your Canadian home before the visa interview:**
Eliminating your most significant Canadian tie before approval signals
that you have already made an irrevocable decision to relocate permanently.
See `kb/canadian-property-sale-timing.md`.

**Pending immigrant petition (I-130, I-140, I-526E):**
If you or a family member has filed an immigrant visa petition on your
behalf, this is near-conclusive evidence of immigrant intent.
> "An approved immigrant visa petition in itself is not a bar to issuance
> of a nonimmigrant visa, but it is a strong indicator of an intention to
> remain permanently in the U.S."
> — 9 FAM 302.1-2(B)(4)

**Applying for a Social Security Number before the interview:**
While an SSN is eventually needed for employment and tax purposes, applying
before your visa is approved can be cited as evidence that you have already
taken steps to permanently integrate into U.S. society.

**Closing all Canadian financial accounts:**
Liquidating all Canadian bank accounts and investments simultaneously with
the visa application suggests you have no intention of maintaining ties.

**Listing a U.S. address as your "home" in application materials:**
You must list your Canadian address as your home address on the DS-160.
Using a U.S. address signals you have already moved.

### Moderate Risk — Requires Good Explanation

**Enrolling children in U.S. schools before visa approval:**
This can be explained as preparatory (you are planning to get the visa),
but do so carefully. Do not enroll children and then represent that you
are still uncertain about relocation.

**Applying for a U.S. driver's licence immediately upon entering the U.S.:**
Normal and expected — this is not a problem at the interview stage.

**Having a U.S. bank account:**
You may need a U.S. bank account to receive funds for the business. Having
one is not disqualifying — it is the combination with other factors that creates risk.

## The "Dual Intent" Issue — Is E-2 a Dual Intent Visa?

E-2 is **not a dual intent visa** in the same sense as H-1B or L-1.
Those visas have statutory provisions explicitly allowing immigrant intent.
E-2 does not.

However, the FAM acknowledges the practical reality:

> "The fact that an alien may be classifiable as both an immigrant and as
> a nonimmigrant at the same time does not preclude the issuance of a
> nonimmigrant visa."
> — 9 FAM 302.1-2(B)

In practice, many E-2 holders eventually pursue immigrant status (often
through EB-1C, EB-5, or National Interest Waiver). This is not automatically
disqualifying. What matters is that **at the time of the E-2 application,**
the applicant has a genuine intent to depart when the visa expires.

The key is: if you have an **active, pending immigrant petition** at the
time of your E-2 application, this is a significant problem. If you simply
have a long-term aspiration to eventually become a permanent resident through
legal channels, this does not automatically disqualify you — but should not
be volunteered in the interview.

## Responding to 214(b) Concerns in the Interview

If the officer appears to be probing immigrant intent, your responses should:

1. **Be honest** — officers detect coached or dishonest answers
2. **Reference your specific Canadian ties** — name the property, the family
   members, the financial accounts
3. **Demonstrate your understanding that the E-2 requires departure** —
   show you understand this is a nonimmigrant visa
4. **Do not promise you will never apply for a green card** — this is
   impossible to credibly promise and may raise further questions

Strong answer framework:
> "My family [or: my wife and children] will be relocating with me. We
> continue to own our home in [city], which we are renting out and intend
> to return to. I have significant financial investments in Canada, including
> [RRSPs, rental property, business interests]. My intention is to operate
> this business under E-2 status and evaluate our options as the visa
> allows — but I understand this is a nonimmigrant visa and plan accordingly."

## What to Maintain in Canada After Moving

To protect your non-immigrant intent position at renewal and in subsequent
applications:

| Asset/Tie | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Canadian home | Keep (rent it out) if financially feasible |
| Canadian bank accounts | Maintain active accounts |
| RRSP/TFSA | Keep — do not collapse unless tax-efficient to do so |
| Canadian investment accounts | Maintain |
| Canadian professional memberships | Renew annually if applicable |
| Canadian driver's licence | May lapse naturally — not required to maintain |
| Provincial health insurance | Will lapse automatically — not required to maintain |
