## Why Franchises Work Well for E-2 Applications

Franchises have become a dominant vehicle for E-2 visa applications among
Canadian investors for several well-founded reasons:

**1. Proven non-marginality evidence**
The Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) — required by the FTC for all
U.S. franchisors — contains Item 19 (Financial Performance Representations).
When an established franchisor includes Item 19 data, it provides documented,
auditable revenue and earnings figures from existing franchise locations.
This gives the consular officer verifiable evidence that the business model
is capable of generating more than a minimal living.

**2. Defined investment range**
The FDD Item 7 contains a detailed Total Initial Investment table specifying
the minimum and maximum cost to open the franchise. This gives the officer
a clear, franchisor-certified cost baseline against which to evaluate the
proportionality of your investment.

**3. Training and support structure**
Franchises come with operating systems, training programs, and ongoing
support. This helps establish that even an investor without prior experience
in the specific industry can operate the business — reinforcing the
develop and direct requirement.

**4. Established brand and customer base**
A franchise with an established brand has a lower proof burden for business
viability than an entirely novel concept. The officer can evaluate an
existing franchise system's track record rather than relying solely on
projections for an untested business model.

## The FDD — What E-2 Applicants Must Review

The **Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD)** is a legally mandated document
that franchisors must provide to prospective franchisees at least 14 calendar
days before signing any agreement or paying any fee. (FTC Franchise Rule,
16 CFR Part 436)

Key FDD Items for E-2 purposes:

| FDD Item | Content | E-2 Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Item 5 | Initial franchise fees | Part of your qualifying investment amount |
| Item 7 | Estimated initial investment | Defines "cost of the business" for proportionality test |
| Item 8 | Restrictions on sources of products/services | Affects operating costs and margins |
| Item 19 | Financial Performance Representations | Primary non-marginality evidence — use only if disclosed |
| Item 20 | Outlets and franchisee information | Contact list of existing franchisees for due diligence |
| Item 21 | Financial statements | Franchisor's audited financials — confirms brand is financially sound |

**Critically:** If the franchisor does NOT include Item 19 data, you cannot
rely on their FDD to support financial projections. You must develop your
own market-based financial analysis. A missing Item 19 is a significant
red flag for E-2 purposes — it means the franchisor is unwilling to
disclose financial performance, often because it is poor.

## Franchise Categories — E-2 Compatibility Assessment

### Strong E-2 Compatibility ✅

These categories have characteristics that align well with E-2 requirements:
significant investment, established revenue potential, employee-driven model,
and clear owner-operator management structure.

**Food Service (Full Service Restaurants)**
- Investment range: $300,000–$1.5M+ depending on concept and location
- Clear owner-operator role
- Multiple employees from day one
- Item 19 commonly disclosed by major brands
- Examples: established regional and national sit-down restaurant concepts
- Note: Fast food (QSR) brands like McDonald's, Subway, Chick-fil-A
  typically do NOT accept E-2 visa applicants as franchisees

**Home Services / Residential Services**
- Investment range: $80,000–$300,000
- Service-based, territory-exclusive models
- Employee-driven (technicians, crews)
- Recurring revenue from service contracts
- Examples: cleaning, restoration, landscaping, painting, pest control

**Fitness and Wellness**
- Investment range: $200,000–$700,000
- Member-based recurring revenue model
- Multiple staff positions
- Strong Item 19 data from many brands

**Child and Senior Care Services**
- Investment range: $100,000–$500,000
- Strong social value / economic contribution argument
- Clear staffing requirements
- High demand in most markets

**Business Services and Staffing**
- Investment range: $100,000–$250,000
- B2B model — lower physical location cost
- Professional services that support the local economy
- Owner-operator in client development and operations management role

**Automotive Services**
- Investment range: $200,000–$800,000
- Equipment-intensive (high investment, good proportionality)
- Multiple technician positions
- Recurring customer base

### Moderate E-2 Compatibility ⚠️

These categories can work but require stronger business planning:

**Food Service (QSR / Fast Food)**
- Many top brands (McDonald's, Subway, Chick-fil-A) explicitly exclude
  E-2 visa applicants from franchisee eligibility
- Always confirm franchisee eligibility requirements with the franchisor
  before proceeding
- Mid-tier and emerging QSR brands often do accept E-2 applicants

**Retail Concepts**
- Highly location-dependent
- E-commerce disruption has weakened many retail franchise models
- Seasonal revenue can complicate projections
- Requires strong local market data

**Education and Tutoring**
- Lower investment threshold
- Need to demonstrate non-marginality carefully at lower investment levels
- Staffing plan is critical

### Weak E-2 Compatibility / Red Flags ❌

**Solo-Operator Service Concepts**
- Any franchise model where the franchisee IS the sole service provider
  (massage therapist, personal trainer, one-person cleaning service)
- These fail the marginality test — the business provides only a living
  for the investor
- Difficult to demonstrate the business can generate more than minimal
  living when all revenue depends on the investor's personal labor

**Kiosk / Vending Machine Models**
- Very low investment (may fail proportionality)
- No employees — fails economic contribution test
- Revenue entirely passive — may not satisfy develop and direct

**Consulting / Professional Services Franchises**
- If the franchisee IS the consultant, this is essentially a branded
  sole proprietorship
- Revenue depends on the investor's personal expertise and relationships
- Harder to demonstrate non-marginality and economic contribution

**Brands That Exclude E-2 Applicants**
Some high-profile franchise brands explicitly prohibit E-2 visa applicants
as franchisees, typically because:
- Their franchise agreement requires U.S. citizenship or permanent residency
- Their SBA loan requirements conflict with E-2 applicant status

Always confirm with the franchisor directly that they accept E-2 applicants
before proceeding. Ask specifically: "Does your franchise agreement permit
non-immigrant visa holders to become franchisees?"

## SBA Franchise Directory — What It Means for E-2

The **SBA Franchise Directory** (sba.gov) lists franchise brands eligible
for SBA-guaranteed loans.

> "The SBA Franchise Directory contains all franchises and other brands
> eligible for SBA financial assistance."
> — SBA.gov

**Relevance to E-2:**
- SBA loans are not typically available to E-2 visa holders (SBA lending
  generally requires U.S. citizenship or permanent residency)
- However, SBA franchise directory listing signals that the franchise model
  has been reviewed and found to have a legitimate, non-affiliated owner
  structure — which is relevant to E-2 eligibility
- Being on the SBA directory is not a requirement for E-2, but it is a
  positive indicator of a legitimate franchise structure

## Due Diligence Before Signing a Franchise Agreement

1. **Obtain and read the full FDD** — particularly Items 5, 7, 19, 20, 21
2. **Confirm the franchisor accepts E-2 applicants** — in writing
3. **Contact existing franchisees** (Item 20 contact list) — ask about
   actual revenue and profitability, not just the franchisor's numbers
4. **Engage a franchise attorney** to review the franchise agreement before
   signing (separate from your immigration attorney)
5. **Engage your immigration attorney** to confirm the franchise is
   structurally compatible with E-2 requirements
6. **Confirm the investment amount** (Item 7 total) will satisfy the
   proportionality test given your planned investment
7. **Understand the territory** — is it exclusive? What market does it cover?
8. **Understand royalty and marketing fee obligations** — these are ongoing
   operating costs that reduce net income
