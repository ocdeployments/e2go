# E‑2 Partnerships and Non‑Typical Ownership Structures

*Scope: How 9 FAM 402.9 treats partnerships, joint ventures, diffuse ownership, and other non‑standard E‑2 investor scenarios, based only on official sources (9 FAM and consular websites).*  
*This is not legal advice; edge cases should be reviewed with qualified U.S. immigration counsel.*

---

## 1. Who can be an E‑2 “investor” under 9 FAM

9 FAM 402.9 provides that a **treaty investor may be an individual, partnership, or corporate entity** that possesses the nationality of a treaty country.[web:107][web:63]

For organizations, at least **50 percent of the business must be owned by nationals of the treaty country** for the company to qualify as a treaty enterprise.[web:63]

Thus, partnerships and joint ventures are permissible investor vehicles so long as treaty‑national ownership and control requirements are satisfied.

---

## 2. Joint ventures and equal partnerships

### 2.1 Two‑party 50/50 partnerships

9 FAM recognizes that a **joint venture or equal partnership between two parties** can meet the E‑2 “control” requirement:

> “As indicated, a joint venture or an equal partnership involving two parties, could constitute control for E‑2 purposes. However, in such a case, the joint venture or partnership must demonstrate that both parties have equal shares in the enterprise and that both parties have full management rights and responsibilities.”[web:63]

This embraces the idea of **“negative control”**: a 50/50 two‑party partnership can qualify if both partners (who must be treaty nationals for E‑2 purposes) hold equal investment and full management rights.

### 2.2 Equal partnerships with more than two partners

9 FAM then draws a clear line for more complex equal partnerships:

> “An equal partnership with more than two partners would not give any of the parties control based on ownership, as the element of control would be too remote even under the negative control theory.”[web:63]

In those cases, no individual partner can establish control **based solely on ownership**. The enterprise might still qualify as an E‑2 treaty enterprise if treaty‑national ownership thresholds are met, but individual visas will generally be as **E‑2 employees** rather than “treaty investors.”[web:63]

---

## 3. Diffuse or layered ownership and control

### 3.1 Treaty‑national ownership threshold

9 FAM 402.9‑4(B) requires that nationals of a treaty country **own at least 50 percent of the U.S. enterprise** for it to have that country’s E‑2 “nationality.”[web:63]

If ownership is diffuse or layered across entities, the applicant must prove that:

- Treaty‑country nationals **collectively own at least 50 percent** of the ultimate equity; and
- As a group, they **have the ability to develop and direct the U.S. enterprise**.[web:63]

### 3.2 Consequences for individual classification

9 FAM explains that when no single person or entity clearly controls the enterprise, **no one can be classified as the “investor”**:

> “If the nationals of a treaty country own 50 percent of a business and together they have the ability to develop and direct the U.S. enterprise, then E visas may be issued to the nationals of that treaty country who are coming to fill executive, supervisory, or essential skills positions, even though no single person or company owns 50 percent. However, in such a case, no individual may receive an E visa as the principal treaty investor.”[web:63]

In practice, this means that with **diffuse ownership**:

- The company may still be treated as a **treaty enterprise**.
- Individuals from that company obtain **E‑2 visas as employees (executive/supervisory or specialized‑skills)**, not as E‑2 investors.

---

## 4. Control through management, not just equity

9 FAM also recognizes that **control can be exercised via management rather than majority equity ownership**:

> “Control of the enterprise may be demonstrated by ownership of at least 50 percent of the business, by possession of operational control through a managerial position or other corporate device, or by other means.”[web:63]

Accordingly, an investor who does not hold more than 50 percent of the equity may still satisfy the “develop and direct” requirement if they can prove:

- A **managerial or executive role** conferring decision‑making authority; or
- Contractual or corporate‑governance rights (e.g., veto rights, board control, special voting shares) that effectively give them control of the enterprise.

However, the burden is on the applicant to document these rights in detail (e.g., operating agreements, shareholder agreements, board resolutions) and explain them in the E‑2 cover letter.

---

## 5. Multiple treaty nationalities and mixed ownership

9 FAM 402.9‑4(B) addresses scenarios where owners hold **different treaty nationalities or dual nationality**:[web:63]

- A business can generally have **only one E‑visa nationality**, determined by the nationality of those who own at least 50 percent of it.  
- **Dual‑national owners** must choose which nationality they will use for E‑visa purposes, and **all E‑2 employees must share the E‑visa nationality** of the enterprise.[web:63]
- If a company is **equally owned and controlled (50/50) by nationals of two different treaty countries**, E‑2 visas “may be issued to employees of either treaty country.”[web:63]

Ownership by U.S. permanent residents or non‑treaty nationals **does not count** toward the treaty‑national ownership threshold, even if those individuals also have the treaty nationality; what matters is their status and nationality as used for immigration purposes.[web:63]

For partnerships, this means your cap‑table analysis must:

- Identify **nationality and U.S. immigration status** of each partner.  
- Determine whether treaty‑national partners collectively meet the **50 percent ownership** threshold under 9 FAM.  
- Decide which nationality is designated as the company’s **E‑visa nationality**, especially where owners hold multiple citizenships.

---

## 6. Non‑profit, passive, and other non‑qualifying structures

9 FAM underscores that some structures cannot support E‑2 **investor** classification even if they involve partnerships or corporate entities:

- **Non‑profit organizations** are explicitly excluded: “E‑2 investor status must not, therefore, be extended to non‑profit organizations.”[web:63]
- **Purely passive investments**—such as un‑developed land or stock held for appreciation—do not meet the “real and operating commercial enterprise” requirement in 9 FAM 402.9‑6(C).[web:63]
- Where an owner does not personally **develop and direct** the business (for example, a silent limited partner with no management authority), they may not qualify as a “treaty investor,” though they may sometimes qualify as a **treaty employee** if they will actually perform executive/supervisory or essential‑skills duties in the United States.[web:63]

These rules apply equally to partnerships, LLCs taxed as partnerships, and corporations.

---

## 7. Nonimmigrant intent and prior U.S. immigration history

While not unique to partnerships, 9 FAM also requires that **E‑2 applicants intend to depart the United States when their E‑2 status ends**.[web:21]

State Department E‑visa FAQs explicitly list this as a separate qualification:  
“Applicant intends to depart the United States when the E‑1/E‑2 status terminates (see 9 FAM 402.9‑4(C)).”[web:21]

Official consular instructions for some posts also require applicants to provide **prior U.S. immigration history** (copies of prior visas, I‑94s, and change‑of‑status approvals), which can be especially relevant in complex partnership situations where multiple entities or status changes are involved.[web:92]

## 8. Practical implications for platform design

Based solely on official sources, a preparation platform that handles partnerships and non‑typical E‑2 cases should:

1. **Capture detailed ownership structure**  
   - Number of owners, their ownership percentages, and whether any equal partnership involves more than two parties.[web:63]

2. **Record each owner’s nationality and U.S. immigration status**  
   - So you can test the 50 percent treaty‑national ownership rule and determine the enterprise’s E‑visa nationality.[web:63]

3. **Identify who actually controls and manages the enterprise**  
   - Distinguish between owners who merely invest and those who hold managerial positions or special control rights.[web:63]

4. **Route some owners to “E‑2 employee” logic instead of “investor” logic**  
   - Especially in diffuse‑ownership situations or equal partnerships with more than two partners, consistent with 9 FAM’s requirement that only someone with control can be the treaty investor.[web:63]

5. **Screen out non‑profit and purely passive structures early**  
   - Because these cannot support E‑2 investor classification under 9 FAM 402.9‑6.[web:63]

6. **Explicitly address nonimmigrant intent and prior immigration history**  
   - Since 9 FAM 402.9‑4(C) and consular FAQs treat intent to depart as an independent requirement.[web:21]

Designing your flows around these official rules will help ensure that partnership and “non‑typical” ownership cases are framed in a way that aligns with 9 FAM and consular expectations, even before you layer on any practitioner‑level best practices.
