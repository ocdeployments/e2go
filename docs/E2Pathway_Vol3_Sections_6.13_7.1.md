## 6.13 What the Consulate Officer Is Actually Looking For

### Purpose of This Section
This section powers the interview preparation module, the confidence scoring
engine, and the cover letter generator's framing logic. It is built from
documented Toronto consulate experiences, attorney reports, 9 FAM 402.9
adjudication guidance, and real applicant accounts. Understanding how an
officer reads a package — and what triggers doubt — is the difference between
a 10-minute approval and a 221(g) suspension.

---

### The Officer's Mental Checklist

A Toronto consular officer reviewing an E-2 application is mentally working
through five questions in this order:

**Question 1: Is this person a Canadian citizen from a treaty country?**
This is the easiest check — passport confirms it. No complexity here for
Canadian applicants.

**Question 2: Is this a real business — not a shell or a scheme?**
The officer is looking for evidence the business exists beyond paper:
- Signed lease or property purchase
- Business bank account opened
- EIN obtained
- Licenses applied for or granted
- Equipment purchased or ordered
- Franchise agreement executed (if franchise)
- Photos of the physical location

A business that exists only as an LLC registration and a business plan is
not a real operating enterprise. The officer wants to see money spent,
contracts signed, and operations in motion.

**Question 3: Is the investment substantial and at risk?**
The officer checks:
- Total amount invested vs. total business cost (proportionality)
- Whether funds are actually in the business (wire confirmations, receipts)
- Whether funds are truly at risk — not sitting in escrow or recoverable
- Whether the source of funds is documented and credible

An officer who cannot trace the investment from its origin to the business
account will issue a 221(g) or deny.

**Question 4: Will this business generate more than a marginal living?**
The officer reviews the business plan for:
- Year 1–5 revenue projections vs. the investor's household income need
- Hiring milestones — will the business create U.S. jobs?
- Market research supporting the revenue assumptions
- Competition analysis demonstrating the investor has done their homework
- Prior business experience suggesting the projections are credible

A business projecting $80,000/year net for a family of 4 fails this test.
The projections must show 3–4x household income AND a hiring plan.

**Question 5: Does this person intend to return to Canada?**
The nonimmigrant intent requirement means the officer is looking for:
- Canadian ties maintained (home, family, bank accounts, health coverage)
- No evidence of abandoning Canadian residency before the visa is granted
- Answers suggesting the investor sees the E-2 as temporary (renewable, yes,
  but with the intention of eventual return or at minimum no permanent settlement intent)

This is the most subjective question and the hardest to prepare for because
it lives in the interview, not the package.

---

### How an Officer Reads the Package

**Before the interview:**
Officers review the package before the applicant arrives at the window. The
order in which they typically review tabs:

1. Tab A (DS-160) — quick overview: who is this person, what visa category,
   what business
2. Tab D (Cover Letter) — the officer's primary narrative guide; if the cover
   letter is clear, organized, and answers the five questions above, the officer
   is already favorably disposed before opening any other tab
3. Tab K (Business Plan) — checks the financial projections for reasonableness
   and the market research for credibility
4. Tab F/H (Investment Proof / Source of Funds) — traces the money; this is
   where most officers spend the most time looking for gaps
5. Tab G (Business is Real) — confirms operational evidence
6. Tab J (Qualifications) — checks whether the investor has relevant experience
7. Tabs B, C, E, L — supporting/personal documents; reviewed briefly

**Implication for app content generation:**
The cover letter is the most important document in the package. It must be
structured to answer all five officer questions in the first two pages. The
app's cover letter generator is built around this structure explicitly.

### What Triggers a 221(g) vs. an On-the-Spot Approval

**On-the-spot approvals occur when:**
- The cover letter answers all five questions clearly
- The source of funds chronology is complete with no gaps
- The investment is clearly at risk (receipts, contracts, bank statements)
- The business plan projections are realistic and supported by market data
- The investor can answer every question about their own business confidently
- The officer finds no reason to doubt the legitimacy of any element

**221(g) administrative processing is typically triggered by:**
- Missing source of funds documentation for a specific deposit or asset sale
- Business plan projections that appear unrealistic without explanation
- Lease or franchise agreement not yet executed (business "not in process")
- Prior U.S. immigration issues flagged during processing
- Security or background checks (administrative processing — no documents
  requested; the applicant simply waits)
- Local market research missing for the specific city/county
- Questions about the business model that require additional evidence

**Outright refusals are most commonly triggered by:**
- 214(b) — immigrant intent (selling Canadian home before visa approval;
  surrendering all Canadian ties; answers suggesting permanent intent)
- Investment clearly not substantial (amount too low, poorly documented)
- Business is marginal (projections too low, no hiring plan, solo operator)
- Source of funds contains unexplained cash deposits or unclear origins
- Business is in a disqualifying category (passive real estate, cannabis)

### The Interview Itself — What to Expect at Toronto

**Physical setup:**
- Interview takes place at a window — not a private room
- The officer has a glass partition between them and the applicant
- They are looking at their screen (reviewing the file) while asking questions
- They may type notes while the applicant is speaking

**Typical duration:**
- Straightforward approvals: 5–10 minutes
- More complex cases or cases requiring clarification: 10–20 minutes
- Cases that result in 221(g): may be cut short once the officer identifies
  the issue — they will explain what additional documents are needed

**The officer's approach:**
Based on documented Toronto experiences, Toronto officers typically:
- Ask an open-ended overview question first ("Tell me about your business")
- Follow with specific questions about investment and funds
- Ask about the hiring plan
- Ask about the investor's specific role
- May ask about competition or market ("Why this location?")
- Rarely ask highly technical legal questions — they are more interested in
  whether the investor knows their own business

**What the officer is observing:**
Beyond the answers themselves, the officer observes:
- Does the investor speak about the business with genuine knowledge and confidence?
- Does the investor hesitate when asked about financial figures? (suggests they
  did not prepare their own package)
- Are the answers consistent with what is written in the package?
- Does the investor seem like someone who will actually run this business?

**App implication — the interview simulator:**
The simulator must train users to speak about their business from memory —
not to recite numbers they memorized the night before. The coaching feedback
after each answer should assess: "Does this answer sound like someone who built
this business plan themselves?"

### The Cover Letter — Structure That Wins Approvals

The cover letter is the officer's roadmap. A cover letter that is clear,
complete, and addresses all five officer questions typically results in an
interview of under 10 minutes — because the officer has no outstanding questions.

**Winning cover letter structure (mirroring the officer's five questions):**

**Paragraph 1 — Introduction and Treaty Eligibility**
Who the applicant is, Canadian citizenship, E-2 treaty basis, overview of
the business in one sentence.

**Paragraph 2 — The Business (Real and Operating)**
What the business does, where it is located, current operational status
(lease signed, licenses applied for, equipment ordered), business entity details.

**Paragraph 3 — The Investment (Substantial and At Risk)**
Total investment amount in USD, how funds are allocated (franchise fee /
build-out / working capital / equipment / professional fees), wire transfer
dates, confirmation funds are at risk.

**Paragraph 4 — Source and Path of Funds**
Brief summary of where the investment funds came from (sale of property,
savings, prior business proceeds) — with reference to Tab H for the full
chronological trail.

**Paragraph 5 — Non-Marginality**
Year 1 revenue projection and household income need comparison, Year 3
and Year 5 projections, hiring plan with specific employee counts and timeline.

**Paragraph 6 — The Investor's Role (Develop and Direct)**
The investor's specific title, specific responsibilities, specific decisions
they will make and operations they will oversee. This paragraph must make
it clear the investor is an active manager — not a passive investor.

**Paragraph 7 — Investor Qualifications**
Relevant experience, education, prior business ownership, industry knowledge
that makes this investor credible to operate this specific business.

**Paragraph 8 — Non-Immigrant Intent**
Canadian ties maintained: home status, Canadian bank accounts kept open,
family in Canada, provincial health coverage maintained until U.S. coverage
active, acknowledgment that E-2 is a nonimmigrant visa.

**Closing — Summary and Request**
Brief closing requesting the E-2 visa, reference to the supporting tabs.

**App implementation:**
The cover letter generator produces all eight paragraphs from the user's
interview engine answers. No paragraph is generated from generic templates —
every sentence references the user's specific data: their name, their business
name, their investment amount, their hiring plan, their specific role.

## 7.1 Eligibility Quiz — Module 1 Specification

### Purpose of This Module
Module 1 is the first thing a user does after creating their account. It
determines whether the user is eligible for the E-2 visa, routes them to
the correct application path, and collects the foundational profile data
that pre-populates all subsequent modules. It also serves as the first
quality gate — users who clearly do not qualify are told this immediately
with alternative options, rather than completing a full application only
to be disqualified.

---

### Module 1 — Design Principles

1. **Never waste a user's time.** Disqualifying questions come first. If a
   user is ineligible, they find out in question 3, not question 47.
2. **Every question has a purpose.** No question is asked unless the answer
   affects either eligibility, routing, or subsequent module pre-population.
3. **Plain language throughout.** No legal jargon in the questions. The
   tooltip/explainer for each question provides legal context for users who
   want it.
4. **Progressive disclosure.** Questions are grouped into short sections
   (3–5 questions each) so the user sees progress and does not feel overwhelmed.
5. **Branching is invisible to the user.** The user sees a smooth, linear
   experience. The branching happens in the background.

---

### Question Set — Full Specification

**SECTION A — Treaty Eligibility (Questions 1–2)**

Q1: "Are you a citizen of Canada?"
- Yes → continue
- No → [DISQUALIFY SOFT] Show: "The E-2 visa is available to citizens of
  treaty countries. Canada qualifies — but the visa requires Canadian
  citizenship, not just Canadian permanent residency. If you hold citizenship
  in another treaty country, you may still qualify. [See the full list of
  treaty countries →] If you are a Canadian permanent resident but not a
  citizen, the E-2 is not available to you through Canada."
- Tooltip: "The E-2 visa requires citizenship in a treaty country — not just
  residency. Canadian permanent residents who are not citizens do not qualify
  through Canada, even if they have lived in Canada for many years."

Q2: "Do you hold a valid Canadian passport?"
- Yes → continue
- No, but I am a Canadian citizen → advisory: "You will need a valid Canadian
  passport for your interview at the Toronto consulate. Please ensure your
  passport is valid for at least 6 months beyond your intended entry date.
  You can continue your application while your passport renewal is in process."
- No, I am not a Canadian citizen → route to treaty country check

---

**SECTION B — Investment Readiness (Questions 3–5)**

Q3: "Do you have funds available to invest in a U.S. business?"
- Yes → continue
- No, I am planning to raise funds → advisory: "The E-2 investment must come
  from your own funds — not from investors, crowdfunding, or business revenue.
  The funds must be lawfully yours and traceable to their origin. If you are
  planning to raise capital from others, please review the source of funds
  requirements before proceeding."
- I'm not sure → explain substantiality concept, continue

Q4: "Approximately how much do you have available to invest in USD?"
- Under $50,000 → [DISQUALIFY SOFT] Show: "The E-2 visa requires a 'substantial'
  investment relative to the total cost of the business. Most Toronto consulate
  approvals involve a minimum of $75,000–$100,000 USD. An investment below
  $50,000 USD faces very high scrutiny and is difficult to approve for most
  business types. We recommend consulting with an E-2 attorney before proceeding.
  [Connect with an attorney →]" — user can override and continue at their own risk
- $50,000–$75,000 → warning displayed, user can continue
- $75,000–$100,000 → continue with investment threshold advisory
- $100,000–$150,000 → continue
- $150,000–$250,000 → continue
- $250,000–$500,000 → continue
- $500,000+ → continue (flag for premium business categories)

Q5: "Can you document the source of these funds — where they came from originally?"
- Yes, from savings over time → continue; source_type = "savings"
- Yes, from the sale of a property → continue; source_type = "property_sale"
- Yes, from a prior business sale → continue; source_type = "business_sale"
- Yes, from an inheritance or gift → continue; source_type = "inheritance";
  advisory: "Inherited or gifted funds require additional documentation showing
  the origin of the gift and the relationship between donor and recipient."
- Yes, from an RRSP withdrawal → continue; source_type = "rrsp";
  advisory: "RRSP withdrawals as a non-resident attract a 25% withholding tax
  (reduced to 15% under the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty in some cases). A cross-border
  tax specialist should review this before you withdraw."
- I'm not sure I can fully document the source → advisory displayed:
  "The source of funds chronology is one of the most scrutinized elements of
  the E-2 application. If you cannot trace the origin of your investment funds
  with bank statements, tax returns, and supporting documents, this may be a
  significant challenge. Continue your application and our source of funds
  template will show you exactly what documentation you need."
- Tooltip: "The consulate requires a complete, documented trail of your
  investment funds — from where the money was originally earned or received,
  through every account it passed through, to its final deployment in the
  U.S. business. Gaps in this trail are the most common reason for 221(g)
  administrative suspensions."

---

**SECTION C — Business Readiness (Questions 6–8)**

Q6: "Do you have a specific business in mind for your E-2 application?"
- Yes, I have a specific business identified → continue; business_status = "identified"
- I have a general idea but not a specific business → continue; business_status = "exploring"
- No — I need help finding the right business → continue; business_status = "needs_guidance";
  flag for franchise broker referral in Module 2

Q7: "How are you planning to acquire or establish the business?"
- Buying a franchise (new territory) → route = "franchise"
- Buying an existing operating business → route = "acquisition"
- Starting a new business from scratch → route = "greenfield"
- Investing in a partner's existing business → route = "partner_investment"
- Not sure yet → route = "undecided"; show business type advisor in Module 2

Q8: "Will you have a business partner who is also applying for an E-2 visa?"
- No, I am the sole investor → application_type = "solo"
- Yes, I have one partner who will also apply → application_type = "partnership";
  advisory: "Partnership applications require each partner to independently
  meet all E-2 requirements. Each partner must own exactly 50% of the business
  and have their own documented source of funds. We'll generate two complete
  application packages from your shared business data."
- Yes, I have a partner but they are a U.S. citizen or resident → application_type = "solo_with_us_partner";
  advisory: "A U.S. citizen or permanent resident partner does not need an E-2
  visa. Only you will be applying. However, the business ownership structure
  must give you at least 50% ownership or documented operational control."

---

**SECTION D — Current Status & Location (Questions 9–11)**

Q9: "Where are you currently located?"
- In Canada → location = "canada"; route to consular path (Toronto)
- In the United States on a valid visa → location = "usa"; route to COS advisory
- Outside Canada and the U.S. → location = "other"; advisory about consular
  options; typically still routes to Toronto if Canadian passport

Q10: (If location = "usa") "What visa status are you currently on in the U.S.?"
- B-1/B-2 Visitor Visa → cos_eligible = true; advisory about COS path
- TN Visa → cos_eligible = true
- L-1 Visa → cos_eligible = true
- F-1 Student Visa → cos_eligible = conditional; flag for attorney referral
- Other → flag for attorney referral
- Tooltip: "If you are currently in the U.S. on a valid nonimmigrant status,
  you have the option to change your status to E-2 without returning to Canada.
  This is called Change of Status (COS) and is filed with USCIS. We will ask
  you which path you prefer in the next step."

Q11: (If location = "usa") "Would you prefer to apply for E-2 through:"
- Change of Status (USCIS) — stay in the U.S. during processing → route = "cos"
- Consular processing (Toronto) — travel to Canada for interview → route = "consular"
- Advisory for each option displayed side by side (5-year stamp vs. 2-year increment,
  interview vs. no interview, travel restriction vs. no restriction)

---

**SECTION E — Family Composition (Questions 12–14)**

Q12: "Are you married or in a common-law partnership?"
- Yes → has_spouse = true
- No → has_spouse = false

Q13: (If has_spouse = true) "Will your spouse be joining you in the U.S. on E-2 status?"
- Yes → spouse_applying = true; advisory about spouse EAD (Employment Authorization Document)
- No → spouse_applying = false

Q14: "Do you have dependent children who will be joining you in the U.S.?"
- No → has_dependents = false
- Yes, 1 child → dependent_count = 1; collect child's age
- Yes, 2 children → dependent_count = 2; collect ages
- Yes, 3 or more children → dependent_count = 3+; collect ages

**Age-out alert (triggered if any child is 17 or older):**
> "⚠️ Important: Your child [name/age] will be [age] at your expected interview
> date. E-2 dependent status ends on a child's 21st birthday. If your child
> will be 21 or older before you achieve permanent residency, you should begin
> planning a separate immigration pathway for them now. Our compliance calendar
> will track this deadline and alert you when action is required."

---

**SECTION F — Immigrant Intent Assessment (Questions 15–17)**

Q15: "Do you currently own or rent a home in Canada?"
- Own a home → canadian_home = "own"
- Rent → canadian_home = "rent"
- Neither (living with family, etc.) → canadian_home = "none";
  advisory: "Maintaining ties to Canada — including housing — helps demonstrate
  non-immigrant intent. If you have no housing tie to Canada, your cover letter
  and interview preparation should address other Canadian ties (family, bank
  accounts, business interests)."

Q16: "Do you plan to sell your Canadian home before your E-2 interview?"
- No, I will keep it (rent it out or maintain it) → immigrant_intent_risk = "low"
- Yes, I plan to sell before the interview → immigrant_intent_risk = "high";
  alert: "⚠️ Selling your Canadian home before your E-2 interview is one of the
  most common triggers for a 214(b) immigrant intent denial. Consular officers
  view the sale of your primary residence as evidence that you do not intend to
  return to Canada. We strongly recommend maintaining your Canadian home (or
  at minimum a Canadian rental property) until after your visa is approved."
- I've already sold it → immigrant_intent_risk = "high"; same alert + advisory
  on how to mitigate with other Canadian ties

Q17: "Do you have immediate family members (parents, siblings, adult children)
      who are Canadian residents or citizens and will remain in Canada?"
- Yes → canadian_family_ties = true; immigrant_intent_risk modifier = reduced
- No → canadian_family_ties = false; note added to cover letter guidance:
  "Since you have limited family ties remaining in Canada, your cover letter
  should emphasize other Canadian connections: bank accounts, professional
  licenses, pension/retirement accounts, business interests, or community ties."

### Eligibility Quiz — Routing Outcomes

Based on the answers to all 17 questions, the app routes the user to one of
six outcomes:

**Outcome 1 — Full Approval Path (Consular, Solo)**
Criteria: Canadian citizen, $100K+ investment, documentable funds, solo investor,
based in Canada or willing to travel to Toronto
Action: Begin Module 2 (Business Type Advisor) → full consular application path

**Outcome 2 — Full Approval Path (Consular, Partnership)**
Criteria: As above + partnership confirmed
Action: Begin Module 2 with partnership flag → dual package generation activated

**Outcome 3 — Change of Status Path**
Criteria: Canadian citizen, currently in U.S. on valid status, elects COS
Action: Begin Module 2 with COS flag → I-129 package instead of DS-160 package;
USCIS timeline and requirements shown throughout

**Outcome 4 — Borderline / Proceed with Caution**
Criteria: Investment $50K–$75K, or funds partially undocumentable, or
immigrant intent risk flags present
Action: Display specific warnings for each flag; user can proceed but sees
risk advisory on every relevant screen; attorney referral recommended prominently

**Outcome 5 — Likely Ineligible (Soft Disqualify)**
Criteria: Not a Canadian citizen, investment under $50K, or business type
clearly disqualifying
Action: Explain why they likely do not qualify; provide alternative options
(LMIA work permit, EB-5, other investor visas); offer attorney referral;
do not prevent access but clearly communicate the challenge

**Outcome 6 — Attorney Referral Required**
Criteria: Complex status (F-1, previous immigration violations, unusual fund
source, already in removal proceedings)
Action: "Your situation has elements that go beyond what a preparation tool
can address. We strongly recommend consulting with an E-2 immigration attorney
before proceeding. [Connect with a vetted E-2 attorney →]"
User can still access the app but prominent advisory shown throughout.
