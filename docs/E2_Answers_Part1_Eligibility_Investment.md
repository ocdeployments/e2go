# PART 1 — BASIC ELIGIBILITY (Questions 1–25)

---

**Q1. What exactly is the E-2 visa and who qualifies for it?**

The E-2 Treaty Investor Visa allows citizens of countries that have a qualifying
commerce and navigation treaty with the United States to enter the U.S. to
develop and direct a business in which they have invested — or are actively in
the process of investing — a substantial amount of capital. It is a nonimmigrant
visa, meaning it does not directly confer permanent residency. To qualify, an
applicant must: (1) be a citizen of a treaty country; (2) have made or be making
a substantial investment; (3) be investing in a real, active, for-profit business;
(4) have funds that are genuinely at risk; (5) be entering solely to develop and
direct the enterprise; and (6) demonstrate non-immigrant intent (intent to
eventually depart).

**Q2. Which countries have a treaty with the US that allows the E-2 visa?**

As of 2024–2026, over 80 countries qualify for E-2 visa status under bilateral
treaties of commerce and navigation with the United States. Notable inclusions:
Canada (since January 1, 1994), the United Kingdom (since July 3, 1815),
Australia (since December 27, 1991), Germany, France, Japan, South Korea, Italy,
and many others. Notable exclusions include India, China (mainland), Brazil, and
Russia — citizens of those countries cannot currently obtain an E-2 visa.
Portugal was the most recently added country, gaining E-2 access on March 15,
2024.

**Source:**
- [DOS-Treaty] U.S. Department of State — Official Treaty Countries List
  https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/visa-information-resources/fees/treaty.html

**Q3. Does Canada have an E-2 treaty with the United States?**

Yes. Canada has held an E-2 treaty with the United States since January 1, 1994,
when the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA, now USMCA) came into force.
Canadian citizens — not just Canadian permanent residents — are eligible to apply
for the E-2 visa.

**Source:**
- [DOS-Treaty] U.S. Department of State — Official Treaty Countries List
  https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/visa-information-resources/fees/treaty.html

**Q4. Is the E-2 a work visa or an investor visa — what is the actual difference?**

The E-2 is classified as a nonimmigrant Treaty Investor visa — not a traditional
"work visa" in the sense of being sponsored by an employer. The key distinction
is that the E-2 holder is entering to own, manage, and operate their own
investment enterprise, not to be an employee. However, the E-2 does authorize
the holder to work — specifically in their own business. Employees sent by an
E-2 company can also obtain E-2 employee visas provided they have the same
nationality as the business owner and hold executive, supervisory, or essential
skills roles.

**Q5. Do I need to already own a business to apply, or can I start one from scratch?**

You do not need an existing business already in operation. However, you must
have made — or be actively in the process of making — a qualifying investment
before the visa is issued. In practice this means: your business does not need
to be fully operating, but funds must be irrevocably committed (e.g., a signed
lease, franchise agreement executed, equipment purchased, or funds transferred
to a US business bank account). A business that exists only as an LLC
registration and a business plan with no money committed is insufficient.

**Sources:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "Do I really have to invest the
  money before I apply?"

**Q6. Can I apply for the E-2 if I am not the sole owner of the business?**

Yes. You do not need to be the sole owner. However, you must own at least 50%
of the business to qualify as the E-2 principal investor. If two partners are
both applying for E-2 status, each must own exactly 50%. A 60/40 split means
the 40% partner cannot qualify for their own E-2. You must also demonstrate
that you actively develop and direct the enterprise — passive ownership is not
sufficient.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — Partnership Applications: The 50/50 Rule
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q7. I am Indian — can I ever qualify for an E-2 visa?**

Not currently through Indian citizenship. India does not have an E-2 qualifying
treaty with the United States. Indian nationals cannot apply for the E-2 visa
based on Indian citizenship alone. However, if an Indian national holds dual
citizenship from a qualifying treaty country (e.g., Canadian citizenship), they
may be able to use that second passport to apply.

**Source:**
- [DOS-Treaty] U.S. Department of State — Official Treaty Countries List
  (India does not appear on this list)
  https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/visa-information-resources/fees/treaty.html

**Q8. Can a person with dual citizenship use the treaty country passport even if
they live in a non-treaty country?**

Yes. The E-2 visa is based on citizenship, not residence. A person who holds
citizenship in a qualifying treaty country and citizenship in a non-treaty
country may use their treaty-country passport to apply for the E-2, regardless
of where they currently reside.

**Source:**
- [DOS-Treaty] U.S. Department of State — Treaty Countries (citizenship basis
  confirmed)
  https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/visa-information-resources/fees/treaty.html
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Eligibility Quiz, Question routing on
  dual citizenship

**Q9. Does the E-2 visa require me to live in the US full-time?**

There is no explicit full-time residency requirement written into the E-2
statute. However, as the principal investor, you must be actively developing
and directing the business. A purely absentee arrangement — where the investor
never enters the US and manages the business entirely remotely from abroad —
would undermine the "develop and direct" requirement. In practice, most E-2
holders maintain their primary residence in the US during the visa period.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "entering solely to develop and direct
  the enterprise"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q10. Is there a minimum percentage of the business I need to own to qualify?**

Yes. To qualify as the E-2 principal investor, you must own at least 50% of the
business. For partnerships where two co-investors both seek E-2 status, the
ownership must be exactly 50/50 — no more, no less for each party. If a partner
owns less than 50% (e.g., 40%), they cannot qualify for the E-2.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — The 50/50 Rule: "A 49/51 split
  disqualifies the minority partner."

---

**Q11. Can I apply for an E-2 to work as an employee in someone else's E-2
company?**

Yes. Employees of an E-2 company can also obtain E-2 visas, provided they: (1)
have the same nationality as the principal E-2 investor/company; (2) are
performing in executive or supervisory roles, OR possess skills essential to the
operation of the company; and (3) apply as an "employee" rather than as a
principal investor — meaning they do not need to meet the investment requirements
themselves.

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "I own an E-visa company and
  want to employ someone not in the U.S. What visa does my employee need?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q12. What does "develop and direct" the business actually mean in practice?**

"Develop and direct" means you must have operational control over the business —
setting policy, making major decisions, overseeing day-to-day management. It
requires the investor to have a real, active management role (e.g., as owner,
president, or CEO), not simply hold equity without involvement. At the Toronto
consulate interview, officers specifically assess whether the investor can speak
knowledgeably about their own business operations, hiring plan, and financial
decisions — because someone who is not actually running the business will not be
able to answer basic operational questions.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "Does the investor seem like someone who
  will actually run this business?"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q13. Can a manager or executive employee (not the owner) get an E-2?**

Yes — as an E-2 employee visa, not as a principal investor. Employees of a
qualifying E-2 enterprise may receive E-2 status if they: (1) have the same
nationality as the principal investor/company; and (2) serve in an executive
or supervisory capacity, or possess skills essential to the enterprise.

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "I own an E-visa company and
  want to employ someone not in the U.S."
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q14. Does the business have to be in operation before I apply, or just planned?**

The business does not need to be fully operational before you apply, but the
investment must be irrevocably committed. This means funds must be at risk in
the business before the visa is issued — not simply promised or planned. The
US Embassy Canada specifically states: "E-2 visa regulations require that the
funds be 'irrevocably committed' to the investment before the visa may be issued.
This requirement can be met by showing that your funds are already at risk. Funds
can also be considered to be irrevocably committed if they are held in an escrow
account contingent only on the issuance of an E-visa."

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "Do I really have to invest the
  money before I apply?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — Funds At Risk Requirement

**Q15. What makes a business "bona fide" for E-2 purposes?**

A bona fide business is one that: (1) is a real, operating, for-profit enterprise
— not a shell company or paper structure; (2) has a physical presence or
operational infrastructure beyond just an LLC registration; (3) offers goods or
services in genuine commerce. Evidence of a bona fide business includes: a
signed lease or property purchase, an open US business bank account, an EIN
(Employer Identification Number) obtained from the IRS, licenses applied for or
granted, equipment purchased or ordered, and a franchise agreement if applicable.
A business that exists only as a registered LLC with a business plan is not
considered bona fide.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — "Is this a real business, not a shell
  or scheme?"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q16. Can a non-profit or charity qualify for an E-2?**

No. The E-2 investment must be in a for-profit enterprise. Non-profit
organizations and charities do not qualify because they do not generate income
or profit in the commercial sense required by E-2 regulations.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "The enterprise must be a real, operating,
  for-profit business."

---

**Q17. Can a real estate investment company qualify for an E-2?**

Generally no — passive real estate investment (buying properties and collecting
rent without active management) is explicitly listed as a disqualifying category.
However, an active real estate services business (e.g., a property management
company, a real estate brokerage, or a real estate development company where
the investor is actively involved in operations) may qualify. The key distinction
is active vs. passive: the investment must be in a real operating enterprise, not
simply the holding of assets.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 and 6.13 — "disqualifying category: passive
  real estate"
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Module 2, Business Type Advisor

---

**Q18. Is an online business or e-commerce company eligible for the E-2?**

Yes, with important caveats. An online or e-commerce business can qualify for
the E-2 if it: (1) is a real operating enterprise with documented operations;
(2) is not a marginal business (i.e., has the capacity to generate more than a
living wage for the investor and ideally creates US jobs); and (3) requires the
investor's active management and direction. A home-based online business with
minimal investment and no employees faces greater scrutiny on the marginality
test.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — Marginality Test
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "How much money do I need to
  invest?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q19. Can a sole proprietorship qualify, or does it need to be a corporation
or LLC?**

The E-2 does not mandate a specific corporate structure. However, in practice,
most successful E-2 applications are filed through a US LLC or corporation, as
this provides clear documentation of ownership, investment, and business
operations. A sole proprietorship is harder to document for E-2 purposes and
is uncommon in approved applications.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Module 1 Question routing on business
  structure
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

**Q20. If I already have a TN visa, can I switch to E-2?**

Yes. Canadians currently in the US on a TN visa can apply for a Change of Status
to E-2 through USCIS using Form I-129 — if they have a qualifying E-2 business
and investment ready. They do not need to return to Canada first. Important note:
a Change of Status approval from USCIS gives you E-2 status (allows you to stay
and work in the US) but not a visa stamp. If you leave the US after a Change of
Status, you must apply for an E-2 visa stamp at the US Consulate in Toronto
before you can re-enter.

**Sources:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "I received a change of status
  from the USCIS. Is that all that I need in order to receive an E-visa? No."
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — Change of Status vs. Consular Processing
  table

**Q21. Is there an age requirement for the E-2 visa?**

There is no minimum or maximum age requirement for the principal E-2 investor.
For children applying as dependents: unmarried children under age 21 may
accompany or follow-to-join as E-2 dependents. Children under 14 years of age
are not required to appear in person for the interview at the US consulate in
Canada.

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "Do I have to come to a
  consulate to apply in person? ... applicants under 14 years of age do not need
  to appear in person."
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q22. Can I apply if I have a prior US visa denial on record?**

Yes, you can still apply. However, if you were previously found ineligible for
a US visa due to criminal convictions, immigration violations, drug charges, or
similar reasons, you will need to address the basis of that ineligibility. The
US Embassy Canada states: "Applicants requiring a waiver face a likely wait of
several months for a waiver to be processed by the U.S. Department of Homeland
Security. Even if the applicant satisfies the requirements for E-visa issuance,
there is no guarantee that a waiver of the ineligibility will be approved."

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "I have previously been found
  ineligible for a U.S. visa. Can I still qualify for an E-visa?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q23. Does having a criminal record disqualify me from the E-2?**

Not automatically, but it depends on the nature of the record. Certain criminal
convictions can trigger grounds of inadmissibility under US immigration law,
which would require a waiver of inadmissibility before an E-2 visa could be
issued. The US Embassy Canada notes applicants with criminal convictions "must
appear to determine their ineligibility and likelihood of a waiver." This is a
situation where consulting an immigration attorney before applying is strongly
recommended.

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "I have previously been found
  ineligible for a U.S. visa."
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q24. What is the difference between E-1 and E-2 — which one do I need?**

- **E-1 (Treaty Trader):** For nationals of treaty countries who carry on
  "substantial trade" in goods, services, or technology between the US and the
  treaty country. The trade must be primarily (more than 50%) between the US
  and the treaty country.
- **E-2 (Treaty Investor):** For nationals of treaty countries who invest a
  substantial amount of capital in a US business.

For most entrepreneurs and business investors looking to move to the US, the
E-2 is the relevant category. Canada qualifies for both E-1 and E-2.

**Sources:**
- [DOS-Treaty] U.S. Department of State — Treaty Countries List (lists both E-1
  and E-2 for Canada)
  https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/visa-information-resources/fees/treaty.html
- [DOS-Visa] U.S. Department of State — Visa Categories
  https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/visa-information-resources/all-visa-categories.html

**Q25. Can a holding company structure qualify for the E-2 investment?**

This is a complex area. The investment must ultimately be in an active,
for-profit, operating enterprise — not simply a holding vehicle. If a holding
company owns and actively manages operating subsidiaries, the structure may
qualify, but it requires careful documentation to show that the investment is
in a real enterprise and not a passive holding arrangement. This is an
"attorney judgment" scenario — consult an immigration attorney for your specific
structure.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — "The enterprise must be a real, operating,
  for-profit business"
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — investment must be in a
  qualifying enterprise
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

# PART 2 — INVESTMENT AMOUNT AND SOURCE OF FUNDS (Questions 26–52)

---

**Q26 & Q27. Is there a minimum investment amount? I keep seeing $50k, $100k,
$500k — what is the actual minimum?**

There is **no fixed statutory minimum** dollar amount. The US Embassy Canada
states explicitly: "E-2 visa regulations do not specify a minimum amount of
investment; the law does state that the investment must be 'substantial and
sufficient to ensure the success of the desired enterprise.' Different types of
businesses require varied amounts of capital, and the amount you will need to
invest depends on the nature of your U.S. enterprise."

What this means in practice:
- The test is **proportionality** — the investment must be substantial *relative
  to the total cost of the business*
- For **low-cost businesses** (total cost under $100K), the investor should
  contribute 90–100% of total costs
- For **medium businesses** ($100K–$500K total cost), 60–75% contribution is
  expected
- For **large businesses** (over $500K), 25–50% may suffice
- Toronto consulate **practical observed floor**: $75,000–$100,000 USD for most
  Canadian applications — this is NOT a legal minimum, but a pattern observed
  in approvals and denials

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "How much money do I need to
  invest?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 — Substantiality and Proportionality
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — Proportionality Test and business-type
  thresholds

**Q28. What does "at-risk capital" mean exactly?**

"At-risk" means the investment funds are genuinely exposed to partial or total
loss if the business fails — they cannot be protected, insured against loss, or
recoverable on demand. The US Embassy Canada clarifies that funds must be
"irrevocably committed" to the investment. Your knowledge base elaborates:

What qualifies as at-risk:
- Funds already transferred to the US business bank account
- Funds already spent on business assets (lease deposit, equipment, franchise
  fee)
- Funds irrevocably committed via signed contracts (franchise agreement, lease)
- Personal guarantees on business loans (puts personal assets at risk)

What does NOT qualify:
- Funds still sitting in a Canadian personal bank account
- Funds in escrow pending visa approval (with one exception — see Q14)
- Loans where a third party bears all repayment risk
- Funds committed only verbally or by letter of intent

**Sources:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "funds be irrevocably committed"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — The Funds At Risk Requirement

---

**Q29 & Q30. Can my investment be in the form of a loan? Can I use a bank loan
secured against my personal assets?**

A loan CAN count toward the E-2 investment — but only if **the investor
personally bears the repayment risk**. If you take a loan and use your personal
assets as collateral (e.g., a HELOC secured against your home), that loan is
considered at-risk because your personal assets are on the line if the business
fails. A loan where a third party bears all repayment risk (e.g., a loan secured
only against business assets with no personal guarantee) generally does NOT
count as qualifying E-2 capital.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — "Financed amounts (loans, seller
  financing) generally do NOT count toward the E-2 investment unless the
  investor is personally at risk for repayment."
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "funds must be at risk"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q31. Does paying upfront rent for a commercial space count toward the
investment?**

Yes. Upfront lease deposits and pre-paid rent paid directly in service of the
business qualify as invested and at-risk capital — they represent funds
irrevocably committed to the business. A signed lease agreement is also evidence
that the business is real and in process.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — "Funds already spent on business assets
  (lease deposit, equipment, franchise fee)" count as at-risk
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — funds at risk
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q32. Can I count equipment I already own as part of the investment?**

Equipment you already owned prior to committing it to the business may be
counted if you can document its fair market value and its transfer or commitment
to the US enterprise. However, the consulate will scrutinize the valuation of
pre-existing assets carefully. Equipment purchased specifically for the business
(with receipts) is more straightforward to document.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 and 6.12 — investment funds documentation
  requirements

---

**Q33 & Q219. What happens if I invest the money and then the visa gets denied?
Do I lose it?**

This is one of the most significant risks of the E-2 process. If your visa is
denied after you have already invested funds in the US business, **you do not
automatically recover your investment**. The business assets remain in the US,
and recouping them depends entirely on: (1) whether the business can be wound
down; (2) whether lease and franchise agreements can be exited; and (3) the
liquidity of the assets purchased. There is no visa-denial insurance or
government recovery mechanism. This is why thorough preparation before
investing — strong business plan, documented source of funds, and ideally legal
counsel — is critical.

**Sources:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — funds must be "at risk" before
  issuance — this explicitly confirms pre-commitment
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — denial risk and pre-investment

**Q34. How do I prove the source of my investment funds?**

The consulate requires a complete, documented chronological trail of your
investment funds — from where the money was originally earned or received,
through every account it passed through, to its final deployment in the US
business. Your knowledge base describes this as a "Source of Funds" chronology,
requiring:
- Bank statements showing accumulation of funds over time
- Tax returns demonstrating lawful income
- Sale documents if funds came from selling a property or business
- Wire transfer confirmations showing the funds arriving in the US business
  account
- Any inheritance or gift documentation

Gaps in this trail are the most common reason for 221(g) administrative
suspensions at the Toronto consulate.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "Source of Funds Unclear" denial reason
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — How the officer reviews Tab F/H
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q35. Can I use a gift from a family member as the investment capital?**

Yes, gifted funds can be used as E-2 investment capital. However, you must be
able to document: (1) the gift relationship (donor and recipient); (2) the
origin of the funds in the donor's hands (where the donor's money came from);
(3) the transfer of the funds to you; and (4) the subsequent transfer to the
US business. Your knowledge base notes: "Inherited or gifted funds require
additional documentation showing the origin of the gift and the relationship
between donor and recipient."

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Module 1, Q5 — source type: inheritance
  advisory

---

**Q36. Can I use proceeds from selling my Canadian house as the investment?**

Yes — the sale of a Canadian property is a well-documented and commonly accepted
source of E-2 investment funds. You will need to provide: the property sale
agreement, the closing statement showing net proceeds, and bank records showing
the funds received. **Critical warning:** Do NOT sell your Canadian home before
your E-2 visa is approved. Selling your primary Canadian residence before the
interview is one of the most common triggers for a 214(b) immigrant intent
denial, as officers view it as evidence you do not intend to return to Canada.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.4 — "214(b) Immigrant Intent: selling Canadian
  home before visa approval"
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Q16: "Selling your Canadian home before
  your E-2 interview is one of the most common triggers for a 214(b) immigrant
  intent denial."

**Q37. What is the "proportionality test" or "inverted sliding scale"?**

The proportionality test (sometimes called the "inverted sliding scale") means:
the smaller the total cost of the business, the higher the percentage of that
cost the investor must personally contribute. Conversely, the larger the
business, the lower the required percentage. The formula is:

  Substantiality = (Investor's Total Investment / Total Business Start-Up Cost)
  × 100

Thresholds from your knowledge base (based on Toronto consulate patterns):
- Total cost under $100K → investor should contribute 90–100%
- Total cost $100K–$500K → investor should contribute 70–90%
- Total cost $500K–$1M → investor should contribute 50–70%
- Total cost over $1M → investor should contribute 25–50%

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — Proportionality Test Calculator

**Q40. Do legal fees and immigration attorney costs count toward the investment?**

Generally no. Professional fees paid to immigration attorneys or business
formation lawyers are considered costs of obtaining the visa, not an investment
in the US enterprise. They do not count toward the E-2 investment amount because
they are not funds placed at risk in the business.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 and 6.12 — investment must be at risk in
  the enterprise, not professional fees

---

**Q41. Can I co-invest with my spouse — does that count as one investment?**

If your spouse will also be applying for their own E-2 visa as a co-investor
(50/50 partnership), each partner must independently meet the investment
requirements with their own documented source of funds — you cannot pool or
share a single investment pool across both applications. If your spouse is
applying as an E-2 dependent (not as an investor), then only you need to meet
the investment threshold.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.3 — "Each partner must independently
  demonstrate their own source of funds — cannot share or combine to meet one
  threshold."

---

**Q44. If I invest in a franchise, does the franchise fee count as part of the
investment?**

Yes. The franchise fee paid to the franchisor is a direct investment in the
business and counts toward your E-2 investment total. It is also an important
piece of evidence that funds are irrevocably committed, since franchise fees
are typically non-refundable once paid.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — "Funds already spent on business assets
  (lease deposit, equipment, franchise fee)" count as at-risk investment
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q45. Can I use a Canadian HELOC (home equity line of credit) as the source
of investment?**

Yes — if the HELOC is secured against your personal assets (your Canadian home),
the funds drawn from it are at risk because you personally bear repayment
liability. This is a recognized and documented source of E-2 investment funds.
You will need to provide: your HELOC agreement, the draw statement showing the
funds withdrawn, and bank records tracing those funds to the US business.

**⚠️ Tax note:** A RRSP withdrawal as a non-resident attracts a 25% withholding
tax (reduced to 15% under the Canada–US Tax Treaty in some cases). Consult a
cross-border CPA before any RRSP or HELOC strategy.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — Funds At Risk Requirement; RRSP
  withdrawal advisory in Q5 of Module 1

---

**Q46. If I buy an existing business, does the full purchase price count as my
investment?**

The purchase price of an existing business is generally the primary investment
figure for E-2 purposes. However, the consulate will assess whether the purchase
price reflects the actual fair market value of the enterprise. You should have
documentation showing: the purchase agreement, business valuation, assets being
acquired, and funds transferred. Working capital committed to run the business
post-acquisition may also be counted.

**Sources:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.1 and 6.12 — investment documentation
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "Different types of businesses
  require varied amounts of capital"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q47. Do working capital reserves count toward the investment total?**

Yes. Working capital committed to the business — funds set aside to cover
operating expenses, payroll, and initial operating losses — counts as part of
the E-2 investment, provided it is held in the US business bank account and
is genuinely committed to business operations (not sitting in a personal account
as a reserve the investor intends to keep).

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — capital allocation table inputs include
  working capital

---

**Q50. Does the investment have to be fully deployed before I apply, or can some
be in escrow?**

The US Embassy Canada provides the definitive answer: "Funds can also be
considered to be irrevocably committed if they are held in an escrow account
contingent **only** on the issuance of an E-visa." This means escrow is
acceptable under specific conditions — if the only condition for fund release
is visa approval, and the funds cannot be reclaimed for any other reason.
Escrow with additional conditions (e.g., "if I decide not to proceed") does
NOT qualify.

**Source:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — "Do I really have to invest the
  money before I apply?"
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/

---

**Q51. Can I use RRSP or TFSA funds from Canada as the source of investment?**

This is possible but requires careful tax planning:

- **RRSP:** Withdrawals as a Canadian non-resident attract a **25% withholding
  tax**, reduced to **15% under the Canada–US Tax Treaty** for periodic payments.
  Lump-sum withdrawals face the full 25%. A cross-border CPA must review this
  before you withdraw.
- **TFSA:** Withdrawals from a TFSA do not attract Canadian tax. However, once
  you become a US tax resident, the TFSA loses its tax-sheltered status in the
  US — the IRS does not recognize the TFSA as a tax-exempt account.
- Both RRSP and TFSA accounts must be reported on the **FBAR (FinCEN Form 114)**
  once you are a US tax resident if combined foreign account balances exceed
  $10,000 USD at any point in the year.

**Source:**
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 7.1 — Module 1 Q5: "RRSP withdrawals as a
  non-resident attract a 25% withholding tax (reduced to 15% under the
  Canada–US Tax Treaty in some cases)."
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.11 — FBAR Requirements

**Q52. What documentation is needed to show the funds are "irrevocably committed"?**

To demonstrate irrevocable commitment of funds, you should provide:
1. Wire transfer confirmation (showing transfer from your Canadian account to
   the US business account)
2. US business bank account statements (showing funds received)
3. Receipts for expenditures (franchise fee paid, equipment purchased, lease
   deposit paid)
4. Signed contracts (franchise agreement, commercial lease)
5. If in escrow: escrow agreement showing the only release condition is visa
   approval

The cover letter and Tab F/H of the Toronto consulate application package must
narrate this chronology clearly — from the origin of the funds to their final
committed state in the business.

**Sources:**
- [USEmb-CA] U.S. Embassy Canada E-Visa FAQs — irrevocably committed requirement
  https://ca.usembassy.gov/treaty-trader-and-investor-visas-faqs/
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.12 — Pre-Transfer Checklist and wire
  confirmation as Tab F evidence
- [KB] E2Pathway Vol 3, Section 6.13 — How the officer reviews Tab F/H
