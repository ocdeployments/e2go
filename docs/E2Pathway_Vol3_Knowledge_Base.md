# SECTION 6 — E-2 VISA KNOWLEDGE BASE

## 6.1 E-2 Visa Fundamentals

### What the E-2 Visa Is
The E-2 Treaty Investor Visa allows citizens of treaty countries (Canada 
included) to enter the U.S. to develop and direct a business in which they 
have invested, or are actively in the process of investing, a substantial amount 
of capital. It is a nonimmigrant visa — not a path to permanent residency on 
its own, but renewable indefinitely as long as the business continues operating.

### Eligibility Requirements
1. Must be a citizen of a treaty country (Canada qualifies)
2. Must have invested, or be actively investing, a substantial amount of capital
3. The enterprise must be a real, operating, for-profit business
4. The investment must be at-risk (not held in a personal account)
5. The investor must be entering solely to develop and direct the enterprise
6. The business must NOT be marginal (must generate more than minimal living)
7. Must demonstrate non-immigrant intent (intent to return to Canada)

### What Does "Substantial" Mean?
There is no fixed minimum dollar amount. The test is proportionality:
- Small businesses ($50K–$150K total cost): investor should contribute 80–100%
- Medium businesses ($150K–$500K): investor should contribute 60–75%
- Large businesses ($500K+): investor should contribute 25–50%
Toronto consulate practical minimum: $75,000–$100,000 USD is the
generally observed floor for Canadian applicants

### The Marginality Test
The business must have the present or future capacity to generate income:
- Beyond providing a minimal living wage for the investor and family
- To make a significant economic contribution (i.e., create jobs)
A business projecting $60,000/year net supporting a family of 4 will fail
the marginality test. Projections should show at least 3–4x household income
need and clear hiring milestones within 5 years.

## 6.3 Partnership Applications (Two Co-Investors)

### The 50/50 Rule
- Maximum two E-2 investors per business
- Each must own exactly 50% — no more, no less
- A 49/51 split disqualifies the minority partner
- 60/40 split: the 60% partner may qualify but the 40% partner cannot apply

### Independence Requirements
Each partner must independently demonstrate:
- Their own source of funds (cannot share or combine to meet one threshold)
- Their own active management role in the business
- Their own operational responsibilities (not just passive equity)
- Their own DS-160, their own cover letter, their own interview prep

### What Gets Shared in the Partnership Application
- Business plan (one plan, dual-investor language)
- Business asset register
- Organization chart (showing both partners' roles)
- Operating agreement (new document — explains 50/50 split, voting, management)

### Partnership-Specific Documents Required
1. Partnership/Operating Agreement — 50/50 ownership, voting rights, management roles
2. Individual investment narratives — each partner's own source of funds
3. Dual cover letters — each independently proves their management role
4. Investment allocation narrative — explains how total investment is split
5. Two DS-160 reference sheets, two interview prep sheets

### The U.S. Citizen Partner Scenario
If one partner is a U.S. citizen or permanent resident:
- Only the Canadian partner applies for the E-2
- The 50% ownership rule still applies
- The Canadian partner must still demonstrate operational control

### Change of Status (COS) vs. Consular Processing
Two paths for applicants already inside the United States:

| Factor | Consular (Toronto) | Change of Status (USCIS) |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------------|
| Who uses it | Most Canadians | Those already in U.S. on valid status |
| Form | DS-160 | I-129 |
| Interview | Required | Usually not required |
| Validity | 5-year stamp | 2-year I-94 increment |
| Travel during process | Normal | Cannot leave U.S. |
| Typical processing | 60–90 days | 3–6 months |

## 6.4 Common Denial Reasons & How the App Addresses Them

### 214(b) — Immigrant Intent (Most Dangerous Denial)
What triggers it:
- Sold Canadian home before visa approval
- Surrendered OHIP, closed Canadian accounts
- Close family ties in U.S. with no Canadian family remaining
- Answers suggesting permanent intent
App response: Immigrant intent risk scoring in Module 4, interview prep
coaching specifically on "Why are you maintaining Canadian ties?", explicit
advisory if risky answers detected during interview module

### Investment Not Substantial
What triggers it: Amount too low relative to total business cost
App response: Substantiality calculator in Module 4, warning when USD 
equivalent after CAD/USD conversion falls below threshold, business-type-
specific minimum investment warnings

### Business Deemed Marginal
What triggers it: Projections too low, no hiring plan, solo operator
App response: Non-marginality scoring in Module 4, 5-year projections 
must include hiring milestones, solo operator risk warning

### Source of Funds Unclear
What triggers it: Gaps in timeline, unexplained deposits, incomplete trail
App response: Template 1 validates chronology, gaps trigger warnings,
app will not generate cover letter until source of funds timeline is complete

### Weak or Generic Business Plan
What triggers it: No market research, unrealistic projections, no competition
analysis, copy-paste plans
App response: Template 2 requires specific inputs; no generic language 
generated — every sentence references user's actual business data

### 221(g) Administrative Suspension (Not a Denial)
Most common supplemental requests:
- Additional source of funds documentation
- Updated business plan
- Local market research for the specific city
- Lease/purchase agreement update
- Evidence business is currently operating
App response: 221(g) emergency guide module with document response
checklists for each common request type

## 6.5 The Renewal / Extension Process

### 5-Year Consular Renewal (Toronto)
- Same tab structure as original application
- Must demonstrate business actually operated as projected
- Must show actual financial performance vs. original projections
- Must document jobs created (the primary marginality test at renewal)
- Business plan updated with actual vs. projected variance narrative
- App generates: variance narrative, updated plan, renewal cover letter

### 2-Year Status Extension (USCIS, Inside U.S.)
- Form I-129
- No interview required
- 2-year increment only (vs. 5-year stamp from Toronto)
- Cannot travel internationally during processing
- Users who cannot travel or want simpler process choose this route

### Marginality Risk at Renewal — The 5-Year Window
At renewal, USCIS/consulate evaluates whether the business has moved
beyond marginality. Red flags:
- Revenue not exceeding investor's household income by 3–4x
- No employees hired despite original hiring plan
- Business projections significantly missed across multiple years
- Solo operator — no staff whatsoever
App response: Renewal Readiness Score at Year 3–4, actual vs. projected
performance table, auto-generated variance narrative explaining any gaps

## 6.6 The Green Card Pathway (Beyond E-2)

The E-2 visa is temporary and does not directly lead to a green card.
However, practical pathways exist:

### EB-1C (Multinational Manager or Executive)
- Requires 1 year of employment by the company outside the U.S.
- Then transfer to the U.S. business as a manager or executive
- The E-2 business must be multinational (operations in Canada + U.S.)
- Processing time: 6–18 months (no quota for Canadians)
- Best option for E-2 holders who establish genuine Canadian operations

### EB-5 (Immigrant Investor)
- Minimum investment: $800,000 USD in a Targeted Employment Area
  or $1.05M in other areas
- Must create 10 full-time U.S. jobs
- Processing time: 2–5 years
- Can be run concurrently with E-2 renewal
- Expensive but the most direct investor route to permanent residency

### National Interest Waiver (EB-2 NIW)
- For individuals with exceptional abilities or advanced degrees
- Self-petition (no employer required)
- Relevant for some E-2 applicants with academic or professional backgrounds

### The Child Age-Out Problem
- Dependent children lose E-2 status at age 21 — on their birthday
- Critical planning trigger: if child is 17+ when parent applies, begin
  planning a separate pathway NOW
- Options at age 21: F-1 student visa, their own work visa, their own E-2,
  or green card if EB-5/EB-1C process started early enough
- The race: green card processing time vs. child's 21st birthday
- App tracks each dependent's age and alerts when planning window opens

## 6.7 What Disqualifies a Business

Businesses that CANNOT be used for E-2:
- Passive real estate investment (rental portfolios, REITs)
- Cannabis/marijuana (federally illegal)
- Any business where investor has no active management role
- Businesses that will not be "real and operating" by interview date
- Single-person consulting where the only "product" is the investor's labor
- Marginal businesses (projecting only enough income for family subsistence)

Businesses that CAN work but require extra documentation:
- Home-based businesses (must show physical workspace, client interaction)
- Online businesses (must demonstrate real revenue and operations)
- Franchise resales (must update all franchise docs to reflect buyer's name)
- Real estate MANAGEMENT companies (active management — not passive holding)

## 6.8 Canadian Departure Tax & Financial Implications

When a Canadian becomes a non-resident for tax purposes:
- CRA deems all capital property disposed of at fair market value
  ("deemed disposition") — tax is owed on unrealized gains
- RRSP: can be kept (deferred) but 25% non-resident withholding tax applies
  on withdrawals; most cases leave it until retirement
- CPP/OAS: continues to be received by non-residents; 25% withholding 
  applies unless reduced by tax treaty (Canada-U.S. treaty reduces to 15%)
- TFSA: ceases to earn contribution room; contributions as non-resident 
  attract 1%/month penalty
- Provincial health insurance (OHIP etc.): cancelled upon departure — 
  critical to maintain until U.S. coverage is active

Pre-departure checklist implications for app:
1. File Form NR73 (Determination of Residency Status) with CRA
2. Arrange departure tax filing for the year of departure
3. Update all financial institutions with non-resident status
4. Do NOT close Canadian accounts before visa approval (ties to Canada needed)
5. Do NOT surrender provincial health card before U.S. health coverage starts
