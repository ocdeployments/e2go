# U.S. Tax Obligations for E-2 Visa Holders
**kb/us-tax-obligations-e2.md**
*Sources: IRS — Taxation of Aliens by Visa Type (irs.gov), IRS Publication 519,
IRS Topic 851, FinCEN FBAR requirements, Greenback Tax Services E-2 tax guide,
Canada-U.S. Tax Convention, IRS Rev. Proc. 2014-55*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## Key Principle: Tax Status Is Not Visa Status

The IRS does not tax you based on your visa type. It taxes you based on
your **tax residency status** — which is determined by the Substantial
Presence Test, not by whether you hold an E-2, TN, H-1B, or any other visa.

> "Generally, for United States tax purposes, a nonimmigrant or immigrant
> visa status under U.S. immigration law is different from U.S. tax residency
> status under U.S. tax law."
> — IRS: Taxation of Aliens by Visa Type and Immigration Status

This means most E-2 holders become **resident aliens for U.S. tax purposes**
within their first calendar year and are taxed on their **worldwide income**
from that point forward — just like a U.S. citizen.

---

## Step 1 — Determine Your U.S. Tax Residency Status

### The Substantial Presence Test

You are a U.S. resident alien for tax purposes if you are physically present
in the United States for:

**183 days** or more, calculated as:
- All days present in the **current year**, PLUS
- 1/3 of days present in the **first preceding year**, PLUS
- 1/6 of days present in the **second preceding year**

> Source: IRS Publication 519, IRS Topic 851

**Practical result for E-2 holders:**
If you move to the United States and operate your business full-time,
you will almost certainly meet the Substantial Presence Test by your
first full calendar year. Most E-2 holders are U.S. resident aliens
from the year of arrival onward.

### Year of Arrival — Dual-Status Year

In the year you arrive in the United States, you are typically a
**dual-status alien:**
- **Non-resident alien** for the period before your arrival
- **Resident alien** from your arrival date forward

**How to file a dual-status return:**
- File Form 1040 (with "Dual-Status Return" written at the top)
- Attach Form 1040-NR as a statement for the non-resident period
- Report only U.S.-source income for the non-resident period
- Report worldwide income for the resident period (from arrival date)

> Source: IRS — Taxation of Dual-Status Individuals (irs.gov)

### First-Year Choice Election

If you do not meet the Substantial Presence Test in your first year but
you are a resident alien in the following year, you may be eligible to
make a **First-Year Choice election** to be treated as a resident alien
for the entire year of arrival.

This election:
- Allows you to file jointly with a spouse in the first year
- May be advantageous or disadvantageous depending on your income mix
- Must be made by the due date of the return (including extensions)
- Consult a CPA before making this election — it is irrevocable

## Step 2 — Federal Income Tax Filing

### Form 1040 — Once You Are a Resident Alien

As a U.S. resident alien, you file **Form 1040** (the same form as a
U.S. citizen) and report **worldwide income:**

- E-2 business income (profits from your U.S. business)
- Canadian rental income
- RRSP/RRIF withdrawals (see kb/rrsp-tfsa-crossborder.md)
- Canadian investment income
- Any other income from any country

**Filing deadlines:**
- Standard: April 15
- Automatic extension: October 15 (file Form 4868 to request)
- Canadian residents filing a U.S. return: June 15 automatic extension
  (but interest accrues from April 15 if tax is owing)

### E-2 Business Income — Entity Structure Matters

How your E-2 business income is taxed depends on your entity structure:

| Entity Type | Tax Treatment | Forms |
|---|---|---|
| Single-member LLC | Disregarded entity — reported on Schedule C of 1040 | Schedule C, Schedule SE |
| Multi-member LLC | Partnership — K-1 flows to 1040 | Form 1065, K-1, Schedule E |
| S Corporation | K-1 flows to 1040 | Form 1120-S, K-1, Schedule E |
| C Corporation | Corporate level tax (21%); dividends taxed again on 1040 | Form 1120 |

**Most E-2 businesses are structured as single-member or multi-member LLCs.
Default LLC taxation means business profit flows directly onto your
personal 1040 — there is no separate entity-level tax.**

## Step 3 — Self-Employment Tax and FICA

### Self-Employment Tax (SE Tax)

If your E-2 business is a sole proprietorship or single-member LLC
(disregarded entity), your business **net income** is subject to
**self-employment tax:**

- Rate: **15.3%** on the first $176,100 of net self-employment income
  (2025 threshold; adjusts annually for inflation)
- Rate: **2.9%** (Medicare only) on net income above the threshold
- An additional **0.9% Medicare surtax** applies on income above $200,000
  (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly)

> SE tax applies only once you are a U.S. resident alien. Nonresident
> aliens are not subject to self-employment tax.
> Source: IRS — Social Security Tax/Medicare Tax and Self-Employment (irs.gov)

**SE tax deduction:** You can deduct **half of your SE tax** from your
gross income on Form 1040 (Schedule 1, line 15). This reduces your
income tax, though not the SE tax itself.

### FICA on Wages from Your Own Corporation

If you structure your E-2 business as an S Corporation or C Corporation
and pay yourself a **salary:**
- Standard FICA applies: 6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare (employee share)
- Employer matching: same rates (6.2% + 1.45%)
- Total FICA cost: **15.3%** split between employee and employer

**S Corporation tax strategy:** Many business owners use an S Corp to
pay themselves a "reasonable salary" (subject to full FICA) while
distributing remaining profits as dividends (not subject to SE tax or
FICA). This requires proper S Corp compliance. Consult a CPA before electing S Corp status.

### Canada-U.S. Social Security Totalization Agreement

Canada and the United States have a **Totalization Agreement** that prevents
double Social Security taxation for individuals working across the border.

For E-2 holders permanently based in the U.S. and operating a U.S. business:
- You contribute to U.S. Social Security (via SE tax or FICA)
- You do not also contribute to Canada Pension Plan on the same income
- However, your CPP contributions from Canadian working years still
  count toward your eventual CPP benefit

## Step 4 — State Income Tax

E-2 holders owe income tax in the state where their business is located
and where they live. There is no federal-state coordination on tax rates —
you pay both.

### States With No Income Tax (as of 2026)

Florida, Texas, Nevada, Wyoming, South Dakota, Washington, Alaska

**For E-2 applicants choosing a business location, the absence of state
income tax in Florida and Texas is a significant financial advantage.**

### States With High Income Tax Rates

California: up to 13.3% (highest in U.S.)
New York: up to 10.9%
New Jersey: up to 10.75%
Hawaii: up to 11%

### State Filing Mechanics

Most states follow the federal definition of taxable income with
modifications. You file a state return for each state where you have
taxable income. If you live in one state and do business in another,
you may owe tax in both (with credit provisions in most cases).

## Step 5 — FBAR (FinCEN Form 114)

### Who Must File

You must file an FBAR (Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts)
if you are a **U.S. person** (including resident aliens) who has:

- A **financial interest in** or **signature authority over**
- One or more foreign financial accounts
- Where the **aggregate maximum value exceeded $10,000 USD at any point**
  during the calendar year

> Source: FinCEN — Report Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (fincen.gov)

### What Counts as a Foreign Financial Account

- Canadian bank accounts (chequing, savings)
- Canadian investment accounts, brokerage accounts
- RRSP, RRIF (yes — even though treaty-deferred for income tax purposes)
- TFSA
- Canadian business bank accounts (if you have signature authority)

### Filing Mechanics

- File electronically through the **BSA E-Filing System** at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov
- **Due date:** April 15 (automatic extension to October 15 — no form required)
- No filing fee
- Report the **maximum value** each account reached during the year
  (not the year-end balance)

### FBAR Penalties

FBAR penalties are severe:
- **Non-willful violation:** Up to $10,000 per violation per year
- **Willful violation:** Greater of $100,000 or 50% of the account
  balance at the time of the violation, per year
- Criminal penalties possible for willful violations

> ⚠️ Most Canadian E-2 holders will have FBAR obligations from their
> first year of U.S. tax residency. This is not optional and is
> aggressively enforced.

## Step 6 — Form 8938 (FATCA — Specified Foreign Financial Assets)

In addition to FBAR, U.S. resident aliens with significant foreign
assets must file **Form 8938** with their federal tax return.

### Thresholds for U.S. Residents

| Filing Status | Year-End Value Exceeds | Any Point During Year Exceeds |
|---|---|---|
| Single or MFS | $50,000 | $75,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $100,000 | $150,000 |

### What to Report

- Foreign bank accounts
- RRSP, RRIF, TFSA
- Foreign brokerage accounts
- Foreign partnership interests
- Foreign business interests not held through a U.S. entity

FBAR and Form 8938 overlap in coverage but are separate requirements
with separate filing systems. Both may apply simultaneously.

## Step 7 — Foreign Tax Credits

As a U.S. resident alien reporting worldwide income, you will pay tax
in both Canada (on Canadian-source income) and the U.S. (on worldwide
income). To prevent double taxation:

**File Form 1116 (Foreign Tax Credit)** with your U.S. return.

The foreign tax credit allows you to offset your U.S. tax liability
dollar-for-dollar by the amount of foreign (Canadian) tax paid on
the same income — up to the U.S. tax liability on that income.

**How it works:**
- You withdraw $20,000 from your RRSP as a non-resident
- Canada withholds 15% = $3,000 Canadian withholding tax
- U.S. taxes the $20,000 at your marginal rate (say 22%) = $4,400 U.S. tax
- Foreign tax credit: claim the $3,000 CAD withholding
- Net U.S. tax owing: approximately $4,400 − $3,000 = $1,400

The credit does not create a refund — it only offsets U.S. tax owing
on the same income.

## Step 8 — Annual Filing Checklist for E-2 Holders

Each tax year, a typical Canadian E-2 holder should expect to file:

| Form | Purpose | Due Date |
|---|---|---|
| Form 1040 | Federal income tax return | April 15 (Oct 15 with extension) |
| Schedule C | Business profit/loss (LLC/sole prop) | With 1040 |
| Schedule SE | Self-employment tax | With 1040 |
| Form 1116 | Foreign tax credit | With 1040 |
| FinCEN 114 (FBAR) | Foreign bank account report | April 15 (auto-ext. Oct 15) |
| Form 8938 | FATCA foreign assets | With 1040 |
| State return | State income tax | Varies by state |

If you have a corporation:
| Form 1120-S | S Corporation return | March 15 |
| Form 1065 | Partnership return | March 15 |

## Year of Departure from the U.S.

If your E-2 visa is not renewed or you choose to return to Canada:

- You file a **dual-status return** for the year of departure
- U.S. resident alien for the period you were in the U.S.
- Non-resident alien for the rest of the year
- File Form 1040 with "Dual-Status Return" at the top; attach 1040-NR
  as statement for the non-resident period
- FBAR is still required if you had reportable accounts at any point
  during the year, even if you have since left the U.S.

## Professional Guidance

U.S. tax compliance for E-2 holders is not a DIY undertaking. The
interaction of federal income tax, SE tax, state tax, FBAR, FATCA, foreign
tax credits, and the Canada-U.S. Tax Treaty requires specialist knowledge.

**Who to engage:**
- A CPA or enrolled agent with specific experience in cross-border
  Canada-U.S. taxation
- Ideally a firm licensed in both Canada and the U.S.
- Engage before you move — pre-departure planning can significantly
  reduce your combined tax liability

> This document is for educational purposes only and does not constitute
> tax advice. Tax rates, thresholds, and rules change annually. Confirm
> all information with a qualified cross-border tax professional before
> filing any return.
