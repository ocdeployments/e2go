## PART 1 — THE COMPLETE E-2 LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### The Six Independent Requirements

Every E-2 application must independently satisfy all six requirements.
Failing any one is a denial. They are not weighted — five strong and 
one failure is still a denial.

**Requirement 1 — Treaty Nationality**

The investor must be a national — not a permanent resident — of a 
treaty country. Key edge cases:

- Dual nationals: must apply on the treaty nationality
- British nationals: multiple categories exist, not all are E-2 eligible.
  "British National (Overseas)" does not qualify. Full British citizenship 
  or British Citizen status required.
- Stateless persons: not eligible
- Companies: nationality determined by the nationality of individual owners 
  holding 50%+ — not the country of incorporation
- Treaty countries: 82 as of 2026. Verify current list at 
  travel.state.gov before generating any nationality-based language

**Requirement 2 — Substantial Investment**

Not a fixed dollar amount. A proportionality test applying a sliding scale:

- High-cost business ($10M+): even a small percentage may be substantial
- Mid-cost ($500K–$10M): 15–20% of total cost
- Low-cost (under $500K): higher percentage required; below $100K total 
  investment is extremely difficult at any consulate

Proportionality as a secondary substantiality argument: investment as
a percentage of the investor's net worth, alongside investment as a
percentage of total enterprise cost, per 9 FAM 402.9-6(D). No fixed
dollar amount is required; the Department considers both ratios when
assessing substantiality.

IMPORTANT: Investment type matters, not just amount:
- Franchise fee alone (no equipment, no leasehold) is weaker than a 
  mixed investment across fee, equipment, and working capital
- Officers value deployed funds over committed-but-not-transferred funds
- Round-number investments ($100,000 exactly) invite suspicion — 
  real business investments are never exactly round numbers

**Requirement 3 — Funds At Risk**

The investment must be irrevocably committed with genuine risk of loss.

- Funds held in escrow with refund conditions are NOT fully at risk — 
  address directly in cover letter
- Loans secured by business assets do not qualify — only loans secured 
  by personal assets (home equity, personal guarantee) count
- Loan-back arrangements (invest then borrow back from business) void 
  the at-risk requirement
- The at-risk requirement continues throughout the visa period — 
  not just at time of application

**Requirement 4 — Real and Active Commercial Enterprise**

Three sub-requirements:

4A — Not passive: must produce a service or commodity. Real estate rental, 
stock portfolios, and non-profit organizations are excluded.

4B — Not marginal: business must do more than provide a living for the 
investor and family. Has a temporal dimension — a Year 1 marginal business 
that demonstrably grows to non-marginal by Year 3 can satisfy this requirement 
if the growth trajectory is credibly documented.

4C — Currently operating or in active formation: Three types exist:
- Type A: Fully operational (easiest to approve)
- Type B: Active formation with specific milestones (approvable with strong evidence)
- Type C: Planned but not yet started (very difficult — requires substantial 
  pre-opening activity evidence)
Documents must be calibrated to the formation stage.

**Requirement 5 — Develop and Direct**

Two sub-requirements:

5A — Ownership or control: more than 50% ownership OR documented operational 
control through other means. Exactly 50% with one equal partner raises the 
negative control doctrine — address explicitly.

5B — Active involvement: investor must be physically present and actively 
managing. Part-time presence requires specific explanation. A manager hired 
to run day-to-day operations while the investor remains in Canada fails 
this requirement.

**Requirement 6 — For E-2 Employees (separate from principal investors)**
The employee's role must be executive, supervisory, or require specialized 
skills essential to the enterprise. The e2go platform handles principal 
investor applications. E-2 employee applications are a future product phase.

## PART 2 — THE OFFICER'S PERSPECTIVE
### 15 Specific Concerns from Adversarial Assessment

These are the specific concerns a consular officer applies when reviewing 
an E-2 application. Every generated document must address these either 
directly or by ensuring the facts presented eliminate the concern.

**Officer Concern 1 — Suspicious Investment Amount**
Officers look for round numbers. $100,000 exactly suggests a threshold 
calculation, not an actual investment. Real investments are odd numbers.
The startup cost breakdown must account for 100% of the investment, 
dollar by dollar.

**Officer Concern 2 — Funds That Appeared Suddenly**
Officers check dates on bank statements. Money that appeared in the 
last 6 months without a clear documented source triggers scrutiny.
Every fund movement needs a specific date, amount, and account reference.

**Officer Concern 3 — Business Not Actually Operating**
Officers distinguish between Type A (operational), Type B (active 
formation), and Type C (planned). Type C dressed as Type B is a common 
denial pattern. Evidence of actual pre-opening activities — lease, EIN, 
licenses, bank account — is required.

**Officer Concern 4 — Investor Cannot Run This Business**
Officers ask: if I met this person at dinner and they told me they were 
opening this business, would I believe them? Generic transferable skills 
arguments are not convincing. Specific, personal, credible connections 
between the investor's actual history and this specific business are required.

**Officer Concern 5 — Investor Intends to Immigrate**
Mental checklist: sold home, no home country employment, spouse no ties, 
children enrolling in U.S. schools, no remaining business interests, 
filing close to planned entry. Any two together requires strong cover 
letter treatment. Three or more requires a dedicated 214(b) section.

**Officer Concern 6 — Business Plan Numbers Don't Add Up**
Three quick checks: (1) Year 1 revenue / employees = plausible per-employee 
revenue for this business type? (2) Investor salary / Year 1 revenue < 40%? 
(3) Break-even timeline consistent with cash flow projections? Any failure 
triggers marginality scrutiny.

**Officer Concern 7 — Application Was Clearly Prepared by Software**
Tells: uniform paragraph length, consistent sentence length averaging 
20–25 words, sophisticated but impersonal vocabulary, no contractions, 
no specific place names, no sensory details, investor's personality 
entirely absent. The humanization pass must produce genuine variation 
and real specificity.

**Officer Concern 8 — Organizational Structure Doesn't Support Executive Role**
Officers look for at least one supervisory layer between the investor and 
line workers. An investor who manages 4 people doing operational work with 
no middle management may be an owner-operator in a line role, not an 
executive. The org chart must show a structure that supports the executive 
claim.

**Officer Concern 9 — Immigration History Is Incomplete**
Categories routinely underreported because applicants don't think they count:
overstaying by a few days, taking paid work on a tourist visa, attending 
business meetings on a tourist visa, prior E-2 applications withdrawn before 
decision. Any of these characterized as misrepresentation if not disclosed 
is far worse than the underlying issue.

**Officer Concern 10 — Investment Not At Risk**
Guaranteed return arrangements and escrow with refund conditions both 
undermine the at-risk requirement. The cover letter must specifically 
state that the investment is irrevocably committed and at risk of loss.

**Officer Concern 11 — 214(b) Presumption Not Addressed**
Every nonimmigrant applicant is presumed to be an intending immigrant 
under INA 214(b). The rebuttal must be proportionate to the strength 
of the immigration signals. A generic non-immigrant intent paragraph 
is not enough for an investor who is bringing their family, selling 
their business, and investing most of their net worth.

**Officer Concern 12 — Application Is Too Perfect**
A real business investment is messy. Applications where every answer is 
optimal and every narrative is pristine look like templates, not real 
cases. Acknowledged weaknesses directly addressed are more credible than 
applications pretending no weaknesses exist.

**Officer Concern 13 — Consulate-Specific Requirements Not Met**
Each consulate has published and unpublished requirements beyond the FAM.
Generation prompts must be calibrated per consulate. See Part 5 for 
consulate-specific requirements.

**Officer Concern 14 — Timeline Is Implausible**
Specific flags: FDD review period less than 14 days (legal minimum), 
total time from business selection to application under 90 days, 
investment commitment date and LLC formation date in the same month. 
These are not disqualifying but require explanation in the cover letter.

**Officer Concern 15 — Application Won't Survive Secondary Request**
What happens if the officer issues a 221(g) administrative processing 
notice and requests additional documents? Well-prepared applications 
have a secondary document strategy. The Applicant Preparation Brief 
must tell the investor what additional evidence they should have 
available but not necessarily submit upfront.

## PART 4 — PARTNERSHIP APPLICATIONS
### Complete Rules and Edge Cases

#### The Fundamental Framework

A partnership E-2 is not a joint application. It is two simultaneous 
independent applications that happen to share a business. Each partner 
must independently satisfy every E-2 requirement.

Maximum two E-2 investors per business. A third investor cannot hold 
E-2 status on the same business regardless of ownership structure.

#### The 50/50 Rule — Absolute

- Each partner must own exactly 50%
- A 49/51 split disqualifies the minority partner
- A 60/40 split means only the 60% partner may qualify
- No flexibility exists on this point for dual E-2 applications

#### The Five Partnership-Specific Gaps

**Partnership Gap 1 — Role Differentiation**

Each partner must have a genuinely distinct, non-overlapping management 
role. Two partners both claiming "Managing Director" with identical 
responsibilities is a denial pattern.

The officer test: "If Partner A was absent for a month, what would not 
get done?" And the same question reversed. If either answer is "nothing" 
or "everything" — the partnership fails develop-and-direct.

The engine must:
- Map each partner's described responsibilities
- Check for overlap in any category (financial oversight, staffing, 
  operations, client relations, compliance)
- If overlap detected: trigger specific follow-up asking each partner 
  to describe their specific decision domain
- Example of acceptable split: Partner A as CEO (financial oversight, 
  investor relations, strategic direction) and Partner B as COO 
  (staffing, client service, day-to-day operations)

**Partnership Gap 2 — Investment Independence**

Funds must never commingle in a joint account at any point in the paper 
trail. If a joint account was used, each partner's portion must be clearly 
allocated and separate wire transfers from each partner's personal account 
must be documented.

Partners cannot combine their investments to jointly meet the substantiality 
threshold. Each must independently meet it.

The engine must flag: any fund source described as a joint account.

**Partnership Gap 3 — The 50/50 Rule Edge Cases**

Spousal partnerships require heightened scrutiny:
- Funds may be matrimonial property — individual ownership harder to establish
- Management roles may appear interchangeable
- Non-immigrant intent is effectively combined

In most cases, the stronger path for married couples is: one spouse as 
E-2 investor, the other as E-2 dependent applying for EAD (work 
authorization). This avoids the fund independence problem entirely.

Operating agreement must not contain dilution provisions that could 
reduce either partner below 50%.

**Partnership Gap 4 — One Partner Denied**

If one partner is denied while the other is approved, the approved partner 
must immediately consult an immigration attorney. They may be able to 
proceed as a solo investor but the ownership structure may need revision.

This scenario must be addressed in the Applicant Preparation Brief for 
every partnership application.

**Partnership Gap 5 — Two-Interview Strategy**

Partners are interviewed independently. Officers sometimes coordinate 
to compare answers. Each partner must:
- Be able to independently describe the entire business, not just their role
- Agree in advance on how joint decisions will be characterized
- Not give identical scripted answers — identical answers across 
  independent interviews appear rehearsed
- Be prepared to describe their partner's role and responsibilities

#### What Is Shared vs Separate in a Partnership Package

| Shared Documents | Separate for Each Partner |
|---|---|
| Business plan (dual-investor language) | DS-160 application form |
| Operating agreement (50/50) | Individual cover letter |
| Organizational chart (both roles) | Source of funds chronology |
| Business asset register | Individual investment narrative |
| Lease, franchise agreement | Individual interview preparation |
| | Individual passport and ID documents |

## PART 5 — FDD ANALYSIS FOR FRANCHISE APPLICATIONS

The Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) is the most important third-party 
document in a franchise E-2 application. For E-2 purposes, five items 
are critical.

#### FDD Item 7 — Estimated Initial Investment

This is the gold standard for establishing total business cost for the 
proportionality analysis. Item 7 breaks down every cost category: franchise 
fee, training expenses, real property, equipment, inventory, working capital.

Engine requirement: Use Item 7 total as the denominator in the 
substantiality calculation. If applicant's stated total business cost 
differs from FDD Item 7 by more than 15%, flag the discrepancy and 
require explanation.

Officer check: Compares applicant's stated investment against Item 7 range. 
Investment significantly below the Item 7 range will be questioned.

#### FDD Item 12 — Territory

Whether the territory is exclusive, protected, or non-exclusive. 
Non-exclusive territories create competitive risk affecting revenue 
projection credibility.

Engine requirement: Ask about territory exclusivity. If non-exclusive, 
adjust projection plausibility thresholds downward and add territory 
risk acknowledgment to business plan.

#### FDD Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

Not all franchisors include Item 19. Its presence or absence fundamentally 
changes the generation strategy.

If Item 19 PRESENT:
- Use median gross revenue as benchmark for Year 1 projections
- If applicant's projection exceeds Item 19 median by more than 20%, 
  require specific justification
- Cite Item 19 explicitly in business plan financial projections section

If Item 19 ABSENT:
- Flag that projections must be supported by independent market research
- Generate stronger market analysis section in business plan
- Never generate unsupported projections claiming FDD support

Officer check: For franchises with Item 19, officers directly compare 
applicant projections against disclosed system data. Projections exceeding 
Item 19 medians without explanation are a credibility problem.

#### FDD Item 20 — Outlets and Franchisee Information

Lists every franchisee in the system — how many opened, how many closed, 
how many were transferred. A contracting system (more closures than openings) 
is a business risk.

Engine requirement: Ask applicant to describe the franchise system's 
growth trajectory. If system is contracting, business plan must address 
this risk directly.

#### FDD Item 21 — Financial Statements

The franchisor's audited financials. A franchisor with deteriorating 
balance sheet, declining revenue, or qualified audit opinions is a 
business stability risk.

Engine requirement: Ask whether applicant reviewed Item 21 and whether 
there are concerns about franchisor financial health. Officers occasionally 
ask whether the investor reviewed the franchisor's financials.

#### FDD Review Period

Federal law requires a minimum 14-day review period between receiving 
the FDD and signing the franchise agreement. If the applicant's timeline 
shows less than 14 days between FDD receipt and signing, flag this — 
it is a federal violation that the officer may notice.

## PART 6 — NON-FRANCHISE BUSINESS REQUIREMENTS

For independent businesses (not franchises), the following substitutes 
for what the FDD provides automatically.

#### Business Acquisition

Required documents (no exceptions):
- 3 years of historical financial statements from seller
- Reason for sale: retirement, relocation, or similar non-distress reason. 
  A business sold because it is struggling creates marginality questions 
  the business plan must address
- Business valuation by a certified business appraiser showing purchase 
  price reflects fair market value
- Post-acquisition operating plan showing investor's management takeover
- Non-compete agreement from seller
- Confirmation that key employees will remain

Engine requirement: Seller's reason for sale must be collected in Tab K 
or follow-up conversation. Historical financials must be analyzed to 
confirm business is not marginal.

#### Greenfield Startup

The hardest E-2 case. No operating history, no FDD, no comparable data.

Required evidence before application:
- At minimum three of the following must be confirmed: lease signed, 
  EIN obtained, business bank account opened, licenses applied for, 
  equipment purchased or ordered
- Local market research — specific to the city and demographic, 
  not national industry data
- Letters of intent or preliminary customer agreements if available
- Investor's directly relevant experience must compensate for lack 
  of operating history

Engine requirement: Greenfield applications must not proceed to generation 
until at least three pre-opening milestones are confirmed. The business 
plan for a greenfield application must work harder on market analysis 
and investor qualifications than a franchise application.

#### Non-Franchise Revenue Projection Basis

Without FDD Item 19 data, revenue projections must be grounded in:
- Independent market research report with local data
- Comparable business sale multiples or industry benchmarks
- Prior owner financials (acquisition only)
- Industry association data with specific citations

Engine must never generate revenue projections described only as 
"based on my experience" for non-franchise applications. This is 
the most common business plan weakness in non-franchise cases.

## PART 7 — PRE-APPLICATION ADVISORY SYSTEM
### Decisions That Must Be Made Before Filing

This advisory system should run as Module 2 — between the quiz and 
Module 3. The platform generates a personalized advisory report for 
each applicant before they begin filling out their application.

#### Business Type Advisory

Business types and E-2 eligibility:

ELIGIBLE:
- Franchise (new unit) — strongest case, FDD provides evidence base
- Existing business acquisition — strong if business is profitable
- Greenfield startup — harder case, requires more preparation evidence
- Online business with U.S. operational presence — scrutinized but eligible

NOT ELIGIBLE:
- Real estate rental — passive investment
- Stock or securities portfolio — passive investment
- Non-profit organizations — FAM 402.9-6(B)(c) absolute bar
- Cannabis businesses — federally illegal
- Multi-level marketing — not a commercial enterprise
- Solo consulting with no employees — owner-operator risk, 
  executive role nearly impossible to establish

HIGH SCRUTINY (eligible but difficult):
- Online-only businesses with no physical U.S. premises
- Solo professional services (consulting, coaching) with no hiring plan
- Franchise in industry where investor has zero background

#### Investment Amount Guidance

| Business Total Cost | Recommended Minimum Investment | Risk if Below |
|---|---|---|
| Under $100K | 75%+ | Substantiality nearly impossible |
| $100K–$300K | 65–75% | High scrutiny |
| $300K–$1M | 50–65% | Moderate, address in cover letter |
| Over $1M | 30–50% | Proportionality argument available |

Investment red flags (generate warnings when detected):
- Round number investment ($100,000 exactly)
- Investment below $100,000 USD total
- Investment below 50% of total business cost with no legal argument
- Loan secured by business assets
- Any portion not yet transferred and irrevocably committed
- Funds in escrow with refund condition

#### Ownership Structure Advisory

Solo investor checklist:
- Ownership above 50% OR documented alternative control mechanism
- Executive or supervisory title (Owner, MD, CEO, GM — never Coordinator, 
  Specialist, Associate, Consultant)
- Hiring and firing authority
- Financial signing authority
- Full-time physical presence plan in the U.S.
- No silent investors holding significant equity
- No management agreements delegating control to third-party manager

Partnership checklist:
- Exactly 50/50 ownership
- Operating agreement with no dilution provisions
- Completely separate fund sources — no commingling
- Genuinely distinct roles — no overlap in daily responsibilities
- Each partner independently meets substantiality threshold
- Both partners can describe the entire business independently

Spousal partnership advisory:
For married couples, the stronger path in most cases is: one spouse 
applies as E-2 investor, the other travels as E-2 dependent and applies 
for EAD. This eliminates the fund independence problem and avoids the 
combined non-immigrant intent issue.

#### Non-Immigrant Intent Advisory

Actions that create strong immigration signals — address BEFORE filing:
- Selling the Canadian home before the interview
- Closing all Canadian bank accounts
- Resigning from Canadian employment without explanation
- Enrolling children in U.S. schools before visa approval
- No remaining Canadian business or professional interests

Required ties — investor should have at least two:
- Property retained in home country
- Family remaining in home country
- Financial accounts maintained
- Professional memberships maintained
- Active business interests remaining
- Community ties documented

#### Timing Advisory

Best time to apply:
- After franchise fee paid and agreement signed
- After LLC formed and EIN obtained
- After U.S. business bank account opened and funds deposited
- After lease signed or letter of intent on specific location
- At least 3 months before planned U.S. entry
- Before selling Canadian home or closing Canadian accounts

Dangerous timing:
- Before franchise agreement is signed
- Within 30 days of a large unexplained fund deposit
- After already entering U.S. as visitor and overstaying
- When FDD review period has been less than 14 days
- When Canadian tax obligations from asset sales are unresolved

## PART 8 — DOCUMENT APPROVAL LOOP DESIGN

### The Three Types of Client Changes

**Type 1 — Wording only (no fact change)**
Client requests different phrasing without changing any fact.
Action: Regenerate that section with same facts, different voice.
Impact: No effect on other documents.
Regeneration time: Under 60 seconds.

**Type 2 — Additional information (new fact added)**
Client provides a fact they did not mention during Module 3 or follow-up.
Action: Add fact to Case Brief. Regenerate affected section.
Impact: Flag any already-approved documents that should reference this fact.
Regeneration time: 2–3 minutes. May require re-review of prior documents.

**Type 3 — Factual correction (existing fact changed)**
Client corrects an existing fact (wrong amount, wrong date, wrong name).
Action: Update Case Brief. Regenerate ALL documents referencing this fact.
Impact: Reset approval status on all affected documents.
Warning to client: "Changing this fact affects [X] previously approved 
documents. Those documents will need re-review."

### The Regeneration Limit

Free regenerations per document: 3 (included in product price)
After 3: Requires a revision credit

Revision credits by tier:
- Solo: 10 revision credits
- Solo + Spouse: 15 revision credits
- Partnership: 20 revision credits
Additional credits: $9.99 for 5 credits

### Option C — Client Chooses Workflow

At generation start, client selects:

**Option Review-As-Ready**: 
"I want to review each document as it's generated. 
Next document will only start after I approve the current one."
(Ensures maximum accuracy. Client must remain engaged.)

**Option Generate-All**: 
"Generate all documents and I'll review everything at the end.
Documents will generate automatically."
(Faster. Client reviews at completion. Same quality gate before download.)

Both paths lead to the same consistency check and quality gate 
before download is unlocked.

### Document Status States

Each document moves through these states:
QUEUED → GENERATING → UNDER_REVIEW → APPROVED → LOCKED

Once LOCKED: document is immutable.
Numbers and facts in locked documents become constraints for 
all subsequent documents.
The consistency engine uses locked document values as the 
source of truth.

## PART 9 — INTERVIEW REFERENCE GUIDE SPECIFICATION

### Purpose

A clean, 4-page document the investor takes to the consulate interview.
Not submitted to the officer. For the applicant's reference only.
Tabbed to match the application binder.

Must be: neat, professional, printed on quality paper, 
in a simple folder — not spiral bound.

### Page 1 — Application At a Glance

Contains:
- Applicant full name, nationality, consulate, interview date
- Business: legal name, type, location, planned opening date
- Investment: total amount, % of business cost, % of net worth, 
  9 FAM 402.9-6(D) proportionality context, at-risk confirmation
- Role: proposed title, authority confirmations (hire/fire/contracts/budget), 
  full-time U.S. presence confirmation
- Top 4 strongest points with specific supporting facts 
  (not generic — must use applicant's actual data)

### Page 2 — Document Reference Index

For each tab in the application:
- Tab letter and name
- Key facts contained in the tab
- Most important exhibit reference
- One-line summary of what that tab proves

Purpose: When the officer asks a question, the investor can immediately 
reference which tab contains the answer.

### Page 3 — Likely Interview Questions and Key Points

For each of the 8 most likely questions for this applicant's 
consulate and business type:
- The question text
- 3–4 key points to make in the answer
- One "DO NOT SAY" example of a common mistake

The 8 questions must be personalized to:
- The applicant's consulate (different posts ask different questions)
- The applicant's business type
- The applicant's specific risk profile (what weaknesses exist)

Generic questions that apply to all applicants (always include):
1. "Tell me about your business."
2. "Where did your investment funds come from?"
3. "What experience do you have in this industry?"
4. "What will happen to your [home/family] in Canada?"
5. "What is your role in the business day-to-day?"

Consulate-specific questions added based on known officer behavior:
- Toronto: "Walk me through your business plan financial projections"
- Frankfurt: "Show me your source of funds documentation"
- Auckland: "What is your specific role and authority in the business?"
- London: "What happens if the business fails — what is your plan?"

### Page 4 — Secondary Document Strategy and Compliance

Section 1: If the officer requests additional documents
Lists specific additional evidence available for each tab — 
not submitted but available to provide if requested.

Section 2: Administrative Processing
If you are not approved or denied at interview:
- Administrative processing is normal — not a denial
- Wait 60 days before making enquiries
- Do not make irreversible business commitments until visa confirmed
- Do not hire employees until visa confirmed
- Contact information for the consulate

Section 3: After Approval
- E-2 status begins on the date of admission, not the interview date
- Renewal typically due: 2 years from admission date
- Material changes to the business may affect E-2 status — 
  consult an attorney before making significant changes
- FBAR filing required annually if Canadian bank accounts exceed $10,000
- U.S. worldwide income tax applies from date of admission

## PART 10 — SCORING FRAMEWORK

### The Three-Layer Score

Every application has three independent scores:

**Layer 1: Calculated Score** (from Analysis Engine)
Based on the applicant's answers against established thresholds.
The engine's own assessment.

**Layer 2: KB Validation Score** (from KB Validation Layer)
Cross-checks each calculated score against consulate intelligence 
data for this specific consulate and business type.
Flags where the calculated score may be optimistic.

**Layer 3: Officer Plausibility Score** (from generation quality)
Will an officer reading these documents find them credible and 
internally consistent? This is the score that determines approval.
The engine cannot directly calculate this — it targets it through 
the generation quality rules above.

### The Scoring Context

This entire platform achieves a maximum theoretical score of 9.4/10 
against what a real E-2 application needs. The remaining 0.6 is 
irreducible — it represents:

1. Real-time judgment under questioning — the moment an unexpected 
   follow-up question is asked and the investor must respond without 
   preparation. No software can rehearse this specific moment.

2. Relationship-based consulate intelligence — practitioners who appear 
   regularly at a post know which specific officers are strict on which 
   dimensions on which days. This cannot be fully captured in a knowledge base.

3. Case theory — an experienced attorney develops a single strongest 
   argument for the whole file and builds everything around it. The engine 
   optimizes each dimension independently. The synthesis is human work.

4. Document review of actual originals — a bank statement with an 
   unexplained deposit three months before the application. A franchise 
   agreement with an unusual refund clause. These require holding the 
   document, not reading a description of it.

The platform is honest about this limit. The Applicant Preparation Brief 
notes that attorney review of the completed package is recommended for 
complex cases. The platform does not claim to replace attorney judgment — 
it claims to produce better-prepared documents than most applicants 
would produce on their own.

## PART 11 — GENERATION RULES THAT APPLY TO EVERY DOCUMENT

These rules apply universally to every document the engine generates.
They are not optional. They are not adjustable per client preference.

**Rule 1 — Legal Boundary (Absolute)**
Never use: "qualifies," "eligible," "meets the standard," "satisfies 
the requirement," "is substantial," "is sufficient," "demonstrates 
eligibility," "establishes qualification."
State facts. Let the officer draw conclusions.
This rule has no exceptions.

**Rule 2 — Specificity Over Generality**
Every document must include at least:
- One specific date (not "recently" or "in recent years")
- One specific number with appropriate context
- One specific place name relevant to the investment
- One specific person, organization, or institution the investor 
  interacted with in preparing or making the investment

Generic language that could apply to any investor is a generation failure.

**Rule 3 — Acknowledged Weakness**
For every WEAK or CRITICAL scoring dimension, the cover letter must 
acknowledge the challenge and address it directly. A document that 
ignores a weakness does not make the weakness disappear — it makes 
the officer find it without the benefit of the investor's explanation.

**Rule 4 — Voice Consistency**
The investor's voice profile, established from their writing sample, 
must be present throughout all prose documents. The documents must 
sound like a specific person wrote them, not like a template was filled in.

**Rule 5 — Document Ownership**
Each fact lives in exactly one document. The cover letter references 
other documents but does not reproduce their content. The business plan 
does not reproduce the source of funds narrative. No repetition across 
documents of substantial blocks of content.

**Rule 6 — No e2go Branding on Submitted Documents**
The e2go platform name, logo, or any reference to the platform never 
appears on any document that will be submitted to the consulate.
Submitted documents must appear to have been prepared by the investor 
or their attorney.

**Rule 7 — Consulate Calibration**
Every generation prompt receives the applicant's consulate as an input.
The generation strategy adjusts based on known consulate requirements:
- Frankfurt: executive summary format, 30-page total limit, 5MB file limit
- Toronto: 50 pages per tab, full narrative format acceptable
- London: 20MB digital upload limit, electronic submission
- All consulates: legal boundary rule applies equally

**Rule 8 — No AI Markers in Final Output**
All generated documents must pass through metadata sanitization before 
being made available for download. No AI-generation signatures, no 
model identifiers, no generation timestamps embedded in document metadata.

## PART 12 — CONSULATE-SPECIFIC REQUIREMENTS
### Key Intelligence by Post

**Toronto (Canada) — Primary e2go consulate**
- 50-page limit per tab (not total)
- All family members must attend interview (2025 requirement)
- Documents must be tabbed and indexed as specified in DS-160 instructions
- Interview preparation letters from franchise corporate office are well-received
- Officers frequently request personal tax returns (3 years)
- Known focus areas: source of funds documentation, business plan projections
- Processing time: approximately 4 months as of Q4 2025

**Frankfurt (Germany)**
- 30-page total package limit — HARD LIMIT
- 5MB file size limit per email submission
- Business plan must be executive summary format — not full narrative
- Email submission (verify current address at de.usembassy.gov/frankfurt)
- English strongly preferred; German documents require certified English translation
- Known focus areas: source of funds (aggressive secondary requests common), 
  business plan revisions frequently requested via 221(g)

**London (United Kingdom)**
- 20MB digital upload limit (not a page limit)
- Documents submitted electronically
- Formatting must survive PDF compression
- Known focus areas: executive role scrutiny, more frequent denials on 
  402.9-7(B) than other posts

**Auckland (New Zealand)**
- Standard page limits apply
- As demonstrated by the June 2026 denial letter reviewed during 
  product development, the two most commonly checked denial reasons are:
  402.9-6(E) Non-marginal economic impact and 
  402.9-7(B) Position not executive or supervisory
- Officers interview thoroughly on business plan financials
- Known as a post that applies the executive role test strictly
