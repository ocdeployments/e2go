# E-2 Visa — Substantiality of Investment
**kb/e2-visa-substantiality.md**
*Sources: 9 FAM 402.9-6(D) — State Department Foreign Affairs Manual (fam.state.gov, updated 02-17-2026),
22 CFR 41.51, INA 101(a)(15)(E)(ii)*
*Last reviewed: May 2026*

---

## The Legal Standard

The E-2 visa requires that the applicant "has invested, or is actively in the
process of investing, a substantial amount of capital in a bona fide enterprise."
(INA 101(a)(15)(E)(ii))

**There is no fixed minimum dollar amount for an E-2 investment.** This is
one of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of the E-2 program — including
by some attorneys who incorrectly cite $100,000 or $150,000 as minimums.

> "No set dollar figure constitutes a minimum amount of investment to be
> considered 'substantial' for E-2 visa purposes."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(b)

Instead, "substantial" is evaluated through three factors:

1. **Proportionality** — Is the amount substantial relative to the total cost
   of the business? (The proportionality test)
2. **Financial commitment** — Is the amount sufficient to ensure the investor's
   commitment to the successful operation of the enterprise?
3. **Operational viability** — Is the amount large enough to support the
   likelihood that the investor will successfully develop and direct the
   enterprise?

> Source: 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(b)(1)–(3)

---

## The Proportionality Test

The proportionality test is the primary mechanism for evaluating substantiality.

> "The proportionality test determines whether an investment is substantial by
> weighing the amount of qualifying funds invested against the cost of the
> business. If the two figures are the same, then the investor has invested
> 100 percent of the needed funds in the business; such an investment is
> substantial. Most cases involve lesser percentages."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)

### The Inverted Sliding Scale

The FAM describes the proportionality test as an **"inverted sliding scale":**

> "The lower the cost of the business the higher a percentage of investment
> is required. On the other hand, a highly expensive business would require
> a lower percentage of qualifying investment."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)

### How This Works in Practice

| Business Cost (Total) | Expected Investment % | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| $50,000–$100,000 | ~90–100% | Small business — nearly all funds must be invested |
| $100,000–$300,000 | ~75–90% | Mid-range — high percentage still required |
| $300,000–$750,000 | ~50–75% | Larger investment — some flexibility |
| $750,000–$2M+ | ~30–50% | Large business — magnitude may establish substantiality |
| $10M+ | ~10%+ | The FAM notes "$10 million in a $100 million business may be substantial based on sheer magnitude" |

> Source: 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c) — these ranges are not codified in the FAM
> as bright lines; they represent the general sliding scale principle as
> interpreted by practitioners and as described in the FAM.

**No bright-line percentages exist:**
> "There are no bright line percentages that exist for an investment to be
> considered substantial."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)

## Qualifying Investment Funds

### What Counts as the Investment

The following may be counted toward the qualifying investment amount:

- **Cash** — wired or deposited into the U.S. business account, spent on
  business assets, inventory, equipment, leasehold improvements
- **Business assets purchased** — equipment, machinery, inventory on hand,
  furniture, technology
- **Franchise fees and initial fees** — amounts paid to establish the franchise
  (but not ongoing royalties paid from future revenue)
- **Leasehold improvements** — amounts paid to build out and improve leased space
- **Prepaid rent** — rent paid in advance (but not future monthly rent paid from
  operating income, which counts only for one month at a time per 9 FAM)
- **Intellectual property** — rights to IP where value can be reasonably
  determined (patents, copyrights, trademarks)
- **Personal loans collateralized by personal assets** — a second mortgage on
  your Canadian home, a personal signature loan; these are at-risk funds

### What Does NOT Count

- **Funds sitting uncommitted in a bank account**
  > "Mere intent to invest, or possession of uncommitted funds in a bank account,
  > or even prospective investment arrangements entailing no present commitment,
  > will not suffice."
  > — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(e)

- **Business loans collateralized by the business itself** — if the business
  assets serve as collateral, there is no personal risk
  > "Indebtedness such as mortgage debt or commercial loans secured by the
  > assets of the enterprise cannot count toward the investment."
  > — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c)(1)

- **Ongoing lease payments** paid from operating income (only prepaid rent or
  one month at a time counts)

- **Prospective or conditional investments** — you must be "close to the start
  of actual business operations, not simply in the stage of signing contracts"

### The At-Risk Requirement

The investment must be **at risk** — meaning the funds are subject to partial
or total loss if the business fails.

> "The concept of investment connotes the placing of funds or other capital
> assets at risk, in the commercial sense, in the hope of generating a
> financial return."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(c)

This is why committed and spent funds count — they cannot be retrieved if
the business fails.

## Cost of the Business

### For a New Business

> "The cost of a newly created business is the actual cost needed to establish
> such a business to the point of being operational."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)(3)

This means: the investment must bring the business to the point where the
investor can arrive in the U.S. and begin operating immediately. You cannot
plan to raise additional funds after visa approval to finish establishing
the business.

Supporting documentation of business cost typically includes:
- Invoices or contracts for equipment and inventory purchases
- Appraisals of the value of assets
- Lease agreements and build-out contracts
- Franchise disclosure document (for franchises — lists total investment range)
- Accounting records

### For an Existing Business (Acquisition)

> "The cost of an established business is generally its purchase price, which
> is normally the fair market value."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(D)(c)(2)

The purchase price must reflect fair market value. A discounted sale
(e.g., buying from a family member at a below-market price) will be
scrutinized — the officer will evaluate the investment against the actual
fair market value of the business.

## Irrevocable Commitment

Funds must be **irrevocably committed** at the time of application.

The purchase of a business conditioned on visa approval can still qualify
if the funds are **held in escrow:**

> "The purchase of a business that is conditioned upon the issuance of the
> E-2 visa may still qualify as an irrevocable investment. Despite the condition,
> the purchase would constitute a solid commitment if the assets to be used are
> held in escrow for release or transfer once the condition is met."
> — 9 FAM 402.9-6(B)(d)

**Practical application:** If you sign a franchise agreement or business
purchase agreement contingent on visa approval, place the investment funds
in escrow. This demonstrates irrevocable commitment while protecting you
if the visa is denied.

## Common Substantiality Questions

### "My attorney says I need $100,000 minimum. Is that true?"

No. The FAM is explicit that no minimum exists. However, some consular posts
(including Toronto) have informal thresholds based on local adjudication
patterns. Lower investments require higher proportionality and more compelling
business cases. An investment below $75,000 for a new business should be
prepared to demonstrate near-100% commitment with strong financials.

### "I have the money in my Canadian bank account. Is that enough?"

No. Uncommitted funds in a bank account do not constitute an investment.
The funds must be committed — spent or placed in escrow — before the interview.

### "Can I count my franchise fee as part of the investment?"

Yes. Amounts paid to the franchisor as initial franchise fees, training fees,
and initial inventory purchases all count toward the qualifying investment.

### "What if my business needs more money after I get the visa?"

The cost of the business is what it takes to become operational. If you need
more capital to open, that amount is part of the business cost — and your
investment must be proportionate to that total cost. Plan for the full
startup cost, not just the minimum.

## Documentation Checklist for Substantiality

- Bank wire transfer records (showing funds sent from Canadian account to U.S.
  business account or escrow)
- Invoices and receipts for all business expenditures
- Lease agreement and rent prepayment documentation
- Franchise agreement and fee payment records
- Business purchase agreement and escrow documentation (if applicable)
- Equipment purchase invoices and delivery confirmations
- Contractor invoices for leasehold improvements
- Summary of all investments (Template 3 — Asset Register)
- Proof that funds are at risk (no side agreements to return funds)
